This is a rich class generalizing conformal and projective structures. We  propose a new abstraction of parabolic geometry based on Lie groupoids whose associated  Lie algebroids are filtered. In particular, we present a novel prolongation procedure to  recover the abstraction from an ``underlying geometric structure'', using semi-direct  products in the Lie groupoid category. The construction is predicated on the observation  that Lie groupoids with filtered Lie algebroids admit a semi-direct product structure  whenever they also admit a Weyl gauge, an ``oid'' version of lifts of grading elements  of a Lie algebra. A novel theme of our exposition is the parallel treatment of its  infinitesimal and global aspects.\end{abstract}\maketitle\tableofcontents%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% Body starts%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%% here %%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%\newpageThis introduction is a work in progress. Among its important applications is the systematicderivation of many invariant differential operators for these geometries\cite{Cap_etal_01,Calderbank_Diemer_01,Cap_Soucek_07,Cap_Soucek_12}.% \footnote{The introductory treatise on the subject \cite{Cap_Slovak_09}, with%  the unambitious sounding subtitle {\em Background and General Theory}, is over 600%  pages.}This article is an outgrowth of our attempts to tame some of the theory's more technicalaspects by approaching it from new points of view.\subsection{The main idea}\label{firstfirst}According to Cartan's generalization of Klein's Elrangen program \cite{Sharpe_97},geometric structures of finite type are viewed as ``symmetries deformed by curvature''. Inthe contemporary formulation, the object deformed is the principal bundle associated withsome homogeneous space, $G/H$, as we describe further in \ref{contemporary} below.  The{\df parabolic} geometries arise when one chooses $G$ to be semisimple, and $H \subset G$to be a parabolic subgroup, objects in rich supply, in which case $G/H $ is a {\df  generalized flag manifold}. The alternative approach promoted here is to considerdeformations of action groupoids, which in the transitive case have the form$G \times G/H$. In particluar, in this approach, the key concept of {\df  prolongation} appears very naturally.For the sake of concreteness, consider the conformal sphere $S^n$, a generalized flagmanifold with action groupoid $\SO(n + 1, n) \times S^n$. Now, differentiating conformalautomorphisms, i.e., elements of $\SO(n + 1, n)$, we obtain a natural Lie groupoidmorphism $\SO(n + 1, n) \times S^n \rightarrow \CO[TS^n]$ onto the frame groupoid$\CO[TS^n]$, consisting of conformal isomorphisms between tangent spaces. The kernel ofthis morphism is a Lie group bundle with commutative, simply-connected fibres. Byarguments omitted here, it has a canonical identification with $T^*\!S^n$. A fundamentalobservation, following from general results presented in this article, is that thismorphism splits (non-canonically) delivering an identification of$\SO(n + 1, n) \times S^n$ with the semi-direct product $\CO[TS^n] \times T^*\!S^n$. Thisis significant, for in the ``curved'' case of an arbitrary conformal manifold $M$, wherethe action groupoid is gone, {\em a proxy for the action groupoid persists}, namely thesemi-direct product $\CO[TM] \times T^*\!M$. This is what we call the {\df algebraic  prolongation} of the conformal structure.In the flat case, we can give the kernel $T^*\!S^n$ the interpretation of all $2$-jets ofconformal automorphisms vanishing to first order. Relevant arguments can be found in\cite{Abadoglu_etal_08}. This connects algebraic prolongation to the usual idea ofintroducing derivatives as new variables.Prolongation allows us to recover, in the typical case, a parabolic geometry from it's``underlying geometric structure'', such as the conformal structure above, also known asan {\df infinitesimal flag structure} \cite{Cap_Slovak_09}. For reasons explained at theend of \ref{levi_}, we prefer to call this the associated {\df Levi structure}.Our version of prolongation is anticipated by the observation that natural candidates forparabolic geometries are certain Lie groupoids that {\em always} admit a semi-directproduct structure. Indeed, splittings like the one above exist for a large class of Liegroupoids, even intransitive ones, provided they admit what we call a {\df Weyl gauge}. Asa special case, we can show that the action groupoid of any generalized flag manifold is asemi-direct product, a fact that does not seem to have been noticed before. Weyl gaugesare discussed further in \ref{intro_prolong} below.Algebraic prolongation methods for Cartan geometries, which are substitutes for theprolongation of PDEs, have a long and complicated history, some of which is recorded in\cite[Appendix A]{Cap_Slovak_09}. The conceptually simple formulation of prolongation,along the lines just intimated, is the most important contribution of the present article.The idea, in the present context, of replacing principal bundles with Lie groupoids, datesback to Ehresmann's 1959 paper \cite{Ehresmann_59}.\footnote{As far as we know, this paper  predates the the introduction of Lie algebroids (essential in the present work) which in  any case had not substantialy matured until the work of Jean Pradines in the late  1960's. Perhaps this explains why the groupoid approach did not gain traction at that  time.}\subsection{The contemporary point of view}\label{contemporary}%In the principal bundle formulation, which originates in Ehresmann's earlier 1951 paper\cite{Ehresmann_51}, a {\df Cartan geometry}, modeled on some homogeneous space $G/H$, isa smooth manifold $M$ and a principal $H$-bundle $P$ over $M$, equipped with: (i) Aparallelism, understood as a $V$-valued one-form $\omega$ on $P$ (the {\df connection}),required to be suitably compatible with the principal bundle structure; and (ii) Acompatible identification of the vector space $V$ with the infinitesimalization${\mathfrak g}$ of $G$ (the {\df model data}). Combining (i) and (ii), we can view$\omega $ as ${\mathfrak g} $-valued one-form, which is the object classically known as a{\df Cartan connection}. See, for example, \cite{Cap_Slovak_09,Blaom_16b} for definitions(which will not be needed here).In the case of a principal $H$-bundle $G \rightarrow G/H$, the Maurer-Cartan form providesa Cartan connection, and in the general case there is a notion of curvature,$d \omega - [\omega,\omega]_{\mathfrak g}$, which vanishes in the homogeneous case, byvirtue of the Maurer-Cartan equations for $G$. Since it depends on the choice of modeldata (it involves the bracket on the Lie algebra ${\mathfrak g} $ of $G$) we refer to thisas the {\df model curvature}; it's significance is that $M$ is locally identifiable withthe model $G/H$ whenever the model curvature vanishes. (As explained later, an alternativenotion of curvature, which does not depend on model data, will play a secondary role inthis article.)The connection and model data allow one to transfer certain structures from the modelspace, such as a $G$-invariant quadratic form on ${\mathfrak g} /{\mathfrak h} $, tostructures on the underlying manifold $M$, for example a Riemannian metric, and thislatter constitutes the ``underlying geometric structure'' of the Cartan geometry. In theparabolic case, the Lie algebra ${\mathfrak h} $ of $H$ defines a filtration of${\mathfrak g} $ which, among other things, implies that $TM$ is non-trivially filtered inthe typical case. A simple example is CR-geometry, where this filtration is defined by thecontact structure ${\mathcal H}=T^{-1}M \subset T^{-2}M=TM$.Different choices of $\omega $ may lead to the same underlying geometric structure, whichleads to the first fundamental problem of the theory, how to choose $\omega $ so as to``normalize'' the model curvature, for then $\omega$ (and its curvature, for example)encode basic invariants about the underlying geometry. The second fundamental problem ishow to reconstruct an abstract Cartan geometry from the underlying geometric structure.Where this is possible one obtains a one-to-one correspondence between (isomorphismclasses) of normalized Cartan geometries and the concrete geometries of interest, solvingCartan's Equivalence Problem for such those structures. The technical tool for making thisreconstruction is known as {\df prolongation}, because of its roots in the procedure inthe theory of partial differential equations (PDE's) of the same name.\subsection{The new approach}In \cite{Morimoto_93}, Tohru Morimoto published a highly original attempt to systematizethe construction and normalization of Cartan connections, applicable in the paraboliccase. In it Morimoto formulates the notion of {\df filtered manifolds}, now a part ofthe contemporary formalism \cite{Cap_Slovak_09}. He also demonstrates the usefulness ofseparating the notions of {\df connection} and {\df model data}, a theme of our own workcited shortly, and the present exposition, but not emphasized more generally.The present article revisits the construction and normalization of Cartan connections inparabolic geometry in the light of a new analysis of ``firmly'' filtered Lie algebroids, anatural generalization of Morimoto's filtered manifolds.  In this recounting, Liegroupoids, whose corresponding infinitesimalization are filtered Lie algebroids, replacethe principal bundles of the conventional approach, along the lines suggested in\ref{firstfirst}. Previous work of ours, reviewed in Section \ref{cartan_section}, showshow to view Lie algebroids or Lie groupoids as (possibly intransitive) ``symmetriesdeformed by curvature'', by equipping them with connections {\em without} model data\cite{Blaom_06,Blaom_12,Blaom_13,Blaom_16,Blaom_16b} (but nevertheless called {\df Cartan  connections}). In the groupoid case, the connection is a multiplicatively closedhorizontal $n$-plane field, $D$, and in the infinitesimal case, it is a Koszul connection,$\nabla $, suitably compatible with the Lie algebroid structure. The type of symmetry, ifit exists, is not specified {\em a priori}, and the natural definition of curvature (theusual one for $\nabla $) does not depend on a notion of model data. Here we introduce, forthe first time in the ``oid'' context, a notion of compatible model data, and of modelcurvature, in which the model is specified ahead of time, as in the standardapproach. With these basic ingredients we can imitate the standard theory, with somenovelties discussed further below,Our primary source for learning about parabolic geometry has been {\v{C}}ap andSlov{\'a}k's seminal monograph \cite{Cap_Slovak_09}, which has significantly informed thepresent work. In particular, prolongation, as described here, while new, is closer to theprocedure described in \cite{Cap_Slovak_09} than to the earlier scheme of Morimoto.\subsection{Weyl gauges and semi-direct products}\label{intro_prolong}The general considerations leading to the Lie groupoid splittings alluded to above can bepreviewed here by considering the special Lie {\em algebra} case of our Theorem\ref{weyl2}:\begin{theorem}  Let  $\cdots \supset {\mathfrak a}^{-1} \supset {\mathfrak a}^0 \supset {\mathfrak a}^1  \supset {\mathfrak a}^2 \supset \cdots$ be a filtration\footnote{See \ref{filtered} for    precisely what we mean here.} of some finite-dimensional Lie algebra ${\mathfrak a} $,  and assume the graded Lie algebra associated with ${\mathfrak a}^0$,  $\graded({\mathfrak a}^0)$, admits a grading element $E$, necessarily lying in  $\graded({\mathfrak a})_0$.  Moreover, assume that $E $ has a lift  $\epsilon \in {\mathfrak a}^0$ such that  \begin{equation}    [{\mathfrak a}, \epsilon] \subset {\mathfrak a}^1.\label{invariance}  \end{equation}  Then ${\mathfrak a}^j\subset {\mathfrak a}$ is an ideal for all $j \ge 0$ and  ${\mathfrak a}$ is isomorphic to the semi-direct product  ${\mathfrak a}/{\mathfrak a}^1 \oplus_{\adjoint}\graded({\mathfrak a}^1)$, the  isomorphism depending on the choice of $\epsilon $.\end{theorem}\noindent%Recall here that an element $E$ of a graded Lie algebra${\mathfrak g}=\oplus_j {\mathfrak g}_j $ is a {\df grading element} if $[E , X] = j X$,whenever $X \in {\mathfrak g}_j$. Note that the theorem requires only a grading element of$\graded({\mathfrak a}^0)$ and not $\graded({\mathfrak a})$. When the additionalinvariance condition \eqref{invariance} is satisfied, we call $\epsilon $ a {\df Weyl  gauge}.If the condition about ideals is satisfied, necessary for the existence of a Weyl gauge,we say that ${\mathfrak a} $ is {\df firmly filtered}. This is a strong condition, but theright one for our general setting, as we shall see already for homogeneous spaces $G/H$and other examples (Section \ref{introduction}). The theorem above has the followingcorollary:\begin{aside}  A filtered Lie algebra ${\mathfrak a}$ admits a Weyl gauge if and only if it is firmly  filtered and $\graded({\mathfrak a}^0)$ admits an ${\mathfrak a}$-invariant grading  element $E$.\end{aside}Weyl gauges have straightforward generalizations to filtered Lie algebroids and Liegroupoids, even intransitive ones. For us, a parabolic geometry is a firmly filteredtransitive Lie groupoid, together with suitably compatible model data (Definition\ref{parabolic_defined}). The semisimplicity of the model data is enough to guarantee theexistence of Weyl gauges, by a generalization of the italicized statement above. In theabove super-simplification of the general scenario, ${\mathfrak a}/{\mathfrak a}^1$,together with its representation $\graded({\mathfrak a}^1)$, play the role of thecorresponding Levi structure.% An immediate application of our results in the general setting (specifically, our Theorems% \ref{weyl2} and \ref{all}) is the following:% \begin{aside}%   Every action groupoid ${\mathcal G} = G \times G/H$ associated with a generalized flag%   manifold $G/H$ is a semi-direct product ${\mathcal H} \times \exp(N)$, where%   ${\mathcal H}$ is a certain transitive Lie groupoid with reductive vertex group (the%   reductive Levi quotient of the parabolic subgroup $H\subset G$) and $N$ a nilpotent Lie%   algebra bundle (modeled on the nilradical of the Lie algebra of $H$).% \end{aside}% Our results may be viewed as a generalization of this one to the ``curved'', possibly% intransitive case.  In the case of the conformal sphere, the result is that% $\SO(n + 1, 1) \times S^n$ is the semi-direct product $\CO[TS^n]\times T^*\!S^n$, where%  Conformal automorphisms are determined by their jets to second order, and we have% the following interpretation: $\CO[TS^n]$ encodes is the zeroeth and first order parts,% while $T^*S^n$ encodes the second order part.The name ``Weyl gauge'' comes from the Weyl structures/connections in the standard theoryof parabolic geometry \cite{Cap_Slovak_09}, which generalize Weyl's own connections inconformal geometry.  It may be there exists a correspondence between the new semi-directproduct description and the constructions in \cite{Cap_Slovak_09} but this is not obviousto us.  Here Weyl gauges ground the entire theory (and make sense before introducing anyassumptions about semisimplicity) while in \cite{Cap_Slovak_09} prolongation is firstdeveloped independently of Weyl structures.  We note that the semi-direct productstructure leads to an explicit characterization of all parabolic geometries --- indeed, ofall transitive filtered Cartan geometries, a mild generalization (Theorem\ref{abstract_levi_}).\subsection{Infinitesimal geometry} An obvious advantage of the Lie groupoid approach is adirect relationship between global and infinitesimal representations of the geometry,namely a natural generalization of the relationship between Lie groups and Liealgebras. Indeed, many constructions in the present development are first conceptualizedat the infinitesimal level, where they are easier to understand (and how we generallydiscovered them) and later globalized. There is even the possibility, not pursued here, ofcompletely reducing the global theory to the infinitesimal one, using the Lie groupoidversions of Lie I and Lie II \cite{Crainic_Fernandes_11}, provided one is happy to ignorecertain topological distinctions between geometries blurred by restricting to the case ofsource-simply-connected Lie groupoids.In the principal bundle approach, it is conventional to view something called the the {\df  adjoint tractor bundle} ${\mathcal A}$, as the corresponding infinitesimal object. Forexample, the (model) curvature of a parabolic geometry is there understood as an${\mathcal A}$-valued two-form. This seems natural because tractor bundles are associated bundles. Inthe Lie groupoid point-of-view the analogue of associated bundles are Lie groupoidrepresentations, but there is no canonical representation of a Lie groupoid on its Liealgebroid. Stated differently, the Atiyah Lie algebroid of a principal bundle is not anassociated bundle; it only becomes one (is identifiable with ${\mathcal A}$) {\em after}choosing a Cartan connection. This essential difference between the two approaches has arelated manifestation. As we recall in Section \ref{cartan_section}, a Cartan connection$D$ on a Lie groupoid immediately infinitesimalizes to a Koszul connection $\nabla $ onthe corresponding Lie algebroid, without model data playing any role, but it seems thisconnection never appears explicitly in the Cartan geometry literature (outside of our ownwork); rather the focus is on the so-called tractor connection on ${\mathcal A}$, whichdepends on its associated bundle structure. While the tractor connection has an analoguein the present approach, the infinitesimal Cartan connection $\nabla $ and the adjointtractor connection are different, except in the flat case. See\eqrefs{tractors}{tractor1}.\subsection{The role of model data}\label{role}Important ingredients of the present approach are the ``oid'' versions of classical Cartanconnections, $D$ and $\nabla $ mentioned above. In \cite{Blaom_12} we outlined anambitious program for constructing such Cartan connections for general geometricstructures of finite type, without the need to prescribe a flat model {\em a priori},using a direct PDE prolongation procedure. While some simple structures are amenable tothis approach, the program has largely been unsuccessful. In the case of parabolicgeometries, the specification of model data {\em a priori} appears to be essential: Itprovides a hook into the rich structure theory of semi-simple Lie algebras, simplifyingthe normalization process which is intractable in the earlier PDE approach.An interesting feature of the current approach is that model data appears initially as anotion independent of the Lie groupoid Cartan connection $D$, but with which it must becompatible. Specifically, model data is a Lie algebra bundle structure on the underlyingLie algebroid that extends the canonical one on the kernel of the anchor; compatibilitymeans invariance with respect to a self-representation of the Lie algebroid defined by$D$.  As we were surprised to discover in the parabolic case, the model data {\em  uniquely} determines the connection $D$, assuming a compatible such connection doesexist (Theorem \ref{parabolic}). A (global) parabolic geometry is accordingly encoded byjust two structures {\em on its infinitesimalization}: the filtration and the compatiblemodel data. This phenomenon is apparently peculiar to the parabolic case, and not true forall Cartan geometries, even filtered ones.% \subsection{Extensions} While not pursued here at all, the present work lays the% foundations for the possible extension of parabolic geometry to {\em intransitive}% geometries of finite type. What is ultimately needed inapplications is non-trivial and deep, but also comprehensively summarized in\cite{Cap_Slovak_09}.\subsection*{Blanket assumptions}All manifolds are smooth, which means $C^\infty$ or real-analytic, and have connectedcomponents sharing a common dimension. With the exception of Lie groupoids, manifolds areHausdorff. All manifolds are second countable. In particular, all transitiveLie groupoids are automatically locally trivial (source maps are both surjective andsubmersive) \cite[Proposition A.3]{Trentinaglia_15} and so are gauge groupoids (groupoidsdefined by a principal bundle).The kernel of the anchor of a Lie algebroid, which is variously known as the isotropy,vertex bundle, or adjoint bundle, is here called the {\df anchor kernel}.% \subsection{Firmly filtered Lie algebroids and semisimplicity}% The extra ``firmness'' requirement for filtered Lie algebroids means that the associated% graded Lie algebra bundle includes the sum of components with negative (or non-negative)% degree as an ideal (Proposition \eqrefs{filtered}{rt2}). All filtrationsare descending.  If $A$ is a vector bundle, then we call the filtration a {\df Lie  algebroid filtration} if $A$ is a Lie algebroid and:\begin{conditions}\item[\bf F1.] The induced filtration  $\cdots \Gamma(A^{-1})\supset \Gamma(A^0) \supset \Gamma(A^1) \supset \Gamma(A^2)  \supset\cdots $ of the section space $\Gamma(A)$ is a filtration of Lie algebras,  meaning $[X, Y] \in \Gamma(A^{j+k})$ for $X \in \Gamma(A^j)$ and $Y \in \Gamma(A^k)$.\item[\bf F2.] The kernel of the anchor contains $A^0$.\label{fa2}\end{conditions}A filtered Lie algebroid $A$ is {\df firmly filtered} if $A^0$ and the filtration of $A^0$are invariant under the canonical representation of $A$ on its anchor kernel, i.e., if$\Gamma(A^j) \subset \Gamma(A)$ is an ideal for all $j \ge 0$.\begin{proposition} Let $A$ be a filtered Lie algebroid. Then:  \begin{conditions}  \item The associated graded vector bundle $\graded(A)$ is a bundle of graded Lie    algebras (a smooth field of graded Lie brackets). \label{b1}  \item If $A$ is firmly filtered, then $\graded(A)$ is the Lie algebra fibre-wise direct    sum of its negative and non-negative parts, i.e., $\oplus_{i<0}\graded(A)_i$ and    $\oplus_{i\ge 0}\graded(A)_i$ are fibre-wise ideals of $\graded(A)$. \label{rt2}    \end{conditions}\end{proposition}%\begin{remark}  If the fibres of $\graded(A)$ are pairwise isomorphic Lie algebras, then $\graded(A)$ is  a bona fide Lie algebra bundle, by, e.g., \cite[Theorem 6.4.5]{Mackenzie_05}; this will  be the case in all the examples discussed in this article. If $A$ is firmly filtered,  transitive, and {\em integrable}, then $\graded(A^0)$ must be a Lie algebra bundle,  because the canonical representation of $A$ on $A^0$ will drop to a representation of  $A$ on $\graded(A^0)$, and the latter integrates to a representation of the transitive  (and so locally trivial) integrating Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G} $ having  simply-connected source fibres. These comments notwithstanding, local triviality of Lie  algebroid gradings will not play an essential role here.\end{remark}\begin{proof}  Let $X \in \Gamma(A^j) $ and $Y \in \Gamma(A^k)$ be given. To prove \eqref{b1}, it  suffices to show that  $$ [fX, Y] \equiv f[X, Y] \mod \Gamma(A^{j+k+1})$$  for any smooth function $f$ on the base manifold. If $\#$ denotes  the anchor, then the necessary and sufficient condition is  that  $$df(\#Y)X \in \Gamma(A^{j + k + 1}).$$  If $k \ge 0$ then this is holds by F2. If instead $k \le -1 $, then $j + k + 1 \le j$,  implying $A^j \subset A^{j + k + 1}$ and the condition is still satisfied.  Suppose $A$ is firmly filtered. Then it is clear that $\oplus_{i<0}\graded(A)_i$ and  $\oplus_{i\ge 0}\graded(A)_i$ are fibre-wise subalgebras of $\graded(A)$. To prove  \eqref{rt2}, it suffices to show that sections of one of these commute with sections of  the other. To this end, suppose $X \in \Gamma(A^k)$ and $Y \in \Gamma(A^j)$, with $k<0$  and $j\ge 0$. Then $j + k + 1 \le j$, which implies $A^j \subset A^{j+k+1}$. Since $A$  is firmly filtered, $\Gamma(A^j) \subset \Gamma(A)$ is an ideal, so that  $[X, Y] \in \Gamma(A^j) \subset \Gamma(A^{j + k + 1})$. It follows that  $[\graded_j(X), \graded_k(Y)]=0$.\end{proof}\begin{notation}[Bounds on degree]  Suppose $A$ is a {\df graded} vector space or vector bundle. Then, we write $k \le A$ if  $A_i=0$ for all $i < k$ and $A \le k$ if $A_i=0$ for all $i > k$. For example, if  $A = A_{-2}\oplus A_{-1} \oplus A_0$, then we write $-2 \le A \le 0$. We write  $|A| = k $ if $-k \le A \le k$ and $A_{-k} \ne 0 \ne A_k$.  If $A$ is merely {\em filtered}, then we write $A \le k$ if $\graded(A) \le k$, etc. For  example, for filtered $A$, we write $|A| = 1$ if  $\graded(A) = \graded(A)_{-1} \oplus \graded(A)_0 \oplus \graded(A)_1$ with  $\graded(A)_{\pm 1} \ne 0$.\end{notation}The remainder of the section is devoted to examples of filtered Lie algebroids. All butthe first class of examples are always firm.\subsection{ Filtered Lie algebras and Lie algebra bundles} Evidently, every filteredLie algebra, and more generally every filtered Lie algebra bundle, is a filtered Liealgebroid.\subsection{ Filtered manifolds} For any Lie algebroid filtration of a tangent bundle$TM$, we have $TM \le -1$, by Axiom F2.  These filtrations are trivially firm. A smoothmanifold $M$ whose tangent bundle is so structured is called a {\df filtered manifold}, anotion introduced by Morimoto \cite{Morimoto_93}, and appearing prominently in\cite{Cap_Slovak_09}.  The anchor $\# \colon A \rightarrow M$ of any {\em transitive}filtered Lie algebroid $A$ pushes the filtration down to a filtration of $TM$. That is,the base of any transitive filtered Lie algebroid is a filtered manifold.\footnote{One can  drop the requirement of transitivity, but only at the expense of dropping our  requirement $\cap_jA_j=0$ in the definition of a filtration.} \label{yt}\subsection{Trivial filtrations}\label{trivial} Suppose $A$ is any Lie algebroid whose anchor kernel hasconstant rank (e.g., is transitive). Choosing $A^0$ to be this kernel, and defining$A^j=A$ for $j<0$ and $A^j = 0$ for $j>0$, we get a firm Lie algebroid filtration with$-1 \le A \le 0 $ that we call the {\df trivial filtration} of $A$.\subsection{ Filtered Klein geometries}\label{eg2}For a more interesting class of examples, let $G$ be a Lie group with Lie algebra${\mathfrak g}$ and $P \subset G$ a closed subgroup. Furthermore, suppose that${\mathfrak g} $ admits a $P$-invariant filtration such that ${\mathfrak g}^0$ is the Liealgebra of $P$. In particular, as recalled in \ref{canonical_filtrations_}, a canonicalfiltration of this kind exists whenever $G/P$ is a generalized flag manifold.  Write$M =G/P$ and let $A={\mathfrak g} \times M$ be the action algebroid. Then we obtain afiltration of $A$, as a vector bundle, by defining$A^j(m) = \Adjoint_g({\mathfrak g}^j)\times \{m\}$, where $g \in G$ is any elementsatisfying $m = g \cdot m_0$ and $m_0$ is the left coset of $P $ containing the identityof $G$. It is not hard to see that $A$ is a firmly filtered Lie algebroid (Proposition\ref{homogeneous}).Significantly, $\graded(A)$, which will be a graded Lie algebra bundle, is {\em not}modeled on $\graded({\mathfrak g})$ but modeled on the ordinary direct sum of the gradedLie algebras $\oplus_{i<0}\graded({\mathfrak g})_i$, re-equipped with {\em negative}bracket, and $\oplus_{i\ge 0}\graded({\mathfrak g})_i$. So $\graded(A)$ has {\em two}different, although related, Lie algebra bundle structures: the one inherited from thefiltered Lie algebroid, and a ``flat'' one inherited from ${\mathfrak g} $. On $A^0$ theycoincide.Note that the filtration of $A$ is firm, irrespective of whether${\mathfrak g}_j \subset {\mathfrak g} $ is an ideal for any $j$!\subsection{ Projective structures.}\label{projective} Let $A \subset J^2(TM)$ be theabstract homogeneous PDE you solve to find the infinitesimal isometries of some projectivestructure on $M$. Then $A$ is a subalgebroid mapped isomorphically onto $J^1(TM)$ by theprojection $J^2(TM) \rightarrow J^1(TM)$, whose kernel intersects $A$ in the image $A^1$of the natural injection $j \colon T^*\!M \rightarrow S^2(T^*\!M)\otimes TM$ defined by$$j(\alpha)(V_1, V_2) = \alpha(V_1)V_2 + \alpha(V_2)V_1.$$ See \cite[\S5.9]{Blaom_12}. If we define$A^0$ to be the anchor kernel of $A$, and $A^{-1}=A$, we get a firm Lie algebroidfiltration of $A$ with $|A|=1$. The corresponding graded Lie algebra bundle is$$\graded(A) = TM \oplus \gl(TM) \oplus T^*\!M,$$where $TM$ and $T^*\!M $ are commutative, $\gl(TM) \oplus T^*\!M $ is the Lie algebrabundle semi-direct product, and $TM$ and $\gl(TM) \oplus T^*\!M $ are ideals, byProposition \eqrefs{filtered}{rt2}.While not immediately obvious, $\graded(A)$ has a second, natural, {\em semisimple,} Liealgebra bundle structure, modeled on $\speciallinear(n + 1, {\mathbb R})$,$n= \dimension(M)$, and compatible with the grading. The bracket for this structure, whichwe will denote $T$, extends the existing bracket on $\graded(A^0)=\gl(TM) \oplus T^*\!M $with the new relations\begin{equation}  T(C, V) = C V, \quad    T(V, \alpha) = j(\alpha)V, \label{projective_bracket}\end{equation}for sections $C$, $V$ and $\alpha $ of $\gl(TM)$, $TM$ and $T^*\!M $ respectively.Significantly, this bracket coincides with the flat one referenced in \ref{eg2}, when $M$is the projective sphere, viewed as a homogeneous$\operatorname{SL}(n + 1, {\mathbb R})$-space.The fact that the Lie algebra $\speciallinear(n + 1, {\mathbb R})$ for the full symmetryof the homogeneous model persists in some form in the ``curved'' case is a surprising butcrucial one, and is shared by all parabolic geometries.\subsection{Conformal structures}\label{conformal}Let $B \subset J^1(TM)$ be the abstract homogeneous PDE you solve to find theinfinitesimal isometries of a conformal structure on $M$ \cite[Section 12]{Blaom_12}. Then$B$ is a transitive subalgebroid whose anchor kernel $\co(TM)\subset \gl(TM)$ consists ofthe infinitesimal conformal automorphisms of tangent spaces. Now $B$ acts on $TM$ viarestricted adjoint action, and so acts on $T^*\!M $. Regarding $T^*\!M $ as a commutativeLie algebra bundle, we construct, in the category of Lie algebroids, the semi-directproduct $A = B \oplus_{\adjoint} T^*\!M $ (what we shall come to call the {\df algebraic  prolongation} of $B$). Then, if we put $A^1 = T^*\!M $, $A^0 = \co(TM) \oplus T^*\!M$(the anchor kernel of $A$), and $A^{-1}=A$, then $A$ becomes a firmly filtered Liealgebroid with $|A| = 1$. The corresponding graded Lie algebra bundle is$$\graded(A)=TM \oplus \co(TM) \oplus T^*\!M,$$ where $\co(TM) \oplus T^*\!M $ is thesemi-direct product, and $TM$ and $\co(TM) \oplus T^*\!M $ are ideals.Again, there is also a semisimple Lie algebra bundle structure on $\graded(A)$ compatiblewith the grading, in this case modeled on $\so(n + 1, 1)$, and coinciding with the flatone of \ref{eg2} when $M$ is the conformal sphere, viewed as a homogeneous$\operatorname{SO}(n + 1, 1)$-space. After redefining the injection $j$ above,\begin{equation*}  j(\alpha)(V_1, V_2) = \alpha(V_1) V_2 + \alpha(V_2)V_1 -  \sigma(V_1,V_2)\sigma^{-1}(\alpha),\end{equation*}now a map$j \colon T^*\!M\rightarrow (S^2(T^*\!M)\otimes TM) \cap (T^*\!M \otimes \co(TM)) $, thesemisimple bracket $T$ extends the existing bracket on$\graded(A^0)=\co(TM) \oplus T^*\!M$, with the same additional relations\eqref{projective_bracket} above.  Here $\sigma $ is any metric in the conformal class.% \vspace{\baselineskip} The semisimple Lie algebra bundles $TM \oplus \gl(TM)\oplus T^*\!M$% and $TM \oplus \co(TM)\oplus T^*\!M$ in the last two examples are generalized by the {\df%   infinitesimal Levi structures} defined in \ref{levi_}.\section{Parabolic geometries. Part I}\label{parabolic_summary}In this section we give our definition of parabolic geometries \`a la Lie groupoids andoutline our main results about them. Proofs appear after various restatements given inSection \ref{parabolic_proofs}.\subsection{Filtered Lie groupoids}\label{filtered_groupoids}We will call a Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G} $ over $M$ {\df filtered} if its associated Liealgebroid $A$ is filtered. Now ${\mathcal G} $ acts on elements of the anchor kernel of$A$, and so on elements of $A^0$, although this action need not preserve $A^0$ ingeneral. This action will be denoted $\Adjoint$. The Lie groupoid is {\em firmly filtered}if $A^j$ is ${\mathcal G}$-invariant under $\Adjoint$, for all $j \ge 0$, a slightlystronger condition than firmness of the filtration of $A$.\subsection{Parabolic subalgebras and canonical filtrations}\label{canonical_filtrations_}Let ${\mathfrak g} $ be a finite-dimensional semisimple Lie algebra over ${\mathbb C} $ or${\mathbb R} $. Then ${\mathfrak p} \subset {\mathfrak g} $ is {\df parabolic} if itsKilling polar ${\mathfrak p}^\perp$ is a nilpotent subalgebra.\footnote{Readers may  understand parabolic subalgebras as those containing a Borel (maximal solvable)  subalgebra. That, over ${\mathbb C} $ or ${\mathbb R} $, this is equivalent to the  stated definition is proven in \cite{Burstall_10}.} Recall these subalgebras areintimately connected with filtrations of $\mathfrak g$: Define${\mathfrak g}^0= {\mathfrak p}$ and ${\mathfrak g}^1={\mathfrak p}^\perp$, andinductively define ${\mathfrak g}^j = [{\mathfrak g}^1, {\mathfrak g}^{j-1}]$, for$j \ge 2$, and${\mathfrak g}^j = \{X \in {\mathfrak g} \suchthat [X, {\mathfrak g}^1] \subset {\mathfrak  g}^{j+1}\}$, for $j\le -1$. Then:\begin{proposition}\mbox{}  \begin{conditions}  \item The above definitions make ${\mathfrak g} $ into a filtered Lie algebra with    $|{\mathfrak g}| \le k$ for some $k\ge 0$, and this is called the {\df canonical      filtration} associated with ${\mathfrak p}$.  \item \label{lqq} In any filtration of ${\mathfrak g} $ defined by a grading    ${\mathfrak g}=\oplus_{j \in {\mathbb Z}} {\mathfrak g}_j$,    ${\mathfrak g}^0 = \oplus_{j\ge0}{\mathfrak g}_j$ is a parabolic subalgebra. Moreover,    this filtration is the precisely the associated canonical filtration, provided    ${\mathfrak g}_1$ generates ${\mathfrak g}^1=\oplus_{j\ge 1}{\mathfrak g}_j$. \end{conditions}\end{proposition}\noindent%An elementary proof of this known result is given in Appendix\ref{lie_algebra_facts}. Part \eqref{lqq} is useful for identifying parabolic subalgebrasin examples.Another crucial significance of a canonical filtration of semisimple ${\mathfrak g}$ isthat passing to the associated grading incurs no essential loss of information, as$\graded({\mathfrak g})$ turns out to be isomorphic to ${\mathfrak g} $ (Lemma\ref{gradings_from_filtrations}).\begin{remark}  In fact, for a parabolic ${\mathfrak p} $, ${\mathfrak p}^\perp$ is the nilpotent radical  of ${\mathfrak p} $, i.e.,  ${\mathfrak p}/{\mathfrak p}^\perp={\mathfrak g}^0/{\mathfrak g}^1$ is the reductive  Levi quotient of ${\mathfrak p}$. We won't need this fact here, but it explains the name  ``Levi structure'' introduced below.\end{remark}\subsection{Generalized flag manifolds}\label{parabolic_}Suppose that $G$ is a semisimple Lie group and $P \subset G$ a parabolic subgroup. Bydefinition, this means $P$ has a parabolic Lie algebra${\mathfrak p} \subset {\mathfrak g} $ and that the associated canonical filtration of${\mathfrak g} $ is $P$-invariant under $\Adjoint$ (it is automatically${\mathfrak p} $-invariant under $\adjoint$). The quotient $M=G/P$ is known as a {\df  generalized flag manifold}. The projective and conformal spheres areexamples. Generalized flag manifolds constitute the flat models of parabolic geometries.The $P$-invariance of the canonical filtration leads to a firm filtering of the actionalgebroid $A := {\mathfrak g} \times M$, as described in \ref{eg2}. It is easy to see thatthe action groupoid ${\mathcal G} := G \times M $ is also firmly filtered. Of particularrelevance here are two structures on ${\mathcal G} $, to be generalized below: (i) Thecanonical horizontal $n$-plane field $D$ on ${\mathcal G} $, tangent to the foliation$G \times \{m\}$, $m \in M$ (and its infinitesimalization, the flat Koszul connection$\nabla $ on the trivial bundle $A$) --- an example of a Lie groupoid (algebroid) {\df  Cartan connection}; and (ii) The trivial Lie algebra bundle structure on theinfinitesimalization $A = {\mathfrak g} \times M$ of ${\mathcal G} $ --- an example of{\df model data}.\subsection{Cartan connections and model data}\label{preview}Let ${\mathcal G} $ be an arbitrary Lie groupoid. As we detail in Section\ref{cartan_section}, a horizontal $n$-plane field $D$ on ${\mathcal G} $ is a {\df Cartan  connection} if it is suitably compatible with the Lie groupoid structure\cite{Blaom_16b}, \cite[Appendix A]{Blaom_12}. It's infinitesimalization, called an {\df  (infinitesimal) Cartan connection}, is a Koszul connection $\nabla $ on thecorresponding Lie algebroid $A$, suitably compatible with the Lie algebroid structure\cite{Blaom_06}. Curvature of $\nabla $ is the obstruction to symmetry: When $\nabla $ isflat, $A$ is a (possibly twisted) action algebroid and, under certain technicalconditions, ${\mathcal G} $ is a (possibly twisted) action groupoid. For details andvariations on these results, see Section \ref{cartan_section}.Associated with any Cartan connection $D$ is a representation $\bar D$ of ${\mathcal G} $on $A$, extending the canonical action $\Adjoint$ of ${\mathcal G} $ on the anchor kernelof $A$, and we say $D$ is {\em compatible} with a filtration of ${\mathcal G} $ (in thesense above) if the filtration of $A$ is $\bar D$-invariant. The filtration of${\mathcal G} $ is necessarily firm in that case (Proposition \ref{compat}).Similarly, there is associated with any infinitesimal Cartan connection $\nabla$, arepresentation $\bar\nabla $ of $A $ on itself, extending the canonical representation of$A $ on the anchor kernel. If $(A, \nabla)$ is the infinitesimalization of some$({\mathcal G}, D)$ as above, then $\bar\nabla$ is the infinitesimalization of thepreceding global representation $\bar D$. In any case, it is related to $\nabla $ by asimple formula, $\bar\nabla_XY = \nabla_{\#Y}X +[X, Y]$, useful in computations. Here $\#$denotes the anchor.A smooth field $\tau $ of Lie brackets on a Lie algebroid $A$ is {\df model data} if$\tau(X,Y) = [X, Y]$ whenever $X$ and $Y$ both lie in the kernel of the anchor of $A$. Inparticular, if $A$ is transitive, any Lie algebra bundle structure on $A$ extending thecanonical Lie algebra bundle structure of the anchor kernel will be model data, andwithout further comment all model data in the present article is understood to be of thistype.Model data $\tau$ is {\df compatible} with a filtration of $A$ if this is also afiltration with respect to $\tau$, i.e., $\tau(X, Y) \in A^{j+k}$ whenever, $X \in A^j$and $Y \in A^k$ have a common base point. We say $\tau $ is compatible with a Cartanconnection $D$ if $\tau $ is $\bar D$-invariant.\subsection{Parabolic geometries defined}\label{parabolic_defined}A {\df parabolic geometry} is a transitive, firmly filtered, Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G} $over some smooth manifold $M$, with associated Lie algebroid denoted $A$, andfiltration-compatible model data $\tau $, such that:\begin{conditions}    \item The Lie algebra model for $\tau $ is semisimple.\label{parabolic2}    \item $A^0$ is the kernel of the anchor of $A$ and a fibre-wise parabolic subalgebra      of $(A, \tau)$. The filtration of $A$ is, fibre-wise, the associated canonical      filtration. \label{parabolic1}    \item There exists a Cartan connection $D$ on ${\mathcal G} $      compatible with $\tau $.\label{parabolic4}\end{conditions}\begin{proposition}%  [Propositions \ref{parabolic}, Theorem \ref{uniquely_determined}, Corollary \ref{all}]\mbox{}  \begin{conditions}  \item Assuming $M$ is connected, it suffices to check that \eqref{parabolic1} holds over    a single point $m \in M$.  \item A $\tau $-compatible connection $D$ is necessarily compatible with the filtration    of $A$, and strongly compatible with $\tau $.  \end{conditions}\end{proposition}\noindent%An infinitesimal Cartan connection $\nabla $ on a Lie algebroid $A$ is {\df strongly  compatible} with model data $\tau $ if it is compatible with $\tau $, and if$\bar\nabla_ZX = \tau(Z, X)$ whenever $Z$ lies in the anchor kernel of $A$. A globalCartan connection $D$ is {\df strongly compatible} with $\tau $ if itsinfinitesimalization $\nabla $ is. For more on this notion, see \ref{compat2}.Parabolic geometries are generalized by {\df filtered Cartan geometries}, introduced inSection \ref{filtered_cartan}. However, the following result is apparently peculiar to theparabolic case:\begin{theorem}[Theorem \ref{uniquely_determined}]  In any parabolic geometry, a compatible Cartan connection $D$ is uniquely determined  by the model data $\tau$.\end{theorem}\noindent One may view this fact as mirroring the classical result that every Riemannianmetric is compatible with a unique torsion free $TM$-connection.\subsection{Regularity}\label{regularity_}Let $A$ be any {\em transitive} filtered Lie algebroid. In that case the anchor$\# \colon A \rightarrow TM$ induces an isomorphism$\oplus_{i<0}\graded(A)_i \cong \graded(TM)$, and model data $\tau $ compatible with thefiltration is said to be {\df regular} if the induced bracket on $\graded(TM)$ is minusone times the canonical bracket (the one induced by the Jacobi-Lie bracket on vectorfields).\footnote{The minus sign requirement is connected to the convention adopted here,  and in most Lie groupoid literature, that brackets on the Lie algebras of Lie groups are  defined by {\em right}-invariant vector fields.} It is not hard to show that thecanonical model data associated with any filtered Klein geometry, and in particular thecanonical model data associated with a generalized flag manifold, is regular (Proposition\ref{homogeneous}).A parabolic geometry is {\df regular} if the associated model data $\tau $ is regular.\subsection{Reduction}\label{reduction_}The concrete manifestations of parabolic geometries (their ``underlying geometricstructures'') are typically obtained by a applying a reduction procedure.  Let$({\mathcal G}, \tau)$ be a parabolic geometry and $D$ the compatible Cartan connection.\begin{proposition}[Proposition \ref{subgroups}]  In a parabolic geometry $({\mathcal G}, \tau)$ let  ${\mathcal G}^1 \subset {\mathcal G} $ denote the source-connected subgroupoid  integrating $A^1$.  Then ${\mathcal G}^1 $ is a bundle of simply-connected nilpotent Lie  groups; it is also a normal subgroupoid of ${\mathcal G}$, so that  ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$ is a Lie groupoid, also transitive.\end{proposition}\begin{remark}  It follows from Remark \ref{canonical_filtrations_} that that $A^0/A^1$ is fibre-wise  reductive, so that ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$ has reductive vertex groups.\end{remark}Since we suppose ${\mathcal G} $ is firmly filtered, we have a grading-preservingrepresentation, $\Adjoint$, of ${\mathcal G} $ on $\graded(A^0)$.  Moreover, it is easy tosee that ${\mathcal G}^1$ acts trivially here (Proposition \eqrefs{weyl2}{xerox1}).  So$\Adjoint$ descends to a representation of ${\mathcal H} := {\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$on $\graded(A^0)$. This canonical representation is extended by a representation of${\mathcal H}$ on $\graded(A)$ induced by the representation $\bar D$ of ${\mathcal G} $on $A$, which we denote by $R^D$.The observations of the preceding paragraph carry over to the canonical, infinitesimalrepresentation, $\adjoint$, of $A$ on $A^0$, delivering a canonical representation of$B := A/A^1$ on $\graded(A^0)$ and an extension $R^\nabla $ of this representation to oneof $B$ on $\graded(A)$, which are simply infinitesimalizations of the global ones:$R^\nabla =dR^D$.If we write $T := \graded_0(\tau)$, then $(\graded(A), T)$ is a graded Lie algebra bundlewith the same semisimple Lie algebra model as $(A, \tau )$ (by Lemma\ref{gradings_from_filtrations}).  By transitivity, we have$\oplus_{i<0}\graded(A)_i \cong \graded(TM)$, so we obtain a representation of${\mathcal H} $ on $\graded(TM)$. On the other hand, we can use the Killing form of $T$,or any fixed scalar multiple, to identify $\graded(A^1)=\oplus_{i>0}\graded(A)_i$ with$(\oplus_{i<0}\graded(A)_i)^*\cong \graded(TM)^*\cong\graded(T^*\!M)$ (see Lemma\ref{parabolics_from_filtrations}). Under this identification, the representation $R^D$ of${\mathcal H} $ on $\graded(T^*\!M)$ must be the dual of the one on $\graded(TM)$, becausethe Killing form of $T$ will be $R^D$-invariant, on account of the $\bar D$-invariance of$\tau $. Finally, we notice that $A^0/A^1 = B^0$, where $B^0$ is the anchor of $B$.In summary, we have\begin{equation}  \graded(A) \cong \graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus \graded(T^*\!M),\label{soldering}\end{equation}supporting the semisimple Lie algebra bundle structure $T$ (c.f.~Examples \ref{projective}and \ref{conformal}) and with ${\mathcal H}$ acting on each component.\begin{remark}  By our preceding Remark, $B^0$ is a fibre-wise reductive Levi subalgebra of the  parabolic $B^0 \oplus \graded(T^*\!M)$.\end{remark}% \begin{proposition}%   Let $R^D$ denote the representation of ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$ on $\graded(TM)$%   obtained by restricting $\bar D$ above to a representation of%   ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$ on $\oplus_{i<0}\graded(A)_i$, and by identifying%   $\oplus_{i<0}\graded(A)_i$ with $\graded(TM)$. Here $C, C' \in \Gamma(B^0)$,        $X \in \Gamma(\graded(TM))$, and $Z\in \Gamma(\graded(T^*\!M))$.      \item The grading of \eqref{fug_} is compatible with $T$.\label{levi6}      \item The natural pairing of $\graded(TM)$ and $\graded(T^*\!M)$ coincides with that        induced by some multiple of the Killing form of $T$.\label{killing_duality}\end{conditions}\noindent Notice that \eqref{levi6} and Proposition \eqrefs{canonical_filtrations_}{lqq}ensure that $B^0 \oplus \graded(T^*\!M) $ is fibre-wise $T$-parabolic. It is not requiredthat $T$ restrict to the canonical Lie algebra bundle structure on $\graded(TM)$ (when$TM$ gets the filtration pushed forward by the anchor of $B$). If this restriction is the{\em negative} of the canonical one, then the geometry is {\df regular}.\begin{proposition}  Let ${\mathcal {\mathcal H}}$, $T$ and $B$ assume the meanings of \ref{reduction_}, and  let $R:= R^D$ be the grading-preserving representation of ${\mathcal H}$ on  $\graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus \graded(T^*\!M)$ described there. Then  $({\mathcal H}, T, R)$ is a Levi structure called the {\df Levi reduction} of  $({\mathcal G}, \tau)$.\end{proposition}\begin{proof}  Our observations under \ref{reduction_} establish everything that is not obvious, with  the exception of property \eqref{levi3_}, which follows from the strong  compatibility of $\tau $ and $D$.\end{proof}If $B$ in the definition of a Levi structure is an arbitrary Lie algebroid (not necessarilyintegrated by some Lie groupoid ${\mathcal H} $) and $R$ now denotes some {\em  infinitesimal} action of $B$ on \eqref{fug_}, and we replace $dR$ in \eqref{levi3_} with$R$, then the preceding conditions define an {\df infinitesimal Levi structure} and, as wewill see later, the immediately preceding proposition has an infinitesimal analogue. Wehave already encountered a simple example of an infinitesimal Levi structure, namelyconformal geometry. If the Levi structure is regular, then the parabolic geometry can  be chosen to be regular.\end{theorem}The model data $\tau $ is not uniquely defined by the Levi structure, but determined up tochoices of a suitable tensor${\mathsf P} \in \Gamma(\graded(T^*\!M)\otimes \graded(T^*\!M))$ called the {\df Rho  tensor}, splitting of the anchor $\# \colon B \rightarrow TM $, and splitting of thefiltration of $TM$. By definition, the model curvature of a parabolicgeometry $({\mathcal G}, \tau)$, with Lie algebroid $A$ and compatible Cartan connection$D$, is the section $\kappa$ of $\wedge^2 T^*\!M \otimes A$ defined by\begin{equation*}  \kappa(\#X, \#Y) = \torsion \bar\nabla (X, Y) - \tau(X, Y); \qquad X, Y \in \Gamma(A),\end{equation*}where $\nabla $ is the infinitesimalization of $D$. Here $\#$ is the anchor and$$\torsion \bar\nabla(X, Y) = \bar\nabla_XY - \bar\nabla_XY - [X, Y] = \nabla_{\#Y} X -\nabla_{\# X} Y + [X, Y].$$ That $\kappa $ is well defined follows from the strongcompatibility of $\tau $ and $D$. Significantly, $\kappa = 0$ implies$\curvature \nabla =0$ (Proposition \ref{model_curvature}). That is, $\kappa $ is also anobstruction to symmetry, but of the kind prescribed {\em a priori} by the choice of theLie algebra modeled by $\tau $. The model curvature has the interpretation of a differencebetween a ``deformed'' Lie bracket, defined by the Cartan connection (see Theorem\ref{curvature}), and a bona fide Lie bracket, prescribed by the model data. Finally, wenote that in the regular case, it is easy to see that $\kappa $ is {\df homogeneous of  degree one}, meaning $\kappa(U,V) \in A^{j+k+1}$, whenever $U$ and $V$ are tangentvectors in $T^jM$ and $T^kM$ respectively.The model curvature is the Liealgebroid analogue of ``curvature'', as that term is generally understood in the Cartangeometry literature.As we have observed, there is considerable freedom in the choice of model data $\tau $appearing in the existence Theorem \ref{existence}, although we will always have$\graded_0(\tau) = T$. Since $A^0 \subset A$ is fibre-wise parabolic, we have$A^0 \perp A^1$, where $\perp$ is with respect to the Killing form of $\tau $. Therefore,fixing some multiple of this form, as we did for $T$ in \ref{reduction_} and \ref{levi_},we can use it to identify $(A/A^0)^*$ with $A^1$. But, by transitivity, $A/A^0\cong TM $,because $A^0$ is the anchor kernel (\eqrefs{parabolic_defined}{parabolic1}), and weconclude $T^*\!M \cong A^1$. In this way, we may view $\kappa$ as a section of$\wedge^2 A^1 \otimes A$.As the bracket $\tau $ makes the vector bundle $A$ into a representation of the Liealgebra bundle $A^1$, $\wedge^2 A^1 \otimes A $ appears in a natural chain complex\begin{equation}  \label{chain1_} 0 \leftpartial{\tau} A \leftpartial{\tau} A^1 \otimes A\leftpartial{\tau} \wedge^2 A^1 \otimes A \leftpartial{\tau} \cdots.\end{equation} Over each point $m \in M$, this is the classical chain complex associatedwith the representation $\adjoint^\tau$ of the Lie algebra $A^1|_m$ on $A|_m$.Following \cite{Cap_Slovak_09},we will refer to $\partial^\tau $ as the {\df Kostant codifferential}.Basic facts about such complexes immediately carryover to the case of Lie algebra bundle representations and we use such facts withoutfurther comment. By definition (c.f.,\cite[p.~262]{Cap_Slovak_09}),%\begin{multline} \partial^\tau(Y_0 \wedge \cdots \wedge Y_n \otimes X) =\sum_{i=0}^n(-1)^{i+1} Y_0 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{Y_i} \wedge \cdots \wedge Y_n\otimes \tau(Y_i,X)\\ +\sum_{i<j}(-1)^{i+j}\tau(Y_i,Y_j) \wedge Y_0 \wedge \cdots \wedge\widehat{Y_i} \wedge \cdots \widehat{Y_j} \wedge \cdots \wedge Y_n \otimes X.\label{form1}\end{multline}%For example,$\partial^\tau(Y_0\wedge Y_1 \otimes X) = Y_0 \otimes \tau(Y_1, X) - Y_1 \otimes \tau(Y_0,X) -\tau(Y_0, Y_1)\otimes X$. Curiously, using the strong compatibility of $\tau$ and$\nabla $, we can express $\partial^\tau $ in terms of the Cartan connection $\nabla $instead,\begin{multline*} \partial^\tau(Y_0 \wedge \cdots \wedge Y_n \otimes X) =\sum_{i=0}^n(-1)^{i+1} Y_0 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{Y_i} \wedge \cdots \wedge Y_n\otimes \bar\nabla_{Y_i}X\\ +\sum_{i<j}(-1)^{i+j}[Y_i,Y_j] \wedge Y_0 \wedge \cdots \wedge\widehat{Y_i} \wedge \cdots \widehat{Y_j} \wedge \cdots \wedge Y_n \otimes X.\label{form1}\end{multline*}but emphasize $\partial^\tau $ is not a differential operator, just an algebraic one.Model data $\tau $ in a parabolic geometry is {\df normal} if $\partial^\tau \kappa =0$.Our proof of the following is an adaption of that of \cite[Propositions 3.1.10,3.1.13]{Cap_Slovak_09}:\begin{theorem}[Theorem \ref{normalization_}] Let $({\mathcal G}, \tau)$ be a regular  parabolic geometry. Then there exists normal model data $\tau'$ such that  $({\mathcal G} , \tau')$ is a regular parabolic geometry with the same Levi reduction as  $({\mathcal G}, \tau)$.\end{theorem}\subsection{Harmonic curvature}\label{harmonic}In a regular parabolic geometry with normal model data $\tau $, the homology class$\kappa_{\mathrm H} := [\kappa] \in H_2(A^1, A)$ is called the {\df harmonic  curvature}. While naively it seems the harmonic curvature contains less information, it  is nevertheless a complete obstruction to flatness, as the corollary below shows.It is not too hard to see that the filtration of $A$ induces a filtration of$H_k(A^1, A)$. However, according to \eqref{poker2} below, something much stronger anduseful is true:\begin{theorem}[Proposition \ref{homology}, Proposition \ref{top_curvature}]  Let $\kappa $ be the model curvature of a regular and normal parabolic geometry  $({\mathcal G} , \tau)$, and let $k \ge 0$ be any integer. Then:  \begin{conditions}     \item In a natural representation of ${\mathcal G} $ on $H_k(A^1, A)$ defined by the    compatible connection $D$, the subgroupoid ${\mathcal G}^1$ acts trivially, i.e.,    $H_k(A^1, A)$ is a representation of ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$.\label{poker1}  \item $H_k(A^1, A)$ admits a natural grading compatible with it's natural filtration,    allowing harmonic curvature to be unambiguously split into grading components,    $\kappa_{\mathrm H} = \sum_{i\ge 1}(\kappa_{\mathrm H})_i$.\label{poker2}     \item  If $\kappa $ is homogeneous of $l \ge 1$ and $(\kappa_{\mathrm H})_l = 0$, then       $\kappa $ is homogeneous of degree $l + 1$.\label{poker3}  \end{conditions}\end{theorem}\begin{remark}  As noted in Remark \ref{reduction_}, the Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$ has  reductive vertex groups, so that \eqref{poker1} shows that harmonic curvature takes  values in a vector bundle with reductive structure group.\end{remark}Obtain the following by applying \eqref{poker3} inductively:\begin{corollary}[Theorem 3.1.12, p.~265, \cite{Cap_Slovak_09}]  In a normal, regular parabolic geometry, $\kappa_{\mathrm H} =0$ implies $\kappa =0$.\end{corollary}\subsection{Uniqueness up to Weyl gauge transformations}\label{uniqueness}The inner automorphisms of an arbitrary Lie groupoid are those given by conjugation bybisections. When ${\mathcal G} $ is firmly filtered, a special role is played by thoseinner automorphisms generated by bisections of ${\mathcal G}^1 \subset {\mathcal G}$, thebundle of simply-connected nilpotent Lie groups integrating the totally intransitive Liesubalgebroid $A^1 \subset A$, where a bisection is the same thing as a section. We callsuch automorphisms {\df Weyl gauge transformations}, for reasons explained in\ref{gauge_transformations}. By definition, there is, for each such transformation, some$Z \in \Gamma(A^1)$ such that each arrow $g \in {\mathcal G} $, beginning at some$m \in M$, is mapped to $\exp(Z(gm))g\exp(-Z(m))$. We give the same name to theinfinitesimal analogues, namely those automorphisms of $A$ of the form$$X \mapsto e^{\adjoint_Z}X:=X + [Z, X] + \frac{1}{2!}[Z, [Z, X]] + \cdots\enspace,$$ for some$Z \in \Gamma(A^1)$. Both global and infinitesimal Weyl gauge transformations cover theidentity of the base $M$, and $e^{\adjoint_Z}$ is filtration-preserving, by F1.\begin{proposition}[Corollary of Proposition \ref{curv_deform_gauge}]%  If $({\mathcal G}, \tau)$ is a (regular) parabolic geometry, and $\tau'$ the  push-forward of $\tau $ under a Weyl gauge transformation, then $({\mathcal G}, \tau')$  is another (regular) parabolic geometry.\end{proposition}\begin{example}  Let $A$ be a filtered Lie algebroid with $|A| = 1$. Then the infinitesimal gauge  transformations have the form $X \mapsto X + [Z, X]$, for some $Z \in \Gamma(A^1)$.\end{example}INCOMPLETE SECTION\section{Weyl gauges and the semi-direct product structure}\label{semi-direct}We now return to the general study of filtered Lie algebroids introduced in Section\ref{introduction}, our main goal being the generalization of Theorem \ref{intro_prolong}to Lie algebroids and Lie groupoids. The idea of the proof of Theorem \ref{intro_prolong}is to show that the isotropy $B$ of $\epsilon $ is a complement for ${\mathfrak g}^1 $ in${\mathfrak g} $, and that $B$ preserves the grading of ${\mathfrak g} $ determined by$\epsilon $. For, choosing any $\epsilon \in {\mathfrak g}^0$ such that$\graded_0(\epsilon) = E$, one can show, using induction and $\cap_j {\mathfrak g}^j=0$,that the maps$$ X \mapsto  [\epsilon, X] - jX \colon {\mathfrak g}^j \rightarrow {\mathfrak g}^{j+1}$$are well-defined projections, whose kernels constitute a grading of ${\mathfrak g} $ forwhich $\epsilon$ is a grading element; this in turn gives rise to an isomorphism$\Phi_\epsilon\colon \graded({\mathfrak g}) \rightarrow {\mathfrak g}$ which must in fact be aLie algebra isomorphism. When we alter the choice of $\epsilon$, the isomorphism $\Phi_\epsilon$ changesin a way made precise in \eqref{splittings2} below.We call $\Phi_\epsilon \colon \graded({\mathfrak g}) \rightarrow {\mathfrak g} $ an {\em  $E$-splitting} of the filtration. With $E$ and $\epsilon$ fixed as above, there is evidently aone-to-one correspondence between $E$-splittings and the affine space$\epsilon + {\mathfrak g}^1$, because $\Phi_{\epsilon'}(E) = \epsilon'$ for all $\epsilon' \in \epsilon + {\mathfrak g}^1$.Now ${\mathfrak g}^1$ is a nilpotent Lie algebra, integrated by a simply connectednilpotent Lie group, which we denote by $\exp({\mathfrak g}^1)$; nilpotency ensures theexponential map is a diffeomorphism. Part \eqref{splittings1} of the lemma below showsthat $\exp({\mathfrak g}^1)$ acts freely and transitively on the set of $E$-splittings byconstructing a concrete realization $G^1$. A proof of this known result appears inAppendix \ref{appendix_splittings}.%\begin{lemma}  Let $\epsilon \in {\mathfrak g}^0 $ be a lift of some grading element  $E \in \graded({\mathfrak g})$. Equation \eqref{w2} then holdsif and only if $E$ is $\adjoint$-invariant. That is, in the firm case, Weyl gauges existif and only if an $\adjoint$-invariant fibre-wise grading section of $\graded(A^0)$exists, and any lift to $A^0$ of such a section is a Weyl gauge. In fact, according to thecorollary stated below all Weyl gauges arise in this way.Note that we do not require that $E$ be a fibre-wise grading section of$\graded(A)$, only of $\graded(A^0)$ (but see also \ref{model_data_gradings}).\begin{theorem}  Let $A$ be a filtered Lie algebroid admitting a Weyl gauge $\epsilon$, and let $B \subset A$  denote the isotropy of $\epsilon$ under the canonical representation of $A$ on its anchor  kernel, i.e., the kernel of the map  $X \mapsto [X, \epsilon] \colon A \rightarrow A^1$. Then:  \begin{conditions}  \item $A$ is firmly filtered, and in particular, $A^1 \subset A$ is an ideal (i.e.,    $\Gamma(A^1) \subset \Gamma(A)$ is an ideal).\label{pq1}  \item $A$ is the internal semi-direct product of $B$ and $A^1$.\label{pq2}  \item The grading $A^0=\oplus_{i\ge0}{\mathfrak a}_i$ associated with $\epsilon$ is    invariant under the canonical representation of $B$. In particular, the splitting    $\Phi_\epsilon \colon \graded(A^1)\rightarrow A^1$ intertwines the representation of    $A/A^1\cong B$ on $\graded(A^1)$ with the representation of $B$ on $A^1$, so that $A$    is the external semi-direct product of $A/A^1$ and $\graded(A^1)$.\label{pq3}  \item Under the identification $A \cong A/A^1 \oplus_{\adjoint}\graded(A^1)$ of    \eqref{pq3}, the Weyl gauge $\epsilon$ takes the form $\epsilon=(E,0)$, for some section $E$ of    $A^0/A^1 \subset A/A^1$ that commutes under Lie algebroid bracket with all    sections of $A/A^1$; and $\adjoint_EY = jY$ for any $Y \in \graded(A^0)_j$.\label{pq4}  \end{conditions}\end{theorem}\noindent%Note that identification $A \cong A/A^1 \oplus_{\adjoint}\graded(A^1)$ transfers to$A/A^1 \oplus_{\adjoint}\graded(A^1)$ the following filtration:$(A/A^1 \oplus_{\adjoint}\graded(A^1))^i= (A^i/A^0)\oplus_{\adjoint}(\oplus_{j\ge\max\{1,i\}}\graded(A)_j)$.\begin{example}[Projective geometry] In example \ref{projective} the Lie algebroid  $A \subset J^1 (TM)$ acts on $TM$ in the usual way, $\adjoint_{J^1 V}W = [V,W]$, which  gives us associated representations of $A$ on $\gl(TM)$ and $T^*\!M $. On each component  of $\graded(A^0)=\gl(TM)\oplus T^*\!M$ the infinitesimal adjoint action of $A$ is given  by these representations, which shows that  $E = -\identity \in \gl(TM) \subset \graded(A^0)$ is $\adjoint$-invariant. Consequently,  any lift $\epsilon $ of $-\identity$ through $A^0 \rightarrow A^0/A^1 = \gl(TM)$ is a  Weyl gauge for $A$. According to the theorem, each such gauge defines an identification  of $A$ with the semi-direct product $J^1 (TM) \oplus_{\adjoint}T^*\!M $.\end{example}\begin{proof}[Proof of theorem]  The isotropy $B \subset A$ is a subalgebroid, by Jacobi's identity. We first prove  \begin{equation}     A = B \oplus A^1, \label{y7}  \end{equation}  as vector bundles. It suffices to show that the restriction $r$ of  $X \mapsto [X, \epsilon]$ to a map $A^1 \rightarrow A^1$ is an isomorphism. However, by  F1, this restriction is filtration-preserving, and the corresponding map  $\graded(A^1) \rightarrow \graded(A^1)$ sends $\xi \in \graded(A^1)_j$ to $j \xi$,  because $\graded_0(\epsilon)$ is a fibre-wise grading section of $\graded(A^0)$. As this  is obviously an isomorphism, $r$ must be too.  Next we prove the grading $A^0=\oplus_{i\ge0}{\mathfrak a}_i$ associated with $\epsilon$ is  invariant under the canonical representation of $B$. To that end, suppose  $Y \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_j)$ for some $j\ge 0$ and let $X \in \Gamma(B)$ be  arbitrary. The Jacobi identity for the bracket on $A$ gives us  \begin{equation*}    [X, [\epsilon, Y]] = [[X, \epsilon], Y] + [\epsilon, [X, Y]].  \end{equation*}  On the other hand, applying $\adjoint_X$ to both sides of the  identity $[\epsilon, Y] = jY$, we have  \begin{equation*}     [X, [\epsilon, Y]] = j\adjoint_XY.  \end{equation*}  Combining these two results, we obtain  \begin{equation*}    [[X, \epsilon], Y] + [\epsilon, \adjoint_XY] = j \adjoint_XY.  \end{equation*}  But $[X, \epsilon] = 0 $, because $X \in \Gamma(B)$. We conclude that  $\adjoint_XY \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_j)$.  Having proven, in particular, that $B$ preserves the filtration of $A^0$, to establish  \eqref{pq1} it suffices, in view of \eqref{y7}, to show that the filtration of $A^0$ is  invariant under the canonical action of itself, which follows immediately from F1.  Claim \eqref{pq2} follows from \eqref{pq1} and \eqref{y7}. The first part of \eqref{pq3}  has already been addressed, and the rest now follows easily. Part \eqref{pq4} is now also  clear.\end{proof}\begin{corollary}\label{ew1}  A filtered Lie algebroid $A$ admits a Weyl gauge if and only if it is firmly  filtered and $\graded(A^0)$ admits an $\adjoint$-invariant fibre-wise grading section $E$.\end{corollary}\begin{proof}  Given a firmly filtered Lie algebroid $A$ with $\adjoint$-invariant fibre-wise grading  section $E$ of $\graded(A^0)$, any lift of $E$ to a section $\epsilon \in \Gamma(A^0)$  is immediately seen to be a Weyl gauge. The converse statement follows from the theorem.\end{proof}If $A$ is a filtered Lie algebroid, then $A^1$, which lies in the anchor kernel of $A$, isa nilpotent Lie algebra bundle, and is accordingly integrated by a bundle ofsimply-connected nilpotent Lie groups, denoted $\exp(A^1)$.\begin{remark}  By simple-connectivity, the adjoint action of $A^1$ on $A^0$ integrates to an action  $\Adjoint$ of $\exp(A^1)$ on $A^0$. We have the identity  $\Adjoint_{\exp(Z)}X=e^{-\adjoint_Z}X$, for any $Z \in \Gamma(A^1)$ and  $X \in \Gamma(A^0)$, but the right-hand side makes sense for any $X \in \Gamma(A)$.\end{remark}\begin{proposition}  Let $A$ be a filtered Lie algebroid admitting a Weyl gauge, and ${\mathcal G} $ any Lie  groupoid integrating $A$. Then the source-connected Lie subgroupoid  ${\mathcal G}^1 \subset {\mathcal G} $ integrating $A^1$ is a bundle of simply-connected  nilpotent Lie groups isomorphic to $\exp(A^1)$.\end{proposition}\begin{proof}  We prove the first part of the conclusion, as the second part follows from the first, by  Lie I for Lie groupoids. By axiom F2, $A^0$ and $A^1$ lie in the kernel of the anchor of  $A$, and so both are bundles of Lie algebras (totally intransitive). The  source-connectedness of ${\mathcal G}^1$ now implies that ${\mathcal G}^1 $ is totally  intransitive (its source and target fibres coincide) so that these fibres are connected  Lie groups. These Lie groups are subgroups of the fibres of the source-connected  subgroupoid ${\mathcal G}^0 \subset {\mathcal G}$ integrating $A^0$ (also a bundle of  Lie groups). With a Weyl gauge $\epsilon$ in hand, we may apply Lemma  \eqrefs{splittings}{splittings1} fibre-wise to show that these groups are in fact  simply-connected and nilpotent.\end{proof}\subsection{Filtered Lie groupoids and Weyl gauges}\label{weyl2}Let ${\mathcal G} $ be a filtered Lie groupoid over $M$. The filtration need not be firm;see \ref{filtered_groupoids} for definitions. We call $\epsilon  \in \Gamma(A)$ a {\df Weyl gauge}for ${\mathcal G} $ if it is a Weyl gauge for $A$, and if for every arrow$g \in {\mathcal G}$ with source $m \in M$ we have\begin{equation}\label{tui}   \Adjoint_g \epsilon(m) - \epsilon(gm) \in A^1(gm),\end{equation}where $gm$ denotes the target of $g$. If ${\mathcal G} $ is firmly filtered, then thiscondition holds if and only if $E= \graded_0(\epsilon)$ is ${\mathcal G}$-invariant under$\Adjoint$, in which case the requirement \eqrefs{weyl}{w2} is automatic.\begin{proposition}  Let ${\mathcal G} $ be a filtered Lie groupoid with Lie algebroid $A$ and  ${\mathcal G}^1 \subset {\mathcal G} $ the source-connected Lie subgroupoid integrating  $A^1$.  \begin{conditions}    \item Under $\Adjoint$, ${\mathcal G}^1$ acts trivially on $\graded(A^0)$.\label{xerox1}    \item If ${\mathcal G} $ admits a Weyl gauge then ${\mathcal G}^1$ is a bundle of      simply-connected nilpotent Lie groups isomorphic to $\exp(A^1)$.\label{xerox2}  \end{conditions}\end{proposition}\begin{proof}  Part \eqref{xerox1} holds because $A^1$ already acts trivially on $\graded(A^0)$ by F1,  and ${\mathcal G}^1$ has connected source-fibres. Part \eqref{xerox2} is an immediate  corollary of Proposition \ref{weyl}.\end{proof}To navigate a mild obstruction to obtaining semi-direct products in the groupoidcase, we will say the filtration of $\mathcal G$ is {\df weakly firm} if $A^1$ is${\mathcal G} $-invariant under $\Adjoint$. Evidently a firm filtration of${\mathcal G} $ is weakly firm.\begin{remark}  Suppose ${\mathcal G} $ is filtered and admits a Weyl gauge $\epsilon$. Then if $\mathcal G$ has  connected source fibres, then we claim it is weakly firm. Indeed, $\epsilon$ will be a  Weyl gauge for the Lie algebroid $A$ of ${\mathcal G} $, so that $A^1 \subset A$ must be an ideal, by  Theorem \eqrefs{weyl}{pq1}. Our claim follows now from the connectedness assumption.\end{remark}Suppose ${\mathcal G} $ is firmly filtered, so that ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1 $ acts on$\graded(A^0)$, by \eqref{xerox1}. This action, also denoted $\Adjoint$, preserves the Liealgebra bundle bracket. Now, let $\exp(\graded(A^1))$ denote the totally intransitive Liegroupoid with simply-connected fibres that integrates $\graded(A^1)$, a Lie algebra bundlewith nilpotent Lie algebra model. Then the action just mentioned integrates to an actionof ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$ on $\exp(\graded(A^1))$, also denoted $\Adjoint$,preserving the totally intransitive Lie groupoid structure, i.e., it maps each (nilpotent)Lie group fibre isomorphically onto another Lie group fibre. We can accordingly definethe semi-direct product ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1 \times_{\Adjoint}\exp(\graded(A^1))$,and have the following globalization of Theorem \ref{weyl}.\begin{theorem}  Let ${\mathcal G} $ be a weakly firm, filtered Lie groupoid with Lie algebroid  $A$. Define ${\mathcal G}^1 \subset {\mathcal G} $ as in the proposition above, and  suppose ${\mathcal G} $ admits a Weyl gauge $\epsilon$.  Then, if  ${\mathcal H} \subset {\mathcal G} $ denotes the isotropy subgroupoid of $\epsilon$ under the  canonical representation of ${\mathcal G} $, then:  \begin{conditions}  \item ${\mathcal G}^1 \subset {\mathcal G} $ is normal,\label{pr1}  \item ${\mathcal G}={\mathcal G}^1{\mathcal H} $ is the internal semi-direct product of ${\mathcal H} $ and    ${\mathcal G}^1$,\label{pr2}  \item The grading $A^0=\oplus_{i\ge0}{\mathfrak a}_i$ associated with $\epsilon$ is    invariant under the canonical representation of ${\mathcal H}$. \label{pr3}  \item ${\mathcal G} $ is firmly filtered.\label{pr4}  \item As a Lie groupoid, ${\mathcal G} $ is identifiable with the external semi-direct    product ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1    \times_{\Adjoint}\exp(\graded(A^1))$. Specifically, let    $\Phi_\epsilon \colon \graded(A^1) \rightarrow A^1$ denote the splitting associated with    $\epsilon$, and let $\tilde \Phi_\epsilon \colon \exp(\graded(A^1)) \rightarrow {\mathcal G}^1$ be    the morphism of totally intransitive Lie groupoids that integrates $\Phi_e$.  For    $g \in {\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$, define $g^{\mathcal H} \in {\mathcal H} $ by the    requirement $g^{\mathcal H} {\mathcal G}^1 = g$.  Then the map    $$(g, k) \mapsto \tilde \Phi_\epsilon(k) g^{\mathcal H} \colon {\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1    \times_{\Adjoint}\exp(\graded(A^1)) \rightarrow {\mathcal G}$$ is a Lie groupoid    isomorphism.\label{pr5}  \end{conditions}\end{theorem}\begin{proof}  Since we assume ${\mathcal G} $ is weakly firm, ${A^1 \subset A}$ is invariant under the  canonical representation $\Adjoint$ of ${\mathcal G} $ on ${\mathcal G}^1$. But, by the  proposition, the exponential map for ${\mathcal G}^1$ is a diffeomorphism, and we will  have $g\exp(Z)g^{-1} = \exp(\Adjoint_gZ)$ for any arrow $g \in {\mathcal G} $ beginning  at the base point of each $Z \in A^1$. This proves \eqref{pr1} because the filtration is  weakly firm.  To prove \eqref{pr2} we now show each arrow $g \in {\mathcal G}$, beginning at some  $m \in M$, can be written uniquely as $g = kh$, with $k \in {\mathcal G}^1$ and  $h \in {\mathcal G} $. Indeed, from \eqref{tui} we have  $\Adjoint_g\epsilon(m) \in \epsilon(gm) + A^1|_{gm}$. But, according to Lemma  \eqrefs{splittings}{splittings1}, the Lie group ${\mathcal G}^1_{gm}$ (the source=target  fibre of ${\mathcal G}^1$ at $gm \in M$) acts freely and transitively on the affine  space $\epsilon(gm) + A^1|_{gm}$. Therefore there exists a unique $k \in {\mathcal G}^1$ such  that $\Adjoint_g\epsilon(m) = \Adjoint_k \epsilon(gm)$. But this means  $\Adjoint_{k^{-1}g}\epsilon(m) = \epsilon(gm) = \epsilon(k^{-1}gm)$, i.e., $k^{-1}g \in {\mathcal H} $.  To prove \eqref{pr3}, suppose $Y \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_j)$ for some $j\ge 0$ and let  $h \in {\mathcal H} $ be an arbitrary arrow beginning at some $m \in M$. Since the  bracket on $A^0$ is ${\mathcal G}$-invariant under $\Adjoint$, the grading identity  $[\epsilon, Y](m) = jY(m)$ gives us $[\Adjoint_h\epsilon(m), \Adjoint_hY(m)] = j\Adjoint_hY(m)$. From  the definition of ${\mathcal H} $, we conclude that  $[\epsilon(hm), \Adjoint_hY(m)] = j\Adjoint_hY(m)$, establishing that  $\Adjoint_hY(m) \in {\mathfrak a}_j$, as required.  In view of the fact that ${\mathcal G} = {\mathcal G}^1 {\mathcal H} $, and having  established \eqref{pr3}, it suffices, in proving \eqref{pr4}, to show that the  filtration of $A^0$ is invariant under the action of ${\mathcal G}^1$. But $A^0$ is  already invariant under the action of $A^1$ by F1. Since ${\mathcal G}^1$ is  source-connected, the filtration of $A^0$ is therefore invariant under the action of  ${\mathcal G}^1$.  Part \eqref{pr5} is a rather straightforward consequence of the other parts, whose  proof is omitted.\end{proof}\begin{corollary}\label{ew2}  A weakly firm, filtered Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G} $ with Lie algebroid $A$ admits a  Weyl gauge if and only if it is firmly filtered and $\graded(A^0)$ admits an  $\Adjoint$-invariant fibre-wise grading section $E$.\end{corollary}\begin{proof}  Given an $\Adjoint$-invariant fibre-wise grading section $E$ of $\graded(A^0)$, any lift  to a section of $A^0$ is a Weyl gauge for $\mathcal G$, as is immediately verified. The  converse statement follows from the theorem.\end{proof}\subsection{Weyl gauge transformations}\label{gauge_transformations}Let $A$ be a firmly filtered Lie algebroid over $M$ and $\epsilon$ a Weyl gaugelifting some grading section $E$ of $\graded(A^0)$. Then, applying Theorem \ref{weyl}, weobtain an isomorphism$\phi_\epsilon \colon (A/A^1)\oplus_{\adjoint}\graded(A^1) \xrightarrow{\sim} A$. A newWeyl gauge $\epsilon'$, lifting $E$, determines a second isomorphism$\phi_{\epsilon'} \colon (A/A^1)\oplus_{\adjoint}\graded(A^1) \xrightarrow{\sim} A$, sothat $\phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \phi_\epsilon^{-1} \colon A \rightarrow A$ is afiltration-preserving Lie algebroid automorphism covering the identity on $M$, and mappingthe gauge $\epsilon$ to $\epsilon'$. We may construct automorphisms of a firmly filteredLie groupoid in a similar way, using Theorem \ref{weyl2}.\begin{theorem}  The automorphisms of firmly filtered Lie algebroids and Lie groupoids described above  are precisely the Weyl gauge transformations defined in \ref{uniqueness}.\end{theorem}\begin{proof}[Proof of the {\bfseries infinitesimal case}]  Let $\epsilon $, $\epsilon'$, $\phi_\epsilon $ and $\phi_{\epsilon'}$ have the meanings  above. Then it is not hard to see, from the statement of Theorem \ref{weyl}, that  $\phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \phi_\epsilon^{-1} $ is the unique Lie algebroid isomorphism  $\varphi$ covering the identity such that:  \begin{conditions}  \item \label{gold1} $\varphi(B_\epsilon) = B_{\epsilon'}$, where $B_\epsilon$ denotes    the isotropy of $\epsilon $ under the canonical representation of $A$ on its anchor    kernel.  \item \label{gold2} $\varphi(X) \equiv X \modulo A^1$, for all $X \in \Gamma(A)$.  \item \label{gold3} $\varphi(Y) = \Phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \Phi_\epsilon^{-1}(Y)$ for all    $Y\in \Gamma(A^1)$, where $\Phi_\epsilon \colon \graded(A^1) \rightarrow A^1$ denotes    the splitting determined by $\epsilon $.  \end{conditions}  Now, a fibre-wise application of Lemma \eqrefs{splittings}{splittings1} show that there  exists $Z \in \Gamma(A^1)$ such that  $\epsilon' = \Adjoint_{\exp(Z)}\epsilon = e^{-\adjoint_Z}\epsilon $. See also Remark  \ref{weyl}. To show $\phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \phi_\epsilon^{-1}$ is a Weyl gauge  transformation, it suffices to show that the Weyl gauge transformation  $\varphi:=e^{-\adjoint_Z}$ satisfies the above conditions. But \eqref{gold1} follows  immediately, by construction, because $e^{-\adjoint_Z}$ is a Lie algebroid morphism;  \eqref{gold2} follows from the expansion  $e^{-\adjoint_Z}X:=X -[Z, X] + \frac{1}{2!}[Z, [Z, X]] - \cdots$ and the fact that  $\Gamma(A^1) \subset \Gamma(A)$ is an ideal ($A$ is firmly filtered); while  \eqref{gold3} follows from the fibre-wise application of Lemma  \eqrefs{splittings}{splittings2}.  It remains to show {\em every} Weyl gauge transformation $e^{-\adjoint_Z}$ is of the  form $\phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \phi_\epsilon^{-1}$ for some $\epsilon $ and  $\epsilon'$. But the above argument already shows this, if we choose $\epsilon $ to be  any Weyl gauge and $\epsilon' := e^{-\adjoint_Z}(\epsilon)$.\end{proof}\begin{proof}[Proof of the {\bfseries global case}]  Let ${\mathcal G} $ be a firmly filtered Lie groupoid with Lie algebroid $A$, and let  $\epsilon$ and $\epsilon'$ have the meanings above. Applying Theorem \ref{weyl2}, we  obtain Lie groupoid isomorphisms  $\phi_\epsilon, \phi_{\epsilon'} \colon {\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1  \times_{\Adjoint}\exp(\graded(A^1)) \rightarrow {\mathcal G}$. From the statement of the  theorem, it is not too hard too see that $\phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \phi_\epsilon^{-1} $ is  the unique Lie groupoid isomorphism $\varphi$ covering the identity such that:  \begin{conditions}  \item \label{silver1} $\varphi({\mathcal H}_\epsilon) = {\mathcal H}_{\epsilon'}$, where    ${\mathcal H}_\epsilon$ denotes the isotropy of $\epsilon $ under the canonical    representation of ${\mathcal G}$ on the anchor kernel of $A$.  \item \label{silver2} $\varphi(g){\mathcal G}^1 \equiv g {\mathcal G}^1$, for all    $g \in {\mathcal G} $.  \item \label{silver3} $d\varphi(Y) = \Phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \Phi_\epsilon^{-1}(Y)$ for all    $Y\in \Gamma(A^1)$, where $\Phi_\epsilon \colon \graded(A^1) \rightarrow A^1$ denotes    the splitting determined by $\epsilon $.  \end{conditions}  In convincing herself of this, the reader will keep in mind that  ${\mathcal G}^1\cong\exponential(A^1)$ and $\exponential(\graded(A^1))$ are  source simply-connected, so that a groupoid morphism having one of these as domain is  determined completely by its infinitesimalization.  Now choose $Z \in \Gamma(A^1)$ as in the infinitesimal argument, so that  $\epsilon'=\Adjoint_{\exp(Z)}\epsilon=e^{-\adjoint_Z}\epsilon $. To show that  $\phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \phi_\epsilon^{-1}$ is a Weyl gauge transformation, it suffices  to show that the Weyl gauge transformation $\varphi :=K_{\exp(Z)}$ satisfies the above  conditions, where $K_{\exp(Z)}\colon {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G}$ denotes  conjugation by the (bi)section $\exp(Z)$. To this end, suppose $h \in {\mathcal G} $ is  an arrow beginning at some $m \in M$ and that $h \in {\mathcal H}_\epsilon$. Then  $\Adjoint_h \epsilon(m) = \epsilon(hm)$. Applying $\Adjoint_{\exp(Z)}$ to both sides, we  conclude that $\Adjoint_{K_{\exp(Z)}(h)}\epsilon'(m) = \epsilon'(m)$, showing  $K_{\exp(Z)}(h) \in {\mathcal H}_{\epsilon'}$. We can reverse the roles of $\epsilon $  and $\epsilon' $ and repeat the argument, and conclude that  $K_{\exp(Z)}({\mathcal H}_\epsilon) = {\mathcal H}_{\epsilon'}$, i.e., \eqref{silver1}  holds. The normality of ${\mathcal G}^1$ (Theorem \eqrefs{weyl2}{pr1}) means  $g {\mathcal G}^1 = {\mathcal G}^1g$, for any $g \in {\mathcal G}$. If  $g \in {\mathcal G}$ is an arbitrary arrow, beginning at some $m \in M$, we compute,  appealing to normality twice,    \begin{multline*}      K_{\exp(Z)}(g){\mathcal G}^1 =    {\mathcal G}^1 \exp(Z(gm))g\exp(-Z(m))\\ =    {\mathcal G}^1 g\exp(-Z(m)) =    g\exp(-Z(m)){\mathcal G}^1 =    g{\mathcal G}^1,  \end{multline*}  which proves \eqref{silver2}. Since $dK_{\exp(Z)}=e^{-\adjoint_Z}$, \eqref{silver3}  already follows from the argument given in the infinitesimal case.\footnote{The source    of the minus sign is our convention of using right-invariant vector fields to define    Lie brackets, so that $dK_{\exp(Z)}X=\Adjoint_{J^1\exp(Z)}X=e^{-\adjoint_Z}X$,    $X \in \Gamma(A)$.}  To show every Weyl gauge transformation $K_{\exp(Z)}$ is of the form  $\phi_{\epsilon'}\circ \phi_\epsilon^{-1}$ for some $\epsilon $ and $\epsilon'$, take  $\epsilon $ to be any Weyl gauge, $\epsilon' := e^{-\adjoint_Z}(\epsilon)$, and repeat  the preceding argument.\end{proof}% According to the theorem below, gauge transformations are closed under% composition.% It is not hard to see that a gauge transformation $\varphi$ must satisfy:% \begin{align}%    \varphi(X) &\equiv X \modulo A^1,\quad  X \in \Gamma(A)\label{gauge1}\\%    \varphi(Y) &\equiv Y \modulo A^{j+1},\quad Y \in \Gamma(A^j),\, j > 0.\label{gauge2}% \end{align}% In particular, the second identity follows from a general fact about splittings of a% filtered vector bundle $V$: If $\Phi_1, \Phi_2 \colon \graded(V) \rightarrow V$ are% splittings, then $\Phi_2 \circ \Phi_1^{-1} - \identity$ is homogeneous of degree one.% The group of all filtration-preserving Lie algebroid isomorphisms% $\varphi \colon A \rightarrow A$ satisfying \eqref{gauge1} and \eqref{gauge2} will be% denoted $\GGauge(A)$, a definition that makes sense for an arbitrary filtered Lie% algebroid $A$. The corresponding infinitesimal notion, Cartan connections on Lie algebroids, wereintroduced some time earlier, in \cite{Blaom_06}. The relationship with the classicalnotion is detailed in \cite{Blaom_16b}.The meaning of curvature for these connections is different from the classicalone. Curvature is still an obstruction to symmetry, but the kind of symmetry is notpreordained by the specification of a flat model {\em a priori}.With the exception of Proposition \ref{cartan}, which we presume is new, this section is asummary of definitions and results presented in the cited works and elsewhere. It islogically independent of Sections \ref{introduction} and  \ref{semi-direct}.\subsection{Cartan connections on a Lie groupoid}\label{cartan}%A {\df Cartan connection} on a Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G} $ over $M$ is an $n$-plane field$D \subset T {\mathcal G}$ arising from some right inverse${\mathcal G} \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G} $ for the canonical projection$J^1 {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G} $, which is necessarily uniquely determined by$D$. Here $J^1 {\mathcal G} $ denotes the Lie groupoid of one-jets of bisections of${\mathcal G} $. Of course, we are understanding the right inverse to be a Lie groupoidmorphism. This is equivalent to requiring that $D \subset TM$ be multiplicatively closed,in the sense of \cite{Salazar_13,Crainic_etal_15}, but this interpretation will play norole here.The usual adjoint action of a Lie group on its Lie algebra is generalized by an action of$J^1 {\mathcal G} $ on the Lie algebroid $A$ of ${\mathcal G} $, denoted $\Adjoint$\cite{Blaom_16b}. Composing with the right inverse${\mathcal G} \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G} $ of$J^1 {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G} $ associated with $D$, we obtain arepresentation $\bar D$ of ${\mathcal G}$ on $A$. This representation extends thecanonical one of ${\mathcal G} $ on elements of the anchor kernel of $A$ (which we havealso been denoting by $\Adjoint$ in Section \ref{semi-direct}).The prototype of a Cartan connection is the horizontal $n$-plane field $D$ onan action groupoid $G \times M$, for a smooth action of some Lie group $ G $ on$M$, which need not be transitive. In this case\begin{equation}  \bar D_{(g, m)}(\xi, m) = (\Adjoint_g \xi, g\cdot m).\label{adjoint}\end{equation}Every Cartan connection $D$ on a {\em transitive} Lie groupoid can be recovered from therepresentation $\bar D$:\begin{proposition}  Suppose ${\mathcal G} $ is a Lie groupoid with a transitive Lie algebroid $A$. Then for  every representation $\rho $ of ${\mathcal G} $ on $A$, extending the canonical  representation of ${\mathcal G} $ on the anchor kernel, there exists a unique Cartan  connection $D$ on ${\mathcal G} $ such that $\bar D = \rho $.\end{proposition}\noindent%The infinitesimal version of this result, Proposition \ref{hsd} below, already appears in\cite{Blaom_06}, and has an easy proof. The proposition above is proven in Appendix\ref{rut}.\subsection{Infinitesimal Cartan connections}\lab{hsd}%Infinitesimalizing a Cartan connection, viewed as a Lie groupoidmorphism ${\mathcal G} \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G}$, we obtain a splitting $s\colon A \rightarrow J^1 A $ of the exactsequence\begin{equation*}  0\longrightarrow T^*\!M\otimes A\longrightarrow J^1A\longrightarrow A\longrightarrow 0\end{equation*}in the category of Lie algebroids. Here $J^1 A $ denotes the Lie algebroid of one-jets ofsections of the Lie algebroid $A $ of ${\mathcal G}$. In the category of vector bundles, splittings$s$ of the above sequence are in one-to-one correspondence with Koszul connections$\nabla$ on $A$; this correspondence is given by\footnote{According to our sign  conventions, the inclusion $T^*\!M \otimes A \rightarrow J^1 A $ induces the following  map on sections: $df \otimes X \mapsto f J^1 X - J^1 (f X)$.}\begin{equation}  sX = J^1 X + \nabla X; \qquad  X \in \Gamma(A). \mathlab{cspan}\end{equation}When $s \colon A \rightarrow J^1 A $ is a morphism of Lie algebroids, as is the case here,then $\nabla $ is called a {\df Cartan connection} on $A$ \cite{Blaom_06}, or an {\df  infinitesimal Cartan Connection} when we want to distinguish it from the correspondingglobal notion. Evidently, the notion makes sense for arbitrary Lie algebroids, not onlythose integrated by Lie groupoids. According to \cite{Blaom_06}, $\nabla $ is a Cartanconnection on $A$ if and only if\begin{equation*}  \nabla_V[X, Y] = [\nabla_VX,        Y]+[X,\nabla_VY]-\nabla_{\bar\nabla_XV}Y+\nabla_{\bar\nabla_YV}X,\end{equation*}for all vector fields $V$ on $M$ and sections $X, Y$ of $A$. Here $\bar\nabla$ denotes theassociated $A$-connection on $TM$ defined by\begin{equation} \bar\nabla_XV= \#\nabla_VX +[\#X, V],\end{equation}an $A$-representation when $\bar\nabla $ is Cartan. Here $\# \colon A \rightarrow TM$denotes anchor. Sometimes the following equivalent condition is more useful:\begin{equation}  (\nabla_V\torsion \bar\nabla)(X,Y) = \curvature \nabla (\#X, V)Y -  \curvature \nabla (\#Y, V) X,\end{equation}where\begin{equation*}  \torsion \bar\nabla (X, Y) = \bar\nabla_XY - \bar\nabla_YX - [X, Y] =  \nabla_{\#Y}X - \nabla_{\#X}Y + [X, Y].\end{equation*}Given a Cartan connection $\nabla $ on some Lie algebroid $A$, we can compose thecorresponding splitting $s \colon A \rightarrow J^1 A$ with the adjoint action of $J^1 A$on $ A$, $\adjoint_{J^1X}Y = [X, Y]$, to obtain a self-representation $\bar \nabla $ of$A$. Explicitly, we have\begin{equation} \bar\nabla_XY = \nabla_{\#Y}X +[X, Y].\label{zero}\end{equation}The anchor $\# \colon A \rightarrow TM$ becomes $\bar\nabla$-equivariant.The representation $\bar\nabla$ of $A$ on itself coincides with the canonicalrepresentation of $A$ on sections lying in the anchor kernel (denoted $\adjoint$ inSection \ref{semi-direct}). In the transitive case, $\nabla $ can be recovered from$\bar\nabla $:\begin{proposition}[\mbox{\cite[Proposition 6.1]{Blaom_06}}]  Suppose $A $ is a transitive Lie algebroid. Then for every representation $\rho $ of  $A $ on itself, extending the canonical representation of $A$ on sections lying in the  anchor kernel, there exists a unique Cartan connection $\nabla $ on $A$ such that  $\bar \nabla = \rho $.\end{proposition}\subsection{Curvature as obstruction to symmetry}\label{curvature}The {\df curvature} of $\nabla $ is defined as usual as per Koszul connections. Thisnotion of curvature is not to be confused with curvature as this is generally understoodin the Cartan geometry literature, what we have called {\df model curvature}, whose Liealgebroid analogue was defined in \ref{normalization}. For further context, see\cite{Blaom_16b}.  The prototype of an infinitesimal Cartan connection is the trivial flatconnection $\nabla $ on an action algebroid ${\mathfrak g} \times M$, for someinfinitesimal action of a Lie algebra $\mathfrak g$ on $M$. If we identify${\mathfrak g} $ with the constant ($\nabla$-parallel) sections of${\mathfrak g} \times M$, then its bracket is$\torsion\bar\nabla(\,\cdot\,,\,\cdot\,)$. Indeed, let$\tau \in \Gamma(\wedge^2 A^* \otimes A)$ denote the trivial extension of the bracket on${\mathfrak g} $ to a fibre-wise bracket on $A = {\mathfrak g} \times M$, and recall thatthe Lie algebroid bracket on ${\mathfrak g} \times M$ is given by\begin{equation}  [X,Y]=\nabla_{\#X}Y-\nabla_{\#Y}X +\tau(X,Y). \label{one}\end{equation}Then we can write\begin{equation}    [X,Y]=\bar\nabla_XY-\bar\nabla_YX - \tau(X,Y),\label{two}\end{equation}from which $\tau = \torsion \bar\nabla $ immediately follows.\begin{theorem}[\cite{Blaom_06}]  If $M$ is simply connected, then every Lie algebroid $A$ equipped with a flat Cartan  connection $\nabla$ is naturally isomorphic to an action algebroid  ${\mathfrak g} \times M$ (possibly intransitive) where ${\mathfrak g}\subset \Gamma(TM) $  is the Lie algebra of $\nabla $-parallel sections, equipped with bracket  $\torsion \bar\nabla$.\end{theorem}\noindent More informally, we have:\begin{aside}  A Lie algebroid equipped with a Cartan connection is an infinitesimal symmetry deformed  by curvature; the torsion of the connection is the deformed symmetry Lie bracket.\end{aside}If $\curvature \nabla =0$ but $M $ is not simply connected, then $A$ is a Lie algebraaction with monodromy twist \cite{Blaom_16b}. Lie groupoids with Cartan connections,whose infinitesimalizations are flat, define pseudogroups of symmetries of $M$, and underan additional completeness assumption, twisted Lie group actions \cite{Blaom_16,  Blaom_16b}. In the transitive case, one can recover a locally homogeneous structure,assuming ``geometric closure'', if not global homogeneity up to cover \cite{Blaom_13}.\begin{example}[Riemannian geometry]  Associated with every Riemannian metric is a canonical Cartan connection $\nabla $ on  the isotropy subalgebroid\footnote{This isotropy is the abstract homogeneous PDE you    solve to find Killing fields (whether or not they exist).} $A \subset J^1 (TM)$ of the  metric \cite{Blaom_06,Blaom_12}, which is an infinitesimalization of a global connection  $D$ on the isotropy subgroupoid ${\mathcal G} \subset J^1(M \times M)$ of the metric  \cite{Blaom_16b}. It's significance is that the anchor induces a map  $\Gamma(A) \rightarrow \Gamma(TM)$ establishing a one-to-one correspondence between  $\nabla $-parallel sections and infinitesimal isometries (Killing fields).  Under a natural identification of $A$ with $ TM \oplus \so(TM)$, the Lie bracket is  given by\begin{equation*}  \left[  \begin{pmatrix}    V_1\\ C_1  \end{pmatrix},  \begin{pmatrix}    V_2\\ C_2  \end{pmatrix}\right] =\begin{pmatrix}  [V_1,V_2]\\  [C_1, C_2] + \nabla^{TM}_{V_1} C_2 - \nabla^{TM}_{V_2}  C_1 + \curvature \nabla^{TM}(V_1,V_2)\end{pmatrix}\enspace,\end{equation*}where $\nabla^{TM}$ is the Levi-Cevita connection. The Cartan connection is given by\begin{equation*}  \nabla_U  \begin{pmatrix}    V \\ C  \end{pmatrix}  =  \begin{pmatrix}    \nabla^M_UV + C U \\    \nabla^M_UC + \curvature \nabla^M (U,V)  \end{pmatrix}\,,\end{equation*}which has torsion\begin{equation}  \torsion \bar\nabla \left(    \begin{pmatrix}        V_1 \\ C_1      \end{pmatrix},      \begin{pmatrix}        V_2 \\ C_2      \end{pmatrix}  \right)=  \begin{pmatrix}    C_1 V_2 -C_2 V_1 \\    [C_1,C_2] - \curvature \nabla^{TM} (V_1,V_2)  \end{pmatrix}\,.  \label{torsion}\end{equation}The curvature of $\nabla $ is readily computed, vanishing precisely when $M$ has constantsectional curvature $s$. From the formula for the torsion, we conclude that${\mathfrak g} $ in the preceding theorem is the symmetry Lie algebra of the Riemanniansphere, plane or hyperbolic space, according to whether $s$ is positive, zero, ornegative.\end{example}\subsection{Cartan connections compatible with a filtration}\label{compat}An infinitesimal Cartan connection $\nabla$ on a filtered Lie algebroid $A$ is {\df  compatible} with the filtration if the filtration is invariant under the associatedrepresentation $\bar\nabla $. Similarly, a Cartan connection $D$ on a filtered Liegroupoid ${\mathcal G} $ with Lie algebroid $A$ is {\df compatible} with the filtrationprovided the filtration of $A$ is invariant under the associated representation $\bar D$,which in turn implies compatibility of the corresponding infinitesimal Cartan connection$\bar\nabla$.In addition to the examples given in \ref{filtered}, the following result justifies ourfocus on {\em firmly} filtered Lie algebroids.\begin{proposition}  If a transitive filtered Lie groupoid (Lie algebroid) admits a compatible  (infinitesimal) Cartan connection, then it is firmly filtered.\end{proposition}\begin{proof}  Suppose $\nabla $ is a Cartan connection on a transitive filtered Lie algebroid  $A$. Then $\bar\nabla $ extends the canonical representation $\adjoint$ of $A$ on its  anchor kernel, which contains $A^0$. So $\adjoint_X Y := [X, Y] = \bar\nabla_XY$ for  $X \in A$ and $Y \in A^0$. If $\nabla $ is compatible with the filtration, then $A^0$  and the filtration of $A^0$ must therefore be $\adjoint$-invariant. The groupoid case  follows immediately from the infinitesimal one.\end{proof}\subsection{Filtered Klein geometries}\label{homogeneous}We now prove some claims made in \ref{eg2} regarding the homogeneous space $M=G/P$described there. Let ${\mathcal G} = G \times M$ be the action groupoid, equipped with itscanonical Cartan connection $D$, so that ${\mathcal G}$ acts on$A = {\mathfrak g} \times M$ according to \eqrefs{cartan}{adjoint}. Since the filtrationof ${\mathfrak g} $ is $P$-invariant, the filtration of $A$ is $\bar D$-invariant.Since $A$ is transitive and $A^0$ is the anchor kernel, we have, as vector bundles,\begin{equation*}  \graded(A) \cong \graded(TM)\oplus \graded(A^0).\end{equation*}As in \ref{curvature}, we denote the fibre-wise Lie bracket on $A={\mathfrak g} \times M$coming from ${\mathfrak g} $ by $\tau $, which coincides with the torsion of theinfinitesimalization $\nabla $ of $D$, namely the canonical flat Koszul connection on thetrivial bundle ${\mathfrak g} \times M$. We note in passing that $\tau$ is evidently$\bar D$-invariant, and so $\bar\nabla$-invariant. When we want to emphasize we are usingthe bracket $T=\graded_0(\tau)$ when viewing $\graded(A)$, $\graded(A^0)$ or $\graded(TM)$as Lie algebra bundles, we shall denote them by $\graded^T(A)$, $\graded^T(A^0)$ and$\graded^T(TM)$.\begin{proposition}  The vector bundle filtration of $A = {\mathfrak g} \times M$ defined in \ref{eg2} is a  firm Lie algebroid filtration, and ${\mathcal G} = G \times M$ is a firmly filtered Lie  groupoid. Furthermore, equipping $\graded(A)$ with the corresponding Lie algebra bundle  structure (Proposition \ref{filtered}), we have  \begin{equation*}    \label{decomposition}    \graded(A) = -\graded^T(TM)\oplus \graded^T(A^0)\quad    \text{(Lie algebra bundle direct sum)}  \end{equation*}  where -$\graded^T(TM)$ is $\graded^T(TM)$ equipped with  negative bracket, and $T=\graded_0(\tau)$.\end{proposition}\begin{proof}  Axiom F2 holds by construction. Let $X\in \Gamma(A^j)$ and $Y\in \Gamma(A^k)$ be  given. Then we claim:  \begin{align}  \bar\nabla_X Y &= [X, Y] \quad\text{if $k \ge 0$}\label{gamma}\\  \bar\nabla_X Y &= \tau(X, Y) \quad\text{if $j \ge 0$}\label{delta}\\  [X, Y] &= \tau(X, Y) \quad\text{if $j \ge 0$ and $k \ge 0$} \label{alpha}\\  [X, Y] &\equiv -\tau(X, Y) \mod A^{j + k + 1} \quad\text{if $j < 0$ and $k < 0$}\label{beta}\\  [X, Y] &\in \Gamma(A^{j + k + 1}) \quad\text{if $j < 0$ and $k \ge 0$}.\label{blue}  \end{align}  Equation \eqref{gamma} follows from \eqrefs{hsd}{zero} and F2. If $j\ge 0$, then  Equation \eqrefs{curvature}{one} reads $[X, Y] = - \nabla_{\#Y}X +\tau(X, Y)$, because  $\#A^j=0$ in that case. This and \eqrefs{hsd}{zero} delivers \eqref{delta}.  Claim  \eqref{alpha} follows immediately from the preceding two.  If $j < 0$, then $j + k + 1 \le k$, and so  $A^k \subset A^{j + k+ 1}$. Similarly, if $k < 0$, then  $A^j \subset A^{j + k + 1}$. Since the filtration is  $\bar\nabla$-invariant, \eqref{beta} now follows from \eqrefs{curvature}{two}.  As already mentioned, if $j <0 $, then $A^k \subset A^{j+k+1}$. On  the other hand, if additionally $k \ge 0$, then  $[X, Y] = \bar\nabla_XY$, by \eqref{gamma}. So \eqref{blue} is a  consequence of the $\bar\nabla $-invariance of $A^k$.  To establish axiom F1 for filtered Lie algebroids, we need to show  $[X, Y] \in \Gamma(A^{j+k})$. Equations \eqref{alpha} takes care of the case $j\ge 0$  and $k \ge 0$. Equation \eqref{beta} takes care of the case $j < 0$ and $k < 0$ because  $A^{j+k+1} \subset A^{j+k}$ is always true. The remaining case is $j < 0$ and $k \ge 0$  (and the case $j \ge 0$ and $k < 0$, which will follow by symmetry). But as  $A^{j + k + 1} \subset A^{j + k}$, this already follows from \eqref{blue}.  The last statement in of the theorem follows easily from \eqref{alpha}, \eqref{beta},  and \eqref{blue}.  Since the filtration of $A$ is $\bar D$-invariant, $D$ is compatible with the  filtration, and so firmness follows from Proposition \ref{compat}.\end{proof}\section{Model data}\label{modeldata}Model data, defined in \ref{preview}, generalizes the trivial Lie algebra bundlestructure of an action algebroid ${\mathfrak g} \times M$.\subsection{Strong compatibility}\label{compat2}Recall that model data $\tau$ is {\df compatible} with a filtration of $A$ if this is alsoa filtration with respect to $\tau$. And $\tau $ is compatible with an infinitesimalCartan connection $\nabla $ (Cartan connection $D$) if $\tau $ is $\bar \nabla $-invariant(respectively $\bar D$-invariant).For the moment suppose that $A$ is any Lie algebroid, which may or may not be filtered. Inthe presence of both model data $\tau$, and an infinitesimal Cartan connection $\nabla$, aLie algebroid $A$ acts on itself in {\em two} distinct ways: first, we have therepresentation $\bar\nabla$ of $A$ on itself as a Lie algebroid; second we have therepresentation $X \mapsto \adjoint^\tau_X = \tau(X,\,\cdot\,)$ of $A$ on itself as a Liealgebra bundle (a special kind Lie algebroid). If $\tau $ is compatible with $\nabla $(is $\bar\nabla$-invariant) and every element of the anchor kernel of $A$ acts the sameway in both cases, then we say $\tau $ is {\em strongly compatible} with $\nabla$.  Insymbols, the additional requirement is:\begin{equation}  \bar\nabla_ZX = \tau(Z, X)\enspace  \text{whenever $\#Z=0$, $X \in \Gamma(A).$}\mathlab{strong}\end{equation}Here $\# \colon A \rightarrow TM $ denotes the anchor.  We say $\tau $ is {\em strongly  compatible} with a Cartan connection $D$ if it is compatible with $D$ ($\tau$ is$\bar D$-invariant) and strongly compatible with the infinitesimalization $\nabla $ of$D$. Theorem \ref{uniquely_determined} stated later shows that compatibility alreadyimplies strong compatibility, for model data relevant to parabolic geometry.\begin{example}[filtered Klein geometries]  If $A$, ${\mathfrak g}$, and $\tau$ have the meanings given for filtered Klein  geometries in \ref{homogeneous}, then $\tau $ is strongly compatible with the canonical  flat connection $\nabla $ on $A = {\mathfrak g} \times M$, by  \eqrefs{homogeneous}{delta}.\end{example}\subsection{Constructing Cartan connections compatible with model data}\label{constructing}In Section \ref{filtered_cartan} we shall need the following important result, reducing,in the {\em transitive} case, the problem of constructing Cartan connections compatiblewith given model data, to the problem of constructing representations of a suitablesubalgebroid/subgroupoid.\begin{proposition}  {\em \bfseries Infinitesimal case.} Let $A$ be a transitive Lie algebroid that can be written as  vector bundle sum $A = B \oplus A'$, where $B \subset A$ is a subalgebroid and  $A' \subset A$ an ideal lying in the kernel of the anchor  $\# \colon A \rightarrow TM$. Let $\tau $ be model data on $A$ and suppose  there exists a representation $\rho^B$ of $B$ on $A$, with respect to which $\tau $ is  invariant, and such that $\rho^B$ extends the canonical representation of $B \subset A $  on the anchor kernel of A:  \begin{equation}    \rho^B_X Z = [X, Z]\enspace \text{whenever $\#Z=0$, $Z \in \Gamma(A)$,      $X \in \Gamma(B)$}.\label{ext1}  \end{equation}  Then there exists a unique Cartan connection $\nabla $ on $A$, necessarily compatible  with $\tau $, such that  \begin{equation}    \bar\nabla_{X + Y}W = \rho^B_XW + \tau(Y, W);\qquad X    \in \Gamma(B),\,Y \in \Gamma(A'),\,W \in \Gamma(A).\label{ext2}  \end{equation}  If $\tau $ is compatible with a filtration of $A$, and $\rho^B$ preserves the  filtration, then $\nabla $ is also compatible with the filtration.  If, additionally,  \begin{equation}    \rho^B_{Z}W = \tau(Z, W)\enspace    \text{whenever $\#Z=0$, $Z \in \Gamma(B)$, $W \in \Gamma(A)$,}\label{ext3}  \end{equation}  then $\tau $ is strongly compatible with $\nabla $.  {\em \bfseries Global case.} Suppose ${\mathcal G} $ is a Lie groupoid, with Lie algebroid $A$,  that can be written ${\mathcal G} = {\mathcal G}' {\mathcal H} $ with  ${\mathcal G}'\cap {\mathcal H} = M$ (groupoid of identity elements), where  ${\mathcal G}' \subset {\mathcal G} $ is a totally intransitive, normal, Lie subgroupoid  with simply-connected fibres. Let $\tau $ be model data on $A$ and suppose there is a  representation $\rho^{\mathcal H}$ of ${\mathcal H} $ on $A$, with respect to which  $\tau $ is invariant, and such that $\rho^{\mathcal H}$ extends the canonical  representation of ${\mathcal H} \subset {\mathcal G} $ on the anchor kernel of A:  \begin{equation}    \label{pony4}    \rho^{\mathcal H}_hZ = \Adjoint_h Z  \enspace      \text{whenever $\#Z=0$, $h \in {\mathcal H}$}.  \end{equation}  Then, there exists a unique Cartan connection $D $ on ${\mathcal G}$, necessarily  compatible with $\tau $, such that  \begin{equation}    \bar D_{kh}W = \Adjoint^\tau_k \rho^{\mathcal H}_hW,\label{ext17}  \end{equation}  for each arrow $h \in H$ beginning at some $m \in M$, $k \in {\mathcal G}^1|_{hm}$ and  $W \in A|_m$. Here $\Adjoint^\tau$ is unique representation of ${\mathcal G}'$ on $A$  integrating the infinitesimal representation $\adjoint^\tau$ of the Lie algebroid $A'$  of ${\mathcal G} '$ on $A$ defined by $\adjoint^\tau_YW=\tau(Y, W)$.  If $\tau $ is  compatible with a filtration of $A$, and $\rho^B$ preserves the filtration, then $D$ is  also compatible with the filtration.\end{proposition}\noindent%Note that $\Adjoint^\tau$ is well-defined on account of the hypothesis ofsimple-connectivity.\begin{proof}{\em \bfseries Infinitesimal case.} Under the hypotheses of the theorem, define an  $A$-connection $\rho $ on $A$ using the right-hand side of \eqref{ext2}:  \begin{equation*}   \rho_{X + Y}W = \rho^B_XW + \tau(Y, W);\qquad    X \in \Gamma(B),\,Y \in \Gamma(A'),\,W \in \Gamma(A).  \end{equation*}  By Proposition \ref{hsd}, to show there exists a Cartan connection $\nabla $ satisfying  \eqref{ext2}, it suffices to show that $\rho $ is actually a representation, and,  secondly, that $\rho $ extends the canonical representation of $A$ on sections of its  anchor kernel:  \begin{equation}    \label{gobbly}    \rho_WZ = [W, Z]\enspace\text{whenever $\#Z=0$, $W \in \Gamma(A)$.}  \end{equation}  If $W \in \Gamma(B)$, then \eqref{gobbly} holds, by hypothesis \eqref{ext1}. If  $W \in \Gamma(A')$ and $\#Z=0$, then $\rho_WZ=\tau(W,Z)=[W, Z]$, because both $W$ and  $Z$ lie in the anchor kernel and $\tau $ is model data. Since $A = B \oplus A'$, this  proves \eqref{gobbly}.  Since the restriction of $X \mapsto \rho_X $ to $B$ is $\rho^B$, a representation, and  the restriction of $Y \mapsto \rho_Y $ to $A'$ is $Y \mapsto \tau(Y, \,\cdot\,)$, also a  representation, to show $\rho $ is itself a representation, it suffices to prove that  \begin{equation*}    \rho_X \rho_Y W - \rho_Y \rho_X W - \rho_{[X,Y]}W = 0, \qquad W \in \Gamma(A),  \end{equation*}  whenever $X \in \Gamma(B)$ and $Y \in \Gamma(A')$. But in that case,  $[X, Y] \in \Gamma(A')$, because $A' \subset A$ is an ideal, and we compute  \begin{align*}    \rho_X \rho_Y W - \rho_Y \rho_X W - \rho_{[X,Y]}W       &= \rho^B_X(\tau(Y, W)) - \tau(Y, \rho^B_XW) - \tau([X,Y], W)\\                     &= \rho^B_X(\tau(Y, W)) - \tau(Y, \rho^B_XW) - \tau(\rho^B_XY, W),                       \quad\text{by \eqref{ext1}}  \\                     &= 0,                       \quad\text{by the $\rho^B$-invariance of $\tau $.}  \end{align*}  Proof of the remaining claims in infinitesimal case is straightforward and omitted.  {\em \bfseries Global case.} Define a map $(g, W) \mapsto \rho_gW \in A$, from the  submanifold of all $(g, W) \in {\mathcal G} \times A$ such that $g$ is an arrow  beginning at the base point of $W$, using the right-hand side of \eqref{ext17}:  \begin{equation*}    \rho_{kh}W=\Adjoint^\tau_k \rho^{\mathcal H}_hW;\qquad k \in {\mathcal G}',\,h \in {\mathcal H}.  \end{equation*}  To show that there exists a unique Cartan connection $D$ on $A$ satisfying  \eqref{ext17}, it suffices to show that $\rho$ is a representation, and that $\rho $  extends the canonical representation of ${\mathcal G} $ on the anchor kernel of $A$:  \begin{equation}    \rho_gZ = \Adjoint_gZ\enspace\text{whenever $\#Z=0$}\label{ext18}  \end{equation}  and $g \in {\mathcal G} $ is an arrow beginning at the base point of $Z$.  By the hypothesis \eqref{pony4}, \eqref{ext18} already holds for $g \in {\mathcal    H}$. Since ${\mathcal G} ={\mathcal G}' {\mathcal H} $, to prove \eqref{ext18} in  general, it suffices to prove  \begin{equation}    \Adjoint^\tau_k Z= \Adjoint_kZ\enspace\text{whenever $\#Z=0$, $k \in {\mathcal G}'$} .  \end{equation}  In fact, since ${\mathcal G}'$ has connected fibres, is suffices to prove the  infinitesimalization,  \begin{equation*}          \adjoint^\tau_YZ = [Y, Z]\enspace\text{whenever $\#Z=0$, $Y \in A'$.}  \end{equation*}  But this true because both $Y$ and $Z$ here lie in the anchor kernel,  $\adjoint^\tau_YZ:=\tau(Y,Z)$, and $\tau $ is model data.  Since $\rho^{\mathcal H} $ and $\rho^{{\mathcal G}^1}$ are already representations, to  show $\rho $ itself is a representation, it suffices to show, for any arrow  $h \in {\mathcal H} $ beginning at some $m \in M$, that  \begin{equation*}    \rho^{\mathcal H}_h \rho^{{\mathcal G}^1}_k \rho^{\mathcal H}_{h^{-1}}W = \rho^{{\mathcal G}^1}_{hkh^{-1}}W,  \end{equation*}  where $k \in {\mathcal G}^1|_m$ and $W \in A|_{hm}$ are arbitrary. In fact, since  ${\mathcal G}^1$ has connected fibres, it is enough to show the two Lie group  homomorphisms ${\mathcal G}^1|_m \rightarrow \automorphism(A|_m)$ below have the same  derivative:  \begin{align*}    k &\mapsto  \rho^{\mathcal H}_h \rho^{{\mathcal G}^1}_k \rho^{\mathcal H}_{h^{-1}}\\    k &\mapsto  \rho^{{\mathcal G}^1}_{hkh^{-1}}.  \end{align*}  The readily computed derivatives are:  \begin{align*}    X &\mapsto \rho^{\mathcal H}_h \adjoint^\tau_X \rho^{\mathcal H}_{h^{-1}}(\,\cdot\,)        = \rho^{\mathcal H}_h \tau(X, \rho^{\mathcal H}_{h^{-1}}(\,\cdot\,))\\    \text{and}\quad X &\mapsto \adjoint^\tau_{\Adjoint_hX}(\,\cdot\,)=\tau(\Adjoint_hX, \,\cdot\,) =                        \tau(\rho^{\mathcal H}_h X, \,\cdot\,),\quad\text{by \eqref{pony4}}.  \end{align*}  Equality of these derivatives follows from the $\rho^{\mathcal H}$-invariance of $\tau $.  Proof of the remaining claims of the proposition is elementary and omitted.\end{proof}% Some of our notions of compatibility are summarized in Table 1.% \begin{table}[]% \centering%   \begin{tabular}{@{}|c|c|c|c|@{}}%   \toprule%   compatibility of $\downarrow$ with $\rightarrow$ & $\tau$   & $\nabla$                        &  $\epsilon$\\ \midrule%   filtration & $\tau(A^j, A^k) \subset A^{j+k}$ &  $A^j$ is $\bar\nabla$-invariant $\forall j$ & -   \\%   $\tau$     &                                 &  $\tau $ is $\bar\nabla $-invariant  & $\graded_0(e)$ grades $\graded_T(A)$ \\%   $\nabla $  &                                 &                                      &  $\epsilon$ is $\bar\nabla $-invariant \\  \bottomrule% \end{tabular}% \vspace{0.75\baselineskip}% \caption{Summary of compatibility conditions for structures on a filtered Lie algebroid%   $A$. Then, according toTheorem \ref{weyl}, we have an associated grading $A^0=\oplus_{i\ge 0}{\mathfrak a}_i$that is invariant under the action of the isotropy subalgebroid $B \subset A$ of$\epsilon$. If $\epsilon$ is compatible with model data $\tau$, that is itself compatiblewith the filtration of $A$, then this grading extends to a grading$A = \oplus_i {\mathfrak a}_i $, where $X \in {\mathfrak a}_j$ if and only if$\tau(\epsilon, X) = jX$. This defines a splitting isomorphism$\Phi_\epsilon \colon \graded^T(A) \rightarrow A$.\begin{proposition} {\em \bfseries Infinitesimal case.} In the scenario above, let $\nabla $ be any  infinitesimal Cartan connection compatible with $\tau $. Then:  \begin{conditions}  \item The grading $A = \oplus_i {\mathfrak a}_i$ is invariant under the representation    $\bar\nabla $ of $B$ on $A$.    \label{u4}  \item For any $X \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_k)$, $k\ge 0$, and    $Y \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_j)$, $j \in {\mathbb Z} $, one has    $\bar\nabla_XY \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_j \oplus {\mathfrak a}_{j+k})$.\label{u3}%  \item $\nabla $ is compatible with the filtration of $A$. \label{u7}  \end{conditions}  {\em\bfseries Global case.} If ${\mathcal G} $ is a weakly firm, filtered Lie groupoid with Lie  algebroid $A$, $\epsilon$ is a Weyl gauge for ${\mathcal G} $ compatible with the model data  $\tau $, and $D$ is a Cartan connection on ${\mathcal G} $ compatible with $\tau $,  then:  \begin{conditions}  \item The grading $A=\oplus_i {\mathfrak a}_i$ associated with $\epsilon$ is invariant under    the representation $\bar D $ of the isotropy subgroupoid    ${\mathcal H}\subset {\mathcal G} $ of $\epsilon$.\label{uu4}%  \item $D$ is compatible with the filtration of $A$.\label{uu7}  \end{conditions}\end{proposition}The reader is warned that $B$ does not generally align itself with the complement$\oplus_{i\le 0}{\mathfrak a}_i$ of $A^1=\oplus_{i\ge 1}{\mathfrak a}_i$ in $A$ defined bythe grading.  We may only conclude that $B$ is the graph of some linear function$\oplus_{j\le 0} {\mathfrak a}_i \rightarrow A^1$ that vanishes on ${\mathfrak a}_0$,which is necessarily contained in $B$.\begin{corollary}  If a (weakly firm) filtered Lie algebroid (resp., Lie groupoid) admits a Weyl gauge  compatible with some model data $\tau $, then any Cartan connection compatible with  $\tau $ is compatible with the filtration.\end{corollary}\begin{proof}[Proof of corollary]  If $\nabla $ is the Cartan connection in the Lie algebroid case, then  $\bar\nabla$-invariance of the filtration of $A$ follows from \eqref{u4} and \eqref{u3},  and the semi-direct product structure of $A$ (Theorem \eqrefs{weyl}{pq2}).  For the Lie groupoid case and a global Cartan connection $D$, we know the filtration is  $\bar\nabla $-invariant, where $\nabla $ is the infinitesimalization of $D$, from the  Lie algebroid case just proven. In particular, continuing in the notation of the  proposition, the restriction of $\bar\nabla $ to a representation of $A^1$ on $A$ is  filtration-preserving. Therefore, the restriction of $\bar D$ to a representation of  ${\mathcal G}^1$ is filtration-preserving, because ${\mathcal G}^1$ is source-connected.  Full ${\bar D}$-invariance of the filtration follows from this fact, \eqref{uu4}, and  the semi-direct product structure of $\mathcal G$ (Theorem \eqrefs{weyl2}{pr2}).\end{proof}%\begin{proof}[Proof of proposition]Our proofs of \eqref{u4} and \eqref{u3} are variations on an argument appearing in theproof of Theorem \ref{weyl}.  Indeed, suppose that $Y \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_j)$ forsome $j$ and let $X \in \Gamma(B)$ be arbitrary. Then, applying $\bar\nabla_X$ to bothsides of the identity $\tau(\epsilon, Y) = jY$, and appealing to the $\bar\nabla$-invariance of$\tau$, we have\begin{equation*}    \tau(\bar\nabla_{X}\epsilon, Y) + \tau(\epsilon, \bar\nabla_XY) = j \bar\nabla_XY.\end{equation*}But $\bar\nabla_{X} \epsilon = [X, \epsilon]$, because $\epsilon$ is in the anchor kernel, while $[X, \epsilon] = 0 $,because $X \in \Gamma(B)$. We conclude that $\bar\nabla_XY \in \Gamma({\mathfrak  a}_j)$. This proves \eqref{u4}.Turning to \eqref{u3}, write $\bar\nabla_XY=\sum_iW_i$ for  some $W_i \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_i)$, which implies\begin{equation*}    \tau(\epsilon, \bar\nabla_XY) = \sum_iiW_i.\end{equation*}On the other hand, $\tau(\epsilon, Y) = jY$, which, after applying $\bar\nabla_X$ to bothsides, and appealing to the $\bar\nabla$-invariance of $\tau $, becomes\begin{equation*}  \tau(\bar\nabla_X \epsilon, Y) + \tau(\epsilon, \bar\nabla_XY) = j\sum_iW_i.\end{equation*}As $X$ and $\epsilon$ are sections of $A^0$, we have$\bar\nabla_X E = [X, \epsilon] = \tau(X, \epsilon) = -kX$. Projecting onto eachcomponent ${\mathfrak a}_i$ of the grading $A = \oplus_i {\mathfrak a}_i$, we obtain$W_i = 0$ for all $i$, with the possible exceptions $i=j, j+k$, which shows$\bar\nabla_XY \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_j \oplus {\mathfrak a}_{j+k})$.The proof of \eqref{uu4} is similar to that of \eqref{u4} above, following from the$\bar D$-invariance of $\tau $, the definition of the spaces ${\mathfrak a}_j$, and thefact that ${\mathcal H} $ fixes $\epsilon$.\end{proof}% \begin{corollary}%   Every gauge transformation $\varphi \colon A \rightarrow A$ induces the identity in%   homology $H_n(A^1, A) \rightarrow H_n(A^1, A)$.% \end{corollary}\section{Filtered Cartan geometries}\label{filtered_cartan}Suppose ${\mathcal G} $ is a firmly filtered Lie groupoid, with Lie algebroid $A$,supporting a Cartan connection $D$ compatible with the filtration, and model data $\tau $compatible with the filtration. Then we call $({\mathcal G}, D, \tau)$ a {\df filtered  Cartan geometry} if:\begin{conditions}   \item $\tau $ is strongly compatible with $D$.   \item ${\mathcal G}$ admits a Weyl gauge compatible with $\tau $.\label{fc2}\end{conditions}Because we assume ${\mathcal G} $ is firmly filtered, \eqref{fc2} just means${\mathcal G}$ admits an $\Adjoint$-invariant, fibre-wise grading section of$\graded^T(A)$, where $T:=\graded_0(\tau)$ (Corollary \ref{weyl2}). There is norequirement here that $\tau $ be semisimple, and semisimplicity plays no role in thecurrent section.If we only have a firmly filtered Lie algebroid $A$, and an infinitesimal Cartanconnection $\nabla$, compatible with the filtration and strongly compatible with $\tau $,and if $A$ admits a $\tau$-compatible Weyl gauge, then $(A, \nabla, \tau)$ is an {\df  infinitesimal filtered Cartan geometry}.In this section, we provide a general, explicit, description of {\em transitive}, filteredCartan geometries, by exploiting theorems on semi-direct product structure established inSection \ref{semi-direct}. In this description, we specify the additional dataneeded to recover a transitive Cartan geometry from its Levi reduction. While we aremainly interested in the special parabolic case introduced in Section\ref{parabolic_summary}, the extra abstraction introduced here emphasizes Weyl gauges asthe essential actor in the resulting characterization.\subsection{Abstract Levi structures: Infinitesimal case}\label{inflevi}The reduction procedure described for parabolic geometries in \ref{reduction_} generalizesto transitive filtered Cartan geometries. We begin with the infinitesimal case.Let $(A, \nabla, \tau)$ be an transitive infinitesimal filtered Cartan geometry. Since $A$is firmly filtered, the canonical representation $\adjoint$ of $A$ on $A^0$, in which$A^1$ is acting trivially, descends to a representation $\adjoint$ of $B:=A/A^1$ on$\graded(A^0)$. This canonical representation is extended by a representation of $B$ on$\graded(A)$, induced by the representation $\bar\nabla $ of $A$ on itself, and we denotethis by $R^\nabla$. If we write ${\mathfrak a}_j:=\graded(A)_j$ for $j>0$, then using thetransitivity we can write\begin{conditions}  \item[{\bfseries C0.}]  \quad $\graded(A) \cong \graded(TM)\oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$.\end{conditions}In addition to the grading-preserving representation $R^\nabla$ on the above, we have the Liealgebra bundle bracket $T := \graded_0(\tau)$ which, on account of strong compatibilitybetween $\tau$ and $\bar\nabla $, shows that\begin{equation}  \label{form_}  T(C, V) = R^\nabla_C V,\quad  T(C, Z) = R^\nabla_C Z,\quad  T(C, C') = [C,C'],\end{equation}whenever $C,C'\in\Gamma(B^0)$, $V\in \Gamma(\graded(TM))$,$Z \in \Gamma(\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j)$. The last equation holds because $\tau$extends the canonical bracket on $A^0$. Note $R^\nabla Z$ does not actually depend on$\nabla $, because it is determined by the canonical representation of $A$ on $A^1$.Motivated by the above, an {\df abstract infinitesimal Levi structure} will be a 4-tuple$(B, \oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j, R, T)$, where:\begin{conditions}  \item $B$ is a transitive, filtered Lie algebroid over $M$ with $B \le 0$ and with $B^0$  as its anchor kernel.  \item $\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$ is a graded vector bundle over $M$.  \item $R$ is a a grading-preserving representation of $B$ on C0 that extends    the canonical action of $B$ on $B^0$  \item\label{chh0} $T$ is an $R$-invariant Lie algebra bundle structure on C0    compatible with the obvious grading and satisfying    \begin{equation*}      T(C, V) = R_C V,\quad      T(C, Z) = R_C Z,\quad      T(C, C') = [C,C'],    \end{equation*}    whenever $C,C'\in\Gamma(B^0)$, $V\in \Gamma(\graded(TM))$,    $Z \in \Gamma(\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j)$.  \item As a graded Lie algebra bundle, C0 admits an $R$-invariant grading    section, necessarily of the form $(0, E, 0)$, for some $E \in \Gamma(B^0)$.\end{conditions}\noindent Note that it is not required that $T$ restrict to the canonical Lie algebrabundle structure on $\graded(TM)$. If this restriction is the {\em negative} of thecanonical one, then the geometry is {\df regular}.According to the immediately preceding observations, associated with every transitiveinfinitesimal filtered Cartan geometry is an infinitesimal Levi structure, called its {\df  Levi reduction}, regular if the filtered Cartan geometry is regular.\subsection{Abstract Levi structures: Global case}\label{abstract_levi}%\mbox{}\newline\noindent%An {\df abstract Levi structure} is a 4-tuple$({\mathcal H}, \oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j, R, T)$ where:\begin{conditions}  \item ${\mathcal H} $ is a transitive, filtered Lie groupoid over $M$ with Lie algebroid    $B$, whose anchor kernel is $B^0$, and with $B \le 0$.  \item  $\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$ is a vector bundle over $M$.  \item $R$ is a grading-preserving representation of ${\mathcal H}$ on    C0 that extends the canonical action of ${\mathcal H}$ on $B^0$.   \item\label{chh00} $T$ an $R$-invariant Lie algebra bundle structure on    C0 compatible with the grading, and such that    \begin{equation*}      T(C, V) = dR_C V,\quad      T(C, Z) = dR_C Z,\quad      T(C, C') = [C,C'],    \end{equation*}    for all $C,C'\in\Gamma(B^0)$, $V\in \Gamma(\graded(TM))$,    $Z \in \Gamma(\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j)$.  \item As a graded Lie algebra bundle, C0 admits an $R$-invariant      grading section, necessarily of the form $(0, E, 0)$, for some $E \in \Gamma(B^0)$.\end{conditions}Here $dR$ is the infinitesimalization of the representation $R$. The geometry is {\df  regular} if its infinitesimalization $(B,\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j, dR, T)$ isregular.  %In analogy with our observations in \ref{inflevi}, we can associate with each transitivefiltered Cartan geometry $({\mathcal G}, D, \tau)$ an abstract Levi structure$({\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1, \graded(A^1), R^D, \graded_0(\tau))$ that we call its {\df  Levi reduction}. It is regular if $({\mathcal G}, D, \tau)$ is. Here $R^D$ is therepresentation of ${\mathcal G}/{\mathcal G}^1$ induced by the representation $\bar D$ of${\mathcal G} $ on the Lie algebroid $A$ of ${\mathcal G} $.\subsection{Consequences of the semi-direct product structure}\label{consequences}%Beginning our analysis with the infinitesimal case, suppose $(A, \nabla, \tau)$ is atransitive infinitesimal filtered Cartan geometry such that $A^0$ is the anchor kernel, and let$\epsilon $ be a $\tau$-compatible gauge. According to Theorem \ref{weyl}, we may suppose,without loss of generality, that $A$ is the semi-direct product of a transitive, filteredLie algebroid $B$, with $|B| \le 0$ and anchor $B_0$, with a filtered Lie algebra bundle$\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$, on which $B $ is acting, an action that preserves thegrading. Moreover, if we denote the action of $B$ on $\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$ by $R$,so that $A = B \oplus_R(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$, then we know there exists a section$E$ of $B^0$ such that:\begin{gather*}  [E, X]=0\quad \text{for all $X \in \Gamma(B)$}\\  R_EY = jY\quad\text{for all $Y \in \Gamma({\mathfrak a}_j)$, $j>0$}.\\  \epsilon = (E, 0) \in \Gamma(A).\end{gather*}The Weyl gauge determines a grading $A = \oplus_i {\mathfrak a}'_i$, where${\mathfrak a}'_i$ is the kernel of$X \mapsto \tau(E,X) - iX \colon A^i \rightarrow A^{i+1}$.In general, ${\mathfrak a}'_k \subset B \oplus 0$ does not hold for $k <0$. However,noting that the filtration of $A=B\oplus_R (\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ is defined by$A^i = B^i \oplus_R(\oplus_{j\ge\max\{1,i\}}{\mathfrak a}_j)$, it is not hard to see,from the identities above, that ${\mathfrak a}'_0=B^0 \oplus 0$ and${\mathfrak a}'_j=0\oplus{\mathfrak a}_j$ for $j> 0$.  From this we may deduce that thesplitting isomorphism $\Phi\colon \graded(A) \rightarrow A$ induced by the grading$A = \oplus_i {\mathfrak a}'_i$ has the form\begin{gather*}    \Phi \colon \graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j)    \rightarrow B\oplus_R(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)\\    (V, C, Z) \mapsto (C + \tilde \sigma (V), Z - \mathsf{P}(V)),\end{gather*}for some $\mathsf{P} \subset \Gamma(\graded(T^*\!M)\otimes(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j))$,and some vector bundle map $\tilde \sigma \colon \graded(TM) \rightarrow B$ satisfying\begin{equation*}  \tilde \sigma(\graded(TM)_k) \subset B^k\enspace\text{and}\enspace    \graded_k(\# \tilde \sigma (V)) = V \quad\text{for all $V \in \graded(TM)_k$, $k < 0$}.\end{equation*}Here $\# \colon B \rightarrow TM$ denotes the anchor. This is just linear algebra and anunpacking of the implicit identification${\mathfrak a}_k\cong\graded(A)_k \cong \graded(TM)_k$ for $k < 0$.The composite $\sigma=\#\circ\tilde\sigma $ satisfies\begin{equation*}  \sigma(\graded(TM)_k) \subset T^kM\enspace\text{and}\enspace    \graded_k(\sigma (V)) = V \quad\text{for all $V \in \graded(TM)_k$, $k < 0$}.\end{equation*}We call such $\sigma \colon \graded(TM) \rightarrow TM $, which is necessarily anisomorphism, a {\df splitting} of the filtered manifold $M$. The composite$s = \tilde \sigma \circ \sigma^{-1} \colon TM \rightarrow B$ is a right-inverse for theanchor $\# \colon B \rightarrow TM$, which we will call a {\df splitting of the anchor of $B$}. Wemay now rewrite our formula for $\Phi $ as\begin{conditions}\item[{\bfseries C1.}]  \quad $\Phi(V, C, Z) = (C + s(\sigma(V)), Z - \mathsf{P}(V))$.\end{conditions}By construction, the model data $\tau $ is the push-forward of $T:=\graded_0(\tau)$ under$\Phi $:\begin{conditions}\item[{\bfseries C2.}]  \quad $\tau(\Phi(\xi_1), \Phi(\xi_2)) = \Phi(T(\xi_1, \xi_2))$,\end{conditions}for $\xi_1,\,\xi_2 \in \graded(TM)\oplus B \oplus_R(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$, and$(0, E, 0)$ is a fibre-wise grading section of$\graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_j {\mathfrak a}_j)$, for the Lie bracket $T$.  Tocomplete our analysis, we derive a formula for the self-representation $\bar\nabla $ of$A$, which characterizes the connection $\nabla$, because $A$ is transitive (Proposition\ref{hsd}).  Since Proposition \ref{model_data_gradings} applies, the grading$A=\oplus_i {\mathfrak a}'_i$ (there denoted $\oplus_i {\mathfrak a}_i$) is$\bar\nabla_{(X,0)}$-invariant, for every section $X$ of $B$.  It follows that $\Phi $intertwines some grading-preserving representation $R$ of $B$ on$\graded(A)=\graded(TM)\oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ with the$\bar\nabla$-representation of $B \oplus 0$ on $A$:\begin{equation*}  \bar\nabla_{(X, 0)} \Big(\,\Phi(V,C,Z)\,\Big) = \Phi\Big(\,R_X(V, C, Z)\,\Big);\quad X \in B.\end{equation*}It's not hard to see that $R$ on the right is the same thing as $R^\nabla $, as defined in\ref{inflevi} above, under the identification $B \oplus 0 \cong A/A^1$. As a consequencewe can write this as\begin{equation}    \bar\nabla_{(X, 0)} \Big(\,\Phi(V,C,Z)\,\Big)=\Phi(R_XV, [X,C], R_X Z),\label{groof}\end{equation}where the ``$R$\,'' in $R_XZ$ has the meaning assigned to it in the beginning of thecurrent discussion, and with\begin{equation*}  T(C, V) = R_C V,\quad  T(C, Z) = R_C Z,\quad  T(C, C') = [C,C'],\end{equation*}for all $C,C'\in\Gamma(B^0)$, $V\in \Gamma(\graded(TM))$,$Z \in \Gamma(\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j)$.On the other hand, for any $Y \in \Gamma(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$, we have$\Phi(0,0,Y)=(0,Y)$, so strong compatibility also leads to\begin{align}  \bar\nabla_{(0, Y)} \Big(\,\Phi(V, C, Z)\,\Big)  &=\tau\Big(\,\Phi(0, 0, Y),\,\Phi(V, C, Z)\,\Big) =    \Phi\Big(\,T((0, 0, Y), (V, C, Z))\,\Big)\label{cab0}\\  &=\Phi\Big(\,(0,0,[Y,Z] - R_CY) + T(Y, V)\,\Big);    \quad Y \in \Gamma(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j).\notag\end{align}Combining this last with \eqref{groof}, we have, for any$(X, Y) \in \Gamma(B \oplus_R(\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j))$,\begin{conditions}\item[{\bfseries C3.}]  \quad $\bar\nabla_{(X,Y)}\Phi(V, C, Z)  =\Phi\Big(\,R_X(V, C, Z) + T((0, 0, Y), (V, C, Z))\,\Big)$,\end{conditions}whose right-hand side can also be written as$$\Phi\Big(\,(R_XV, [X,C], [Y,Z] +R_XZ - R_CY) + T(Y, V)\,\Big)$$.The above analysis leads to the characterizations of transitive filtered Cartan geometriesgiven next.\subsection{Algebraic prolongation: Infinitesimal case}\label{inflevi_}\mbox{}\newline\noindent%Let $(B, \oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j, R, T)$ be an abstract infinitesimal Levistructure. The structure $T$ makes $\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$ into a graded Lie algebrabundle and the {\df algebraic prolongation} of the abstract infinitesimal Levi structure isthe Lie algebroid semi-direct product $A = B \oplus_R(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$. Thisis a firmly filtered Lie algebroid with$A^i = B^i \oplus_R(\oplus_{j\ge\max\{1,i\}}{\mathfrak a}_j)$.\begin{theorem}[A characterization of transitive infinitesimal filtered Cartan geometries]  Let $(B, \oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j, R, T)$ be an abstract infinitesimal Levi structure,  $\mathsf{P}$ be an arbitrary section of  $\graded(T^*\!M)\otimes(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$, $s \colon TM \rightarrow B$ any  splitting of the anchor of $B$, and $\sigma \colon \graded(TM) \rightarrow TM $ some  splitting of the filtered manifold $M$. Then:  \begin{conditions}  \item The map    $\Phi \colon \graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_j {\mathfrak a}_j) \rightarrow B    \oplus_R(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ defined by {\normalfont C1} is    invertible. Specifically, if $\pi \colon B \rightarrow B^0$ is defined by    $\pi(X):=X - s(\#X)$, and if $(X, Y) = \Phi(V, C, Z)$, then $C = \pi X$,    $V = \sigma^{-1}(\#X)$, and $Z = Y + \mathsf{P}(V)$. \label{chh1}  \item\label{chh2} The algebraic prolongation    $A = B \oplus_R(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j) $ is a transitive, infinitesimal,    filtered Cartan geometry, with model data $\tau $ defined by    {\normalfont C2}  and Cartan connection $\nabla $ uniquely determined by    {\normalfont C3}.  \item\label{cch3} Every transitive, infinitesimal, filtered Cartan geometry    $(A, \nabla, \tau)$, with anchor kernel $A^0$, is, up to isomorphism, the algebraic    prolongation of its Levi reduction, with Cartan connection $\nabla $ and model data    $\tau $ of the form just described.  \end{conditions}\end{theorem}\noindent%The section $\mathrm P$ is called the {\df Rho tensor}.\begin{proof}  It is a triviality that $\Phi $ is injective. A dimension count implies $\Phi$ must be an  isomorphism. A substitution verifies the rest of \eqref{chh1}.  That $\tau $, defined by C2, is model data follows from the  properties of $T$ posited in \eqref{chh0} and the definition of the semi-direct product  Lie bracket for $B^0 \oplus_R(\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j)$.  The isomorphism $\Phi$ intertwines the representation $R$ of $B$ on  $\graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_j {\mathfrak a}_j)$ with some representation  $\rho^{B \oplus 0}$ of $B \oplus 0 \subset A$ on $A$:  \begin{equation}    \rho^{B \oplus 0}_{(X,0)}\Phi(V, C, Z) = \Phi\Big(\,R_X((V, C, Z))\,\Big).\label{ret1}  \end{equation}  To apply Proposition \ref{constructing} to $A$, with $\tau $ defined by  C2, and $A'=A^1=0 \oplus (\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j)$, we need to  establish \eqrefs{constructing}{ext1}. Here this means verifying that  \begin{equation*}    \rho^{B \oplus 0}_{(X,0)}\Phi(0, C, Z) = [(X,0), \Phi(0, C, Z)],  \end{equation*}  for $X \in \Gamma(B),\,C \in B^0,\,Z \in \Gamma(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$, which is  an easy computation following from \eqref{ret1}, and the fact that the bracket on the  right-hand side is the semi-direct product bracket. Similarly, since $\tau $ is defined  by C2, the properties of $T$ in \eqrefs{inflevi}{chh0} allow one to show that  \eqrefs{constructing}{ext3} also holds in the present case. We conclude that there  exists a unique Cartan connection $\nabla $ on $A$, strongly compatible with $\tau $,  such that  \begin{equation*}    \bar\nabla_{(X,Y)} \Phi(V, C, Z) = \rho^{B \oplus 0}_{(X,0)} \Phi (V, C, Z)      + \tau((0,Y), \Phi(V, C, Z)).  \end{equation*}  The formula C3 follows from \eqref{ret1},  C2, and the identity $(0, Y) = \Phi(0, 0, Y)$. This concludes  the proof of \eqref{chh2}. Part \eqref{cch3} of the theorem was already established in  \ref{consequences}.\end{proof}\subsection{Algebraic prolongation: Global case}\label{abstract_levi_}Let $({\mathcal H}, \oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j, R, T)$ be an abstract Levi structure. Thevector bundle $\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j$ gets an $R$-invariant graded Lie algebrabundle structure by restricting $T$. This bundle has a necessarily nilpotent Lie algebramodel, and we let $\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ denote the nilpotent Lie groupbundle with simply connected fibres that integrates $ \oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$. Therestriction of $R$ to a representation of ${\mathcal H} $ on$\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$, by fibre-wise Lie algebra derivations, integrates to arepresentation of ${\mathcal H}$ on $\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ by fibre-wise Liegroup isomorphisms; denoting this by $R$ also, we have $R_h(\exp(Y))=\exp(R_hY)$, for anyarrow $h \in {\mathcal H}$ beginning at the base point of$Y \in \oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j $, because $\exp$ is a diffeomorphism in this case.The {\df algebraic prolongation} of the Levi structure is the Lie groupoid semi-directproduct ${\mathcal G} = {\mathcal H} \times_R\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$. This isa filtered Lie groupoid with Lie algebroid $B \oplus_{dR} (\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$,the algebraic prolongation of Levi structure's infinitesimalization.Additionally, the representation $\adjoint^T$ of the nilpotent Lie algebra bundle$\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$ on$\graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ integrates to arepresentation of the Lie group bundle $\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ on$\graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$. We denote thisrepresentation by $\Adjoint^T$.\begin{theorem}[A characterization of transitive filtered Cartan geometries]\mbox{}\newline  Let $({\mathcal H}, \oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j, R, T)$ be an abstract Levi structure,  $B$ the Lie algebroid of ${\mathcal H} $, $\mathsf{P}$ an arbitrary section of  $\graded(T^*\!M)\otimes(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$, $s \colon TM \rightarrow B$ any  splitting of the anchor of $B$, and $\sigma \colon \graded(TM) \rightarrow TM $ some  splitting of the filtered manifold $M$. Then:  \begin{conditions}  \item\label{chh01} The map    $\Phi \colon \graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_j {\mathfrak a}_j) \rightarrow B    \oplus_{dR}(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ defined by C1 is    invertible.  \item\label{chh02} The algebraic prolongation    ${\mathcal G}  = {\mathcal H} \times_R\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j) $ is a transitive,    filtered Cartan geometry, with model data $\tau $ defined by    C2 and Cartan connection $D$ uniquely determined by  \item[{\normalfont\bfseries C3$^\prime$.}]    \vspace{0.5\baselineskip}    \quad $\bar D_{(h,k)}\Phi(V, C, Z) = \Phi(\Adjoint^T_k(R_hV, \Adjoint_h C, r_h Z))$.  \item\label{cch03}    \vspace{0.5\baselineskip}    Every transitive, filtered, Cartan geometry    $({\mathcal G}, D, \tau)$, with Lie algebroid $A$ and anchor kernel $A^0$, is, up to    isomorphism, the algebraic prolongation of some abstract Levi structure, with Cartan    connection $D $ and model data $\tau $ of the form just described.  \end{conditions}\end{theorem}\begin{proof}  The preceding infinitesimal result, Theorem \ref{inflevi_}, applies with $R$ there  replaced with $dR$. In particular, \eqref{chh01} is just a restatement of  \eqrefs{inflevi_}{chh1}.  The isomorphism $\Phi$ intertwines the representation $R$ of ${\mathcal H} $ on  $\graded(TM) \oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_j {\mathfrak a}_j)$ with some representation  $\rho^{{\mathcal H} \times \identity}$ on  ${\mathcal H} \times \identity \subset {\mathcal G} $ on  $A = B\oplus_{dR}(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j) $:  \begin{equation}    \rho^{{\mathcal H} \times \identity}_{(h,\identity)}\Phi(V, C, Z) =      \Phi\Big(\,R_h((V, C, Z))\,\Big).\label{ret01}  \end{equation}  To apply the global case of Proposition \ref{constructing} to ${\mathcal G}$, with  $\tau $ defined by C2, and  ${\mathcal G}'=\identity \times \exp(\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j)$, we  need to establish \eqrefs{constructing}{pony4}. Here this means verifying that  \begin{equation*}    \rho^{{\mathcal H} \times \identity}_{(h,\identity)}\Phi(0, C, Z) =    \Adjoint_{(h,\identity)}\Phi(0, C, Z),  \end{equation*}  for each arrow $h \in {\mathcal G}$ beginning as some $m \in M$, $C \in B^0|_m$,  $Z \in \oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j|_m$. With the help of \eqref{ret01}, and the  definition of $\Adjoint$ for semi-direct products, this is readily checked. We conclude  that there exists a unique $\tau $-compatible Cartan connection $D$ on ${\mathcal G} $  such that  \begin{equation}    \label{mask1}    \bar D_{(h,k)}\Phi(V, C, Z) = \Adjoint^\tau_{(\identity,k)}\rho^{{\mathcal H}\times \identity}_{(h,\identity)}\Phi(V, C, Z),  \end{equation}  where $\Adjoint^\tau$ is the representation of  $\identity \times \exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j) \subset {\mathcal G} $ on $A$  integrating the representation $\adjoint^\tau$ of  $A^1=0\oplus (\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ on $A$ defined by  $\adjoint^\tau_{(0,Y)}W = \tau((0,Y), W)$. By the definition of $\tau $, the isomorphism  $\Phi $ intertwines the representation $\adjoint^\tau$ of $A^1$ with the representation  $Y \mapsto T((0,0,Y),\,\cdot\,)$ of $\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$. Consequently, as  $\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ has connected fibres, $\Phi $ intertwines the  representations $\Adjoint^\tau$ and $\Adjoint^T$:  \begin{equation}    \label{mask2}    \Adjoint^\tau_{(\identity, k)}\Phi(V, C, Z) = \Phi(\Adjoint^T_k((V, C, Z)).  \end{equation}  Combining \eqref{ret01}, \eqref{mask1} and \eqref{mask2}, we obtain C3${}^\prime$.  It is easy to see that the infinitesimalization $\nabla $ of $D$ must be the Cartan  connection delivered by Theorem \ref{inflevi_}. Since $\nabla $ is {\em strongly}  compatible with $\tau $, $D$ is also.  Turning to \eqref{cch03}, let $({\mathcal G}, D, \tau)$ be a transitive, filtered, Cartan  geometry with Lie algebroid $A$ and anchor kernel $A^0$. By Theorem \eqrefs{weyl2}{pr5},  we may suppose, without loss of generality, that  ${\mathcal G} = {\mathcal H} \times_R\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$, for some  filtered Lie groupoid ${\mathcal H}$ and graded Lie algebra bundle  $\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$, where ${\mathcal H} $ acts on  $\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j$ by grading-preserving fibre-wise derivations, an action  that integrates to an action of ${\mathcal H} $ on $\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$  by fibre-wise Lie group isomorphisms, both actions to be denoted $R$. Moreover, the Lie  algebroid $B$ of ${\mathcal H} $ will satisfy $B^j=0$ for $j>0$ and, by our hypotheses  on $A$, $B$ is transitive with $B^0$ being the anchor kernel.  Evidently, $A$ is identifiable with $B \oplus_{dR}(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$,  which the infinitesimalization $\nabla $ of $D$ makes into a transitive, infinitesimal,  filtered Cartan geometry. Consequently, the analysis of \ref{consequences} applies, with  $R$ in that analysis replaced with $dR$. Define ${\mathfrak a}'_j $, $j>0$, and $\Phi$  as in that analysis. Then $\tau $ can be recovered from $T=\graded_0(\tau)$ according to  C2, and, according to Proposition  \eqrefs{model_data_gradings}{uu4}, the grading $A=\oplus_i {\mathfrak a}'_i$ (there  denoted $\oplus_i {\mathfrak a}_i$) is $\bar D_{(h,\identity)}$-invariant, for every  $h \in {\mathcal H} $. It follows that $\Phi $ intertwines some grading-preserving  representation $R$ of ${\mathcal H}$ on  $\graded(A)=\graded(TM)\oplus B^0 \oplus (\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ with the  $\bar D$-representation of ${\mathcal H} \times \identity$ on $A$:  \begin{equation}    \bar D_{(h, \identity)} \Phi(V,C,Z) = \Phi\Big(\,R_h(V, C, Z)\,\Big);\quad h \in {\mathcal H}.  \end{equation}  Since $\Phi(0, C, Z) = (C, Z)$ and $\bar D $ is just the canonical  representation on sections of $A^0$, $R$ must extend the canonical representation of  ${\mathcal H}$ on $\graded(A^0) = B^0 \oplus(\oplus_{j>0} {\mathfrak a}_j) $, which  gives  \begin{equation}    \bar D_{(h, \identity)} \Phi(V,C,Z)\Phi(R_hV, \Adjoint_hC, R_h Z);\quad h \in {\mathcal H},\label{cab1}  \end{equation}  where the ``$R$'' in $R_hZ$ has the meaning given at the beginning of this discussion.  On the other hand, \eqrefs{consequences}{cab0} implies  \begin{equation*}    \bar\nabla_{(0,Y)} \Phi(\xi) = \Phi(\adjoint^T_Y \xi),\qquad    Y \in \Gamma(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j),\,\xi \in \Gamma(\graded(A)).  \end{equation*}  Because $\exp(\oplus_{j>0}{\mathfrak a}_j)$ has simply-connected fibres, this  intertwining by $\Phi $ of infinitesimal actions is an intertwining of their  corresponding globalizations:  \begin{equation}    \label{cab2}    \bar D_{(\identity, k)} \Phi(V, C, Z) = \Phi(\Adjoint^T_k(V,C, Z)).  \end{equation}  The formula C3$^\prime$ follows from \eqref{cab1} and \eqref{cab2} and the identity  $(h, k) = (\identity, k)(h, \identity)$ for the semi-direct product.\end{proof}\section{Deforming model data and Cartan connections}\label{deform}We need a way to relate different choices of model data in a filtered Cartan geometryusing transformations of the underlying Lie algebroid or integrating Liegroupoid. Proposition \ref{deformations} below addresses this need.\subsection{$\psi$-deformations}\label{deformations}For each $\psi \in \Gamma(T^*\!M \otimes A) $, define$\psi_{TM}:TM \rightarrow TM$ by\begin{equation}\label{opal1}  \psi_{TM} V = V + \#\,\psi\,V,\end{equation}and define $\Adjoint_\psi \colon A \rightarrow A$ by\begin{equation}  \label{opal2a}  \Adjoint_\psi X = X + \psi(\#X).\end{equation}Here $\# \colon A \rightarrow TM$ denotes the anchor. The natural context giving rise tothese definitions is provided in \ref{kernel}. For the moment, it suffices to observethat: (i) If $\psi_{TM}$ is invertible, then so is $\Adjoint_\psi$, with\begin{equation}  \label{opal2}  \Adjoint_\psi^{-1} = \Adjoint_{-\psi\circ(\psi_{TM})^{-1}};\end{equation}and (ii) If $A$ is filtered and transitive and $\psi$ is homogeneous of degree one, i.e.,$\psi(A^k) \subset A^{k+1}$ for any $k$, then $\psi_{TM}$ is invertible, because in thatcase $\psi_{TM}$ is also homogeneous of degree one with$\graded_1(\psi_{TM})=\identity$.If $\psi $ above is homogeneous of degree $l \ge 1$, then $\Adjoint_\psi - \identity$ istoo, and the following easily follows:\begin{lemma}  If $A$ is transitive then the push-forward $\tau' = (\Adjoint_\psi)_*\tau  $ of any model  data $\tau $ on $A$ compatible with a filtration will share the same reduction to a Lie  algebra bundle structure on $\graded(A)$, that is, $\graded_0(\tau')=\graded_0(\tau)$.\end{lemma}\noindent%Conversely, we have:\begin{proposition}  Let $A$ be a transitive filtered Lie algebroid with anchor kernel $A^0$, and $\tau$,  $\tau'$ model data compatible with the filtration. Assume $T := \graded_0(\tau)$ admits  a fibre-wise grading section $E$, and that $\graded_0(\tau') = T$. Then:  \begin{conditions}  \item\label{saturn1} There exists a unique $\psi \in \Gamma(T^*\!M \otimes A)$,    necessarily homogeneous of degree one, such that $\tau' = (\Adjoint_\psi)_*\tau$.  \item\label{saturn2} If $\tau $ is compatible with some infinitesimal Cartan connection    $\nabla $, then $\tau'$ is compatible with the infinitesimal Cartan connection    $\nabla'$ defined implicitly by    $$\bar\nabla'_XY = \Adjoint_\psi (\bar\nabla_X(\Adjoint_\psi^{-1}Y)).$$  \item \label{saturn3} If ${\mathcal G} $ is a Lie groupoid integrating $A$ and $\tau $    is compatible with some Cartan connection $D$, then $\tau'$ is compatible with the    Cartan connection $D'$ defined implicitly by    $$\bar D'_gX = \Adjoint_{\psi(gm)}\bar D_g \Adjoint_{\psi(m)}^{-1}X,$$ for each arrow    $g \in {\mathcal G} $ beginning at some $m \in M$, and each $X \in A|_{m}$, and whose    infinitesimalization is $\nabla'$ above,  \item\label{saturn5} If $({\mathcal G}, D, \tau)$ is a transitive filtered Cartan    geometry, then $({\mathcal G}, D', \tau')$ is another transitive filtered Cartan    geometry with the same Levi reduction. An analogous statement holds in the    infinitesimal case.  \end{conditions}\end{proposition}%\noindent%When $\tau'=(\Adjoint)_*\tau$, and $D'$ and $\nabla'$ are defined (implicitly) by\eqref{saturn3} and \eqref{saturn2}, then the primed quantities are called the {\df  $\psi$-deformations} of $\tau $, $D$ and $\nabla $. The proof of the proposition isgiven in \ref{existence_deformations}--\ref{same_levi} below. In\ref{gauge_transforms} we connect $\psi$-deformations to Weyl gauge transformations.\subsection{The existence of deformations}\label{existence_deformations}The existence part of Proposition \eqrefs{deformations}{saturn1} has an elementary proofproceeding as follows. Let $\tau$ and $\tau'$ be model data as in the proposition and fixa lift $\epsilon \in \Gamma(A^0)$ through $A^0 \rightarrow A^0/A^1$ of $E$. Then, as wehave described in \ref{splittings}, we get two corresponding splittings$\Phi, \Phi' \colon \graded(A) \rightarrow A $ of the Lie algebra bundles $(A, \tau)$ and$(A, \tau')$. By definition, we have $\Phi_*T=\tau $ and $\Phi'_*T= \tau'$, and, because$\tau $ and $\tau' $ agree on $A^0$ (by the definition of model data)$\Phi' \circ \Phi^{-1}$ is the identity on $A^0$. It follows from the transitivity of $A$that $\tau' = \Psi_* \tau$, where $\Psi := \Phi' \circ \Phi^{-1}$ satisfies$\Psi(X) = X + \psi (\#X)=\Adjoint_\psi X$ for some vector bundle morphism$\psi \colon TM \rightarrow A$.Also following from the definition of splittings, we have, for any $X \in \Gamma(A^k)$ and$k \in {\mathbb Z}$,\begin{gather}  X \equiv \Phi(\graded_k(X)) \modulo A^{k+1},\label{opal3}\\  \Phi'(\graded_k(X)) \equiv X \modulo A^{k+1}.\label{opal4}\end{gather}From \eqref{opal3} we conclude that$(\Phi' \circ \Phi^{-1})(X) \equiv \Phi'(\graded_k(X)) \modulo A^{k+1}$, because$\Phi' \circ \Phi^{-1}$ is filtration-preserving. Combining this with \eqref{opal4}, weconclude that $\Phi' \circ \Phi^{-1}(X) \equiv X \modulo A^{k+1}$, i.e., $\psi$ ishomogeneous of degree one.\subsection{The kernel of $J^1 {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G} $}\label{kernel}% Indeed, as the proof will make clear, the sections of $T^*\!M \otimes A$ form a group (a% subgroup of all bisections of $\model$) and this group acts transitively and freely on the% space of model data $\tau $ with the stated compatibility requirements, and which satisfy $\graded(\tau) = T$ for some fixed $T$.Let $A$ be any Lie algebroid. The sections $\psi$ of $T^*\!M \otimes A$ for which$\psi_{TM}$ are invertible are sections of an open neighborhood of the zero section of$T^*\!M \otimes A$ that we denote $\model$. The proposition below shows that this set is amodel for the kernel of the projection $J^1 {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G} $ for{\em any} Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G} $ integrating $A$. In particular, it is a totallyintransitive Lie groupoid (a bundle of Lie groups). We now describe the correspondinggroupoid structure directly.The zero section of $T^*\!M \otimes A$ constitutes the set of identity elements of$\model$. Multiplication is given by\begin{equation}  \label{clost}  \psi \varphi = \psi + \varphi + \psi \circ \# \circ \varphi = \varphi + \psi \circ \varphi_{TM}.\end{equation}Inversion is given by\begin{equation*}  \psi \mapsto  -\psi \circ (\psi_{TM})^{-1}.\end{equation*}A convenient consequence we mention in passing is the formula\begin{equation}  \label{clost2}  \psi \varphi^{-1} = (\psi - \varphi)\circ \varphi_{TM}^{-1}.\end{equation}With respect to these groupoid structures, the map$$\psi \mapsto \psi_{TM} \colon \model \rightarrow\automorphism({TM})$$ is a morphism of totally intransitive Lie groupoids and thecommutative kernel consists of those $\psi \colon TM \rightarrow A$ with image lying inthe kernel of the anchor.The reader will readily see that the maps defined by \eqrefs{kernel}{opal1} and\eqrefs{kernel}{opal2a} define representations of $\model$ on $A$ and $TM$ respectively.With respect to these representations the anchor is equivariant:$\#\Adjoint_\psi X = \psi_{TM}\#X$.Although the proposition below provides useful context, the proof of Proposition\ref{deformations} will not actually require it. However, the result is needed in Appendix\ref{rut}.Let ${\mathcal G} $ be any Lie groupoid with Lie algebroid $A$. Represent elements of$J^1 {\mathcal G} $ by maps tangent to some local bisection of ${\mathcal G} $, i.e., bycertain vector space morphisms $T_{m}M \rightarrow T_g {\mathcal G} $, where$g \in {\mathcal G} $ is some arrow beginning at some $m \in M$. Recall that the adjointrepresentation for a Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G} $ is actually a representation of$J^1 {\mathcal G} $ on $A$ \cite{Blaom_16b}, and that $J^1 {\mathcal G} $ also actsnaturally on $TM$. Both representations are denoted $\Adjoint$, and with respect to thesethe anchor is equivariant.\begin{proposition}[\cite{Blaom_16b}]  The map $\psi \mapsto \varcheck \psi \colon \model \rightarrow J^1 G$, defined by  $\varcheck \psi V = V + \psi\,V$, is a Lie groupoid morphism and embedding, whose image  is the kernel of the natural projection $J^1 G \rightarrow G$.\footnote{\cite{Blaom_16b}    adopts a different sign convention for the embedding and definition of $\model$.} This  map is equivariant with respect to the natural representations of both groupoids on $A$  and $TM$: $\Adjoint_{\varcheck\psi} V = \psi_{TM}V$,  $\Adjoint_{\varcheck\psi} X = \Adjoint_\psi X$, for $V \in {T_{m}M}$, $X \in A|_m$, and  $m \in M$.\end{proposition}\subsection{The deformed Cartan connections}In passing, let us explain how the deformed connections $\nabla'$ and $D'$ appearing inProposition \ref{deformations} arise very naturally. Recall that a Cartan connection $D$is the $n$-plane field determined by some Lie groupoid right-inverse$S \colon {\mathcal G} \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G}$ for the natural projection$ J^1 {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G}$. Given some $\psi \in \Gamma(\model)$, wemay, according to the preceding proposition, view it as bisection of $J^1 {\mathcal G} $and obtain, via conjugation, a corresponding automorphism $K_\psi$ of $J^1 {\mathcal G}$,sending an arrow $\mu \in J^1_g {\mathcal G} $, beginning at some $m \in M$ withprojection $g \in {\mathcal G}$, to $\varcheck{\psi(gm)}\mu (\varcheck{\psi(m)})^{-1}$. Itis easy to see that $S':=K_\psi \circ S \colon {\mathcal G} \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G} $is another right-inverse, defining a new Cartan connection $D'$. From the definition ofthe corresponding representations $\bar D$ and $\bar D'$, one immediately derives theformula in \eqrefs{deformations}{saturn3}.Now $\bar\nabla $ is the infinitesimalization of the representation $\bar D $, and ittherefore follows from the formula in \eqrefs{deformations}{saturn3}, and a simplecomputation, that the infinitesimalization $\bar\nabla'$ of $\bar D'$ is given by theformula in \eqrefs{deformations}{saturn2}.We now return to the proof of Proposition \ref{deformations}. Regardless of whether $A$ isintegrated by some Lie groupoid, the formula for $\bar\nabla'$ given in\eqrefs{deformations}{saturn2} must define an infinitesimal Cartan connection, by thecharacterization of such connections given in Proposition \ref{hsd}, which applies becausewe assume $A$ is transitive. The main point here is that \eqrefs{deformations}{saturn2}indeed defines a representation. The details are not difficult and are left to thereader. It is a simple computation to show that $\tau' = (\Adjoint_\psi)_*\tau $ must be$\bar\nabla'$-invariant ($\nabla'$-compatible) whenever $\tau $ is $\bar\nabla$-invariant($\nabla$-compatible).Similarly, the formula for $\bar D'$ given in \eqrefs{deformations}{saturn3} must define aglobal Cartan connection, by the characterization of such connections given in Proposition\ref{cartan}, and checking that $\tau' = (\Adjoint_\psi)_*\tau $ must be$\bar D'$-invariant ($D'$-compatible) whenever $\tau $ is $\bar D$-invariant($D$-compatible) is a simple computation.This completes the proof of parts \eqref{saturn2} and \eqref{saturn3} of Proposition\ref{deformations}.\subsection{The uniqueness of $\psi$-deformations}\label{deformation_uniqueness}Having seen that $\psi \mapsto \Adjoint_\psi $ defines a representation of $\model$ on$A$, to prove the uniqueness of $\psi $ in Proposition \eqrefs{deformations}{saturn1}, itevidently suffices to show that $(\Adjoint_\psi)^*\tau = \tau$ implies $\psi = 0$,whenever $\psi $ is homogeneous of degree one, because $\Adjoint_0=\identity$.Let $\tau $ and $E$ be as in Proposition \ref{deformations} and$\psi \colon TM \rightarrow A$ be homogeneous of degree one. If we temporarily write$\hat \psi(X) = \psi(\#X)$, then $(\Adjoint_\psi)^*\tau = \tau$ means\begin{equation*}  \tau(\hat\psi X, Y) + \tau(X,\hat\psi Y) + \tau(\hat\psi X, \hat\psi Y) = \hat\psi \tau(X, Y),\end{equation*}for all $X, Y \in \Gamma(A)$. In particular, if $\epsilon $ is any lift of $E$ through$A^0 \rightarrow A^0/A^1$, then\begin{equation}\label{opal5}  \tau(\hat\psi X, \epsilon) = \hat\psi(\tau(X, \epsilon)); \qquad X \in \Gamma(A).\end{equation}For an inductive argument, suppose $\psi $ (and therefore $\hat\psi $) is homogeneous ofdegree $l\ge 1$. Then \eqref{opal5} gives$T(\graded_l(\hat\psi)\xi, E) = \graded_l(\hat\psi)(T(\xi, E))$, for any$\xi \in \graded(A)$. In particular, if $j < 0$ and $\xi \in \graded(A)_j$, then$(j+l) \graded_l(\hat\psi)\xi = j \graded_l(\hat\psi)\xi $, which can only be true if$\graded_l(\hat\psi)\xi =0$. Since this last is trivially true in the other cases$j\ge 0$, we conclude that $\hat\psi$, and hence $\psi $, is homogeneous of degree$l+1$. Induction on $l$ shows $\psi = 0$. This completes the proof of Proposition\eqrefs{deformations}{saturn1}.\subsection{Levi reductions are unchanged by $\psi $-deformations}\label{same_levi}Conclusion \eqrefs{deformations}{saturn5} follows fairly easily from the definitions andthe statement preceding Lemma \ref{deformations} and is left to the reader.\subsection{Weyl gauge transformations and $\psi$-deformations}\label{gauge_transforms}If we use a Weyl gauge transformation to transform model data, then we can say somethingabout the $\psi$-deformation realizing the same change:\begin{proposition}  Let $A$ be a transitive filtered Lie algebroid with anchor kernel $A^0$, $\tau$ model  data compatible with the filtration, and assume $T := \graded_0(\tau)$ admits a  fibre-wise grading section $E$. Then for any $Z \in \Gamma(A^l)$, $l \ge 1$, there  exists unique $\psi \in \Gamma(T^*\!M \otimes A)$, homogeneous of degree $l$, such  that $(e^{-\adjoint_Z})_*\tau = (\Adjoint_\psi)_* \tau$. In that case, for any  $X \in A^k$, $k \in {\mathbb Z}$,  \begin{equation}    \label{opal7}    \psi(\#X) \equiv \tau(Z, X) \modulo A^{k+l+1}.  \end{equation}\end{proposition}\begin{proof}  By Proposition \ref{deformations}, there exists unique $\psi \in \Gamma(T^*\!M \otimes A)$  such that $(e^{-\adjoint_Z})_*\tau = (\Adjoint_\psi)_* \tau$, but {\em a priori} we know  only that $\psi $ is homogeneous of degree {\em one}. If $k \ge 0$, then \eqref{opal7}  is trivially true, because $\#X = 0$ in that case. So we suppose $k < 0$. Fixing some  lift $\epsilon $ through $A^0 \rightarrow A^0/A^1$ of $E$, we obtain corresponding  splittings $A = \oplus_j \oplus {\mathfrak a}_j$ and $A = \oplus_j {\mathfrak a}_j'$  with respect to the Lie algebra bundle structures $\tau $ and  $\tau':=(\Adjoint_\psi)_*\tau$. To prove \eqref{opal7}, it suffices to prove it, for any  $k <0 $, under the more restrictive hypothesis $X \in {\mathfrak a}_k$, in which case  \begin{equation}    \label{opal0}    \Adjoint_\psi X \in {\mathfrak a}_k'.  \end{equation}  Since $\Adjoint_\psi(X) = X + \psi(\#X)$ and $\psi$ is homogeneous of degree one, we may  define $X_k:=X$ and write $\Adjoint_\psi(X)=\sum_{m\ge k} X_m$, for some  $X_{k+1} \in {\mathfrak a}_{k+1}, X_{k+2}\in {\mathfrak a}_{k+2},$ etc., finite in  number. These preliminaries set up a simple but tedious computation, tracking the  relevant degrees of homogeneity. For completeness, details now follow.  The requirement \eqref{opal0} gives  \begin{equation}    \label{opal8}    \tau'\big(\epsilon, \sum_{m\ge k}X_m\big) = k\sum_{m\ge k}X_m.  \end{equation}  Now, using the fact that $\tau(Z, \epsilon) = - lZ$, we have  \begin{gather*}     e^{\adjoint_Z}\epsilon=\epsilon +[Z, \epsilon] + \frac{1}{2}[Z,[Z,\epsilon]] + \cdots\\       =\epsilon +\tau(Z, \epsilon) + \frac{1}{2}\tau(Z,\tau(Z,\epsilon)) + \cdots       = \epsilon - lZ,     \end{gather*}     [SOME PROBLEM HERE: $Z$ also needs to be split into components.] More generally, F1     and the expansion of $e^{\adjoint_Z}$ show that  \begin{equation}     e^{\adjoint_Z}Y \equiv Y \modulo A^l; \qquad Y \in \Gamma(A).\label{ruby0}  \end{equation}  Regarding the left-hand side of \eqref{opal8}, we now compute  \begin{gather}    \tau'\big(\epsilon, \sum_{m\ge k}X_m\big) =    e^{-\adjoint_Z}\tau\Big(e^{\adjoint_Z}\epsilon,    e^{\adjoint_Z}\big(\sum_{m\ge k}X_m\big)\Big)\notag\\    = e^{-\adjoint_Z}\tau\big(\epsilon - lZ, \sum_{m\ge k}X_m\big) +      e^{-\adjoint_Z}\tau(\epsilon - lZ, W),\label{ruby5}  \end{gather}  where $W := e^{-\adjoint_Z}(\sum_{m\ge k}X_m) - \sum_{m\ge k}X_m$, which lies in $A^l$,  by \eqref{ruby0}. In particular, the second term on the right-hand side of \eqref{ruby5}  must lie in $A^l$ also. Developing \eqref{ruby5} further:  \begin{gather*}    \tau'\big(\epsilon, \sum_{m\ge k}X_m\big) \equiv  e^{-\adjoint_Z}\tau\big(\epsilon - lZ, \sum_{m\ge k}X_m\big) \modulo A^l \\    \equiv  e^{-\adjoint_Z}\Big(\,\sum_{m\ge k}m X_m -    l\sum_{m\ge k}\tau(Z, X_m)\,\Big) \modulo A^l\\  \equiv \sum_{m\ge k}m X_m - l\sum_{m\ge k}\tau(Z, X_m) \modulo A^l,  \end{gather*}  where in the last step we have again applied identity \eqref{ruby0}. Combining this with  \eqref{opal8}, we conclude  \begin{equation}    \label{ruby6}    \sum_{m>k}(m-k) X_m \equiv \sum_{m\ge k}\tau(Z, X_m) \modulo A^l.  \end{equation}  Since $\sum_{m\ge k}\tau(Z, X_m)$ lies in $A^{k+l}$, this shows that $X_m=0$ whenever  $k +1 \le m \le k + l -1$. In particular, this shows that  $\psi (\#X)=\sum_{m\ge k+1}X_m$, lies in $A^{k+l}$, completing the proof that $\psi $ is  homogeneous of degree $l$. But now we may rewrite \eqref{ruby6} as  \begin{equation*}    \sum_{m \ge l} m X_{m+k} \equiv l\sum_{m\ge k}\tau(Z,X_m)\modulo A^l.  \end{equation*}  Since $l \ge l + k + 1$, because $k < 0$, we have $A^l \subset A^{l +k +1}$, and it  follows that  \begin{gather*}    \sum_{m \ge l} m X_{m+k} \equiv l\sum_{m\ge k}\tau(Z,X_m)\modulo A^{l+k+1}\\    \implies lX_{l+k} \equiv l \tau(Z, X_k) \modulo A^{l+k+1}\\    \implies \psi(\#X) \equiv \tau(Z, X) \modulo A^{l+k+1}  \end{gather*}\end{proof}\section{Model curvature}\label{modelcurvature}Model curvature, defined for parabolic geometries in \ref{normalization}, makes sense foran arbitrary infinitesimal filtered Cartan geometry $(A, \nabla, \tau)$, so long as werestrict to the transitive case. However, it now depends on both $\tau $ and thecompatible connection $\nabla$, which is not generally determined uniquely by $\tau $. Themain goal of this section is to understand how model curvature transforms under Weyl gaugetransformations (Proposition \ref{curv_deform_gauge}) and $\psi$-deformations (Proposition\ref{curv_deform}). We additionally define the ``oid'' analogue of tractor bundles\cite{Bailey_etal_94}, \cite[\S1.5.7]{Cap_Slovak_09} to explain the relationship betweeninfinitesimal Cartan connections on Lie algebroids and the adjoint tractor connectionsappearing in the principal bundle approach to parabolic geometries. Tractor bundles willnot be discussed further in the present article.\subsection{Model curvature and consequences of its vanishing}\label{model_curvature}Given a transitive global or infinitesimal filtered Cartan geometry with underlying Liealgebroid $\# \colon A \rightarrow TM$, let $\tau $ denote the model data and $\nabla $the compatible infinitesimal Cartan connection. With Theorem \ref{curvature} in mind, thedifference between between $\torsion \bar\nabla$ (a ``deformed bracket'') and $\tau $ (themodel bracket) is the {\df model curvature} of the geometry. Specifically, this isthe section $\kappa$ of $\wedge^2 T^*\!M \otimes A$ defined by\begin{equation*}  \kappa(\#X, \#Y) = \torsion \bar\nabla (X, Y) - \tau(X, Y); \qquad X, Y \in \Gamma(A),\end{equation*}which makes sense, on account of the strong compatibility property\eqrefs{compat2}{strong} and equation \eqrefs{hsd}{zero} defining $\bar\nabla $.\begin{proposition}  The model curvature $\kappa $ determines the curvature of $\nabla $ according to  \begin{equation*}    \curvature \nabla (U, V)X = (\bar\nabla_X \kappa)(U, V).  \end{equation*}  In particular, if $\kappa =0$, then $\curvature \nabla =0$ and  $\torsion \bar\nabla =\tau $. If the geometry is regular, then the model curvature is  homogeneous of degree one, i.e., $\kappa(U, V) \in \Gamma(A^{j+k+1})$ for  $U \in \Gamma(T^jM)$ and $V \in \Gamma(T^kM)$; it is otherwise homogeneous of degree  zero.\end{proposition}\noindent The consequences of $\curvature \nabla =0$ have already been discussed in\ref{curvature}. At the very least, according to Theorem \ref{curvature}, $A$ will locallybe a transitive action algebroid, with the acting Lie algebra isomorphic to the Liealgebra model for $\tau $.\begin{proof}  For any Cartan connection $\nabla $ on a transitive Lie algebroid, one has the easily  derived identity,  \begin{equation}    \label{solidarity}    \curvature\nabla(\#X, \#Y)Z =  \bar\nabla_Z\torsion \bar\nabla (X, Y).  \end{equation}  See, for example, \cite[\S5.4]{Blaom_06}. From the definition of $\kappa $,  \begin{equation}    \label{poland}    (\bar\nabla_Z\kappa)(\#X, \#Y) = (\bar\nabla_Z\torsion \bar\nabla)(X, Y) -    (\bar\nabla_Z \tau)(X, Y).  \end{equation}  The stated identity follows from \eqref{solidarity}, \eqref{poland} and the  $\bar\nabla $-invariance of $\tau $ (compatibility of $\tau$ and $\nabla$).  By definition,  \begin{equation}    \label{pot}    \kappa(\#X, \#Y) = \bar\nabla_XY - \bar\nabla_YX - ([X, Y] + \tau(X, Y))  \end{equation}  Suppose $X \in \Gamma(A^j)$ and $Y \in \Gamma(A^k)$ for some $j, k < 0$. As we argued in  the proof of Proposition \ref{homogeneous}, the non-positivity of $j$ and $k$ means that  $A^j \subset A^{j+k+1}$ and $A^k \subset A^{j+k+1}$. By $\bar\nabla $-invariance of the  filtration of $A$, the first two terms on the right-hand side of \eqref{pot} lie in  $\Gamma(A^{j+k+1})$. The last bracketed term lies in $\Gamma(A^{j+k})$, by axiom F1 for  Lie algebroid filtrations, and compatibility of $\tau $ with the filtration of $A$. In  the regular case, this bracketed term lies in $A^{j+k+1}$, by the definition of  regularity.\end{proof}\subsection{Model curvature under gauge transformations}\label{curv_deform_gauge}\begin{proposition}  Let $\tau $ be model data on a firmly filtered Lie algebroid $A$, $\nabla $ a compatible  Cartan connection on $A$, and $q \colon A \rightarrow A$ an infinitesimal Weyl gauge  transformation, which is necessarily of the form $q = e^{-\adjoint_Z}$, for some  $Z \in \Gamma(A^1)$. Then the pushforward $\tau'=q_*\tau$ is also model data, with  $\graded_0(\tau')=\graded_0(\tau)$, and  \begin{equation}    \label{tarragon1}      \bar\nabla'_XY = q\nabla_{q^{-1}X} (q^{-1}Y)  \end{equation}  implicitly defines a Cartan connection $\nabla' $ compatible with $\tau' $. If,  additionally, ${\mathcal G} $ is a firmly filtered Lie groupoid integrating $A$,  $\nabla $ the infinitesimalization of a Cartan connection $D$ on ${\mathcal G} $, and  $Q \colon {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G} $ is the global Weyl transformation  integrating $q$ (conjugation by $\exp(Z)$), then  \begin{equation}    \label{tarragon2}    \bar D_g' = q \circ \bar D_{Q^{-1}(g)} \circ q^{-1}  \end{equation}  implicitly defines a Cartan connection $D'$ on ${\mathcal G} $ compatible with $\tau'  $. In any case, if $\kappa$ is the model curvature of $(\tau, \nabla)$ and $\kappa'$ the  model curvature of $(\tau', \nabla')$, then  \begin{equation}    \label{tarragon5}    \kappa'(V_1, V_2) = q \kappa (V_1, V_2); \qquad V_1, V_2 \in \Gamma(TM).  \end{equation}\end{proposition}\begin{corollary}  Suppose $|A| = 1$. Then $\tau'$ is normal if $\tau$ is. That is, normality is preserved  by Weyl gauge transformations\end{corollary}\begin{proof}  Regard $\kappa $ and $\kappa'$ as sections of $\wedge^2 A^1 \otimes A$. In the special  case $|A| = 1$, we have $q Y = Y$, for any $Y \in \Gamma(A^1)$ and, applying the  proposition, we deduce that $\kappa' = q_*\kappa$. But then we have  $\partial^{\tau'}\kappa' = \partial^{\tau'}q_*\kappa = q_*\partial^\tau \kappa$, so that  normality of $\kappa'$ follows from the normality of $\tau $.\end{proof}\begin{proof}[Proof of proposition]  Since $q$ maps the anchor kernel of $A$ to itself, and $q$ is a Lie algebroid morphism,  it is easy to see $\tau' $ is model data, given that $\tau $ is. That  $\graded_0(\tau')=\graded_0(\tau)$ follows from the fact that $q - \identity$ is  homogeneous of degree one. To show $\nabla'$ is well-defined, we need to apply  Proposition \ref{hsd}, which means showing: (i) $\bar\nabla'$, as defined by  \eqref{tarragon1}, is a representation, and supposing $\#Y=0$, that (ii)  $\bar\nabla'_XY = [X, Y]$. Verifying (i) is is an easy computation appealing to the fact  that $\bar\nabla $ is a representation and that $q$ is a Lie algebroid  morphism. Regarding (ii), we have  \begin{equation*}    \bar\nabla'_XY = q \bar\nabla_{q^{-1}X}(q^{-1}Y) = q[q^{-1}X, q^{-1}Y] = [X, Y],  \end{equation*}  where the second equality follows from the fact that  $\bar\nabla_{\xi}{\eta} = [\xi, \eta]$ whenever $\# \eta =0$, and that  $\# q^{-1} Y = \# Y$ for any $Y \in \Gamma(A)$. Since $\tau $ is $\bar\nabla$-invariant  by hypothesis, another easy computation shows that $\tau'$ is $\bar\nabla'$-invariant,  i.e., is compatible with $\bar\nabla'$.  Regarding the global case, we prove a little more, indicating the origins of formulas  \eqref{tarragon1} and \eqref{tarragon2}. Note first that because $Q$ is a groupoid  morphism, we have the identity  \begin{equation}    \label{tarragon3}    q \circ \Adjoint_\mu = \Adjoint_{(J^1Q)\mu} \circ q; \qquad \mu \in J^1 {\mathcal G},  \end{equation}  where $\Adjoint$ is the adjoint representation of $J^1 {\mathcal G} $ on ${\mathcal G} $  (see \cite{Blaom_16b}). For a proof of \eqref{tarragon3}, suppose $\mu = J^1_mb$, for  some bisection $b$ of $\mathcal G$. Then for an arbitrary element of $A$, represented by  source-fibre path $t \mapsto g(t)$ beginning at $m$, we have  \begin{equation*}    \Adjoint_{J^1_m b}\left(\frac{d}{dt}g(t)\big|_{t=0}\right)    = \frac{d}{dt}b(g(t)m)g(t)b(m)^{-1}\big|_{t=0},  \end{equation*}  so that  \begin{multline*}    (q \circ \Adjoint_{J^1_m b})\left(\frac{d}{dt}g(t)\big|_{t=0}\right)    = \frac{d}{dt}Q(b(g(t)m))Q(g(t))Q(b(m))^{-1}\big|_{t=0}\\    = \Adjoint_{(J^1Q)(J^1_m b)}  \frac{d}{dt}Q(g(t))\big|_{t=0}    = \Adjoint_{(J^1Q)(J^1_m b)}  q\left(\frac{d}{dt}g(t)\big|_{t=0}\right).  \end{multline*}  and \eqref{tarragon3} is proven. If  $S \colon {\mathcal G} \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G} $ is the splitting associated with  $D$, then associated with the splitting  \begin{equation}    \label{tarragon4}      S ' = J^1 Q \circ S \circ Q^{-1}  \end{equation}  is a new Cartan connection $D'$, and applying the identity \eqref{tarragon3} and the  definition of representations $\bar D $ and $\bar D'$ associated with these connections,  we obtain the formula \eqref{tarragon2}. One can either show directly that $\bar\nabla'$  is the infinitesimalization of $\bar D'$ or argue as follows. Let  $s, s' \colon A \rightarrow J^1 A$ be the splittings associated with the  infinitesimalizations $\nabla $ and $\nabla'$ of $D$ and $D'$. Then, differentiating  \eqref{tarragon4}, we have $s' = J^1q \circ s \circ q^{-1}$. Now we compute  \begin{multline*}    \bar\nabla'_XY = \adjoint_{s'X}Y = \adjoint_{J^1q(s(q^{-1}X))}Y      = \adjoint_{J^1q(J^1(q^{-1}X) + \nabla(q^{-1} X))}Y\\      = \adjoint_{J^1X + q \circ \nabla (q^{-1} X)} Y      = [X, Y] + q(\nabla (q^{-1}X))(\# Y)\\      = q\big(\,[q^{-1}X, q^{-1}Y] + \nabla_{\# q^{-1} Y} (q^{-1} X)\,\big)      = q\bar\nabla_{q^{-1}X}(q^{-1}Y),  \end{multline*}  using $\#Y = \# q^{-1} Y$ in the second last step. This recovers \eqref{tarragon2}. The  $\bar D'$-invariance of $\tau'$ readily follows from the $\bar D$-invariance of $\tau $,  i.e., $\tau' $ is indeed compatible with $D'$.  Regarding \eqref{tarragon5}, we compute, for any $X, Y \in \Gamma(A)$,  \begin{align*}    \kappa'(\#X, \#Y) &= \bar\nabla'_XY - \bar\nabla'_YX - [X, Y] - \tau'(X, Y)\\                      &= q \bar\nabla_{q^{-1}X}(q^{-1}Y) - q\bar\nabla_{q^{-1}Y}(q^{-1}X)                        - q[q^{-1}X, q^{-1}Y] - q(\tau(q^{-1}X, q^{-1}Y)) \\    &= q\kappa(\# q^{-1}X, \# q^{-1}Y) = q \kappa(\# X, \# Y).  \end{align*}\end{proof}As remarked in \ref{normalization}, in the parabolic case we can view model curvature as asection of $\wedge^2 A^1 \otimes A$, which appears in a natural chain complex. We examinethis complex in more detail next.\subsection{The Kostant codifferential and its homology}\label{homology}Let $A$ be a filtered Lie algebroid with filtration-compatible model data $\tau $. For themoment, the Lie algebra model for $\tau $ is arbitrary; it need not be semisimple, and weneed not suppose $A$ is transitive. However we will assume $A$ admits a $\tau$-compatibleWeyl gauge.Recall from \ref{normalization} that we have a natural chain complex\begin{equation}  \label{chain1} 0 \leftpartial{\tau} A \leftpartial{\tau} A^1 \otimes A\leftpartial{\tau} \wedge^2 A^1 \otimes A \leftpartial{\tau} \cdots,\end{equation}where $\partial^\tau$ is defined by \eqrefs{normalization}{form1}. In the propositionbelow, we will establish that the corresponding bundles of homology groups haverealizations depending only on $T = \graded_0(\tau)$, and are canonically {\em graded},and not just canonically filtered.Notice that the bracket $T = \graded_0(\tau)$ makes $\graded(A)$ into a representation ofthe Lie algebra bundle $\graded(A^1)$ and we obtain a graded version of \eqref{chain1},\begin{equation}  \label{chain2} 0 \leftpartial{*}  \graded(A)  \leftpartial{*} \graded(A^1) \otimes \graded(A)  \leftpartial{*} \wedge^2 \graded(A^1) \otimes \graded(A) \leftpartial{*} \cdots,\end{equation}where $\partial^*$ is defined by the same formula \eqrefs{normalization}{form1}, with $\tau $ therereplaced with $T$.% \footnote{{\em Explanation of terminology.} In later applications, where%   $T$ is fixed, and $\tau $ variable, the differential $\partial^*$ will be the formal adjoint of the differential $\partial$ for some cochain complex.}The filtration of $A$ induces a filtration of each term in the complex \eqref{chain1}; forexample decomposable elements of $(\wedge^2 A^1 \otimes A)^l$ are of the form$Y\wedge Z \otimes X$, for some $Y\in A^i$, $Z \in A^j$ and $X \in A^k$ with $i, j \ge 1$and $i + j +k = l$. Similarly, each term of the complex \eqref{chain2} is graded. Thedifferential $\partial^\tau$ is filtration-preserving, while $\partial^*$ isgrading-preserving. There is no canonical chain map from complex \eqref{chain1} to\eqref{chain2}. However, for each fixed $l \in {\mathbb Z} $, we have subcomplexes\begin{gather}  \label{chain3} 0 \leftpartial{\tau}  A^l \leftpartial{\tau} (A^1\otimes A)^l \leftpartial{\tau} (\wedge^2 A^1 \otimes A)^l \leftpartial{\tau} \cdots \\  \label{chain4} 0 \leftpartial{*}  \graded(A)_l  \leftpartial{*} (\graded(A^1) \otimes \graded(A))_l  \leftpartial{*} (\wedge^2 \graded(A^1) \otimes \graded(A))_l \leftpartial{*} \cdots,\end{gather}and $\graded$ is a chain map from \eqref{chain3} to \eqref{chain4}.We let $H_n(A^1, A)$, $H_n(\graded(A^1), \graded(A))$, $H_n(A^1, A)^l$, and$H_n(\graded(A^1),\graded(A))_l$ denote the degree $n$ homologies of the complexes\eqref{chain1}, \eqref{chain2}, \eqref{chain3}, and \eqref{chain4}, understood as vectorbundles over $M$. Because $\partial^\tau $ is filtration-preserving and $\partial^*$ isgrading-preserving, for each $l \in {\mathbb Z}$, the natural morphisms\begin{align*}  H_n(A^1,A)^l &\rightarrow H_n(A^1,A)\\  H_n(\graded(A^1),\graded(A))_l &\rightarrow H_n(\graded(A^1),\graded(A))\end{align*}are injective, and we see easily that this allows us to view $H_n(A^1, A)$ as a filteredvector bundle, and $H_n(\graded(A^1),\graded(A))$ as a graded vector bundle.Each term in the complex \eqref{chain1} is a representation of $A^1$, and $\partial^\tau $is equivariant with respect to these representations, so that $H_n(A^1, A)$ is a also an$A^1$-representation.  These integrate to representations of the bundle ofsimply-connected nilpotent Lie groups $\exp(A^1)$ integrating $A^1$. Tractor bundles are relevant to theproblem of constructing invariant differential operators, which is beyond the scope of thepresent article.Let $({\mathcal G}, D, \tau)$ be a transitive, filtered, Cartan geometry and $\nabla $ thecorresponding infinitesimal Cartan connection on the Lie algebroid $A$ of ${\mathcal G} $.Recall that strong compatibility means that the representation $\bar\nabla$ of $A$ onitself as a Lie algebroid, and the representation of $A$ on itself as a Lie algebrabundle, $X \mapsto \adjoint^\tau(X) := \tau(X,\,\cdot\,)$, restrict to the samerepresentation of the anchor kernel $A^0$. So, we have\begin{gather*}  \adjoint^\tau_Y X = \bar\nabla_YX;\qquad Y \in \Gamma(A^0),\, X \in \Gamma(A)\\  \bar D_g\adjoint^\tau_Y X = \adjoint^\tau_{\bar D_gY}\bar D_gX; \qquad Y,\,X \in A|_m,\,m \in M,\, g \in {\mathcal G},\end{gather*}where the second identity is just the $\bar D $-invariance of $\tau $, and we thereunderstand $g$ to be any arrow beginning at $m$.More generally, a {\df tractor bundle} is any representation $E$ of ${\mathcal G}$,together with some infinitesimal representation $\adjoint^\tau$ of the Lie algebra bundle$(A, \tau)$ on $E$, satisfying\begin{gather}  \adjoint^\tau_Y\sigma  = Y \sigma;\qquad Y \in \Gamma(A^0),\, \sigma \in \Gamma(E)\label{hj1}\\  g\cdot\adjoint^\tau_Y \sigma = \adjoint^\tau_{\bar D_gY}(g\cdot \sigma);  \qquad Y \in A|_m,\,m \in M,\, \sigma \in E,\, g \in {\mathcal G}.\label{hj2}\end{gather}Here we use juxtaposition to denote the action of $A$ on $E$ as a Lie algebroid (theinfinitesimalization of the representation of ${\mathcal G}$ on $E$).The significance of tractor bundles is that they come equipped with Koszulconnections, called {\df tractor connections}, well-defined by\begin{equation*}  \nabla^E_{\#X} \sigma = X \sigma - \adjoint^\tau_X \sigma.\end{equation*}Here $\#$ denotes anchor. Returning to the special case $E=A$, where $\nabla^A$ is knownas the {\df adjoint tractor connection}, we readily derive the formula\begin{equation}  \nabla^A_VY = \nabla_VY + \kappa(V, \#Y),\label{tractor1}\end{equation}which proves the following:\begin{aside}  The infinitesimal Cartan connection is the adjoint tractor connection if and only if the  model curvature vanishes.\end{aside}For an explicit construction of tractor bundles, connecting them with definition given in\cite{Cap_Slovak_09}, we adopt the principal bundle point of view. Fix some arbitrary$m_0 \in M$, let $\Pi \subset {\mathcal G} $ denote all arrows beginning at $m_0$,$P \subset \Pi$ the Lie group of arrows beginning and ending at $m_0$, ${\mathfrak g} $the semisimple Lie algebra $(A|_{m_0}, \tau)$, and ${\mathfrak g}^0$ the Lie algebra of$P$. Transitivity means $\Pi $ will be a principal $P$-bundle. Now let $V$ be a finitedimensional representation of $P$ and {\em suppose the corresponding infinitesimal  representation ${\mathfrak g}^0 \rightarrow \gl(V)$ is the restriction of a  representation $\rho \colon {\mathfrak g} \rightarrow \gl(V)$}. Then, we have:\begin{proposition}  The associated bundle $E(\rho) = \Pi \times_P V$ is a tractor bundle, and all tractor  bundles are of the form $E(\rho)$ for some  $\rho \colon {\mathfrak g} \rightarrow \gl(V)$ as above.\end{proposition}\begin{proof}For elements $g_1, g_2 \in \Pi $ and $\xi \in {\mathfrak g} $, let us write\begin{gather*}  \langle g_1, g_2 \rangle  = g_1g_2^{-1} \in {\mathcal G}\\  \langle g_2, \xi \rangle  = \bar D_{g_2} \xi \in A.\end{gather*}Transitivity means arbitrary elements of ${\mathcal G} $ and $A$ are of this form, and we have\begin{equation}  \bar D_{\langle g_1, g_2 \rangle } \langle g_2, \xi \rangle  = \langle g_1, \xi \rangle .\label{hj3}\end{equation}If, for each $[g_2, v] \equiv (g_2, v) \modulo P \in E(\rho)$, we define\begin{equation*}  \adjoint^\tau_{\langle g_2, \xi \rangle} [g_2, v]= [g_2, \rho_\xi v],\end{equation*}then, with the help of \eqref{hj3}, it is easy to see that \eqref{hj1} and \eqref{hj2}hold.  The proof of the second assertion is left to the reader.\end{proof}\section{Parabolic geometries. Thelast two facts are readily proved by readers familiar with the basic structure theory ofsemisimple Lie algebras.\begin{proof}  In the infinitesimal case, suppose that $\nabla^1$ and $\nabla^2$ are Cartan connections  compatible with model data $\tau$ on $A$, and write  $\delta_XY = \bar\nabla^1_XY - \bar\nabla^2_XY$. For each $X \in A|_m$, $m \in M$, we  may regard $\delta_X$ as a linear map $A|_m \rightarrow A|_m$ and, because $\tau $ is  both $\bar\nabla^1$ and $\bar\nabla^2$-invariant (by compatibility) it follows that  $\delta_X$ is even a Lie algebra derivation, with respect to the bracket $\tau  $. Moreover, as $\bar\nabla^j$ extends the canonical representation of $A$ on its anchor  kernel $A^0$, it follows that $\delta_XY = 0$ for all $Y$ in the parabolic subalgebra  $A^0|_m \subset A|_m$. By semisimplicity, there exists $Z \in A|_m$ such that  $\delta_X(\,\cdot\,) = \tau(Z,\,\cdot\,)$, and in particular such that $\tau(Z, Y)=0$  for all $Y \in A^0|_m$. So $Z$ is in the centralizer of a parabolic subalgebra of a  semisimple Lie algebra, and so is in the centralizer of some Borel subalgebra, and  consequently zero. This shows $\delta_X=0$ and we conclude  $\bar\nabla^1 = \bar\nabla^2$. Since $A$ is transitive, $\nabla^1=\nabla^2$, by  Proposition \ref{hsd}.  To prove strong compatibility, suppose $\bar\nabla $ is a $\tau $-compatible Cartan  connection (the infinitesimalization of some Cartan connection $D$ in the global  case). Fix some $X \in A^0|_m$, $m \in M$.  Then as $A^0$ lies in the anchor kernel, we  may view $\bar\nabla_X$ as a linear map $A|_m \rightarrow A|_m$. In fact, with respect  to the bracket $\tau $, this is a Lie algebra derivation, because $\tau $ is  $\bar\nabla $-invariant ($\nabla $ and $\tau $ are compatible). By the semisimplicity,  there is $X' \in A|_m$ such that $\bar\nabla_XY=\tau(X',Y)$ for all $Y \in A|_m$.  % Moreover, since $A^0|_m \subset A|_m$ is a {\em parabolic} subalgebra, and  % $\bar\nabla $ is filtration-preserving (Proposition \eqrefs{model_data_gradings}{u7}) we  % must have $X' \in A^0|_m$.  In the special case $Y \in A^0|_m$, we have $\bar\nabla_XY = [X, Y] = \tau(X, Y)$, the  first equality following from \eqrefs{hsd}{zero}, the second because $\tau $ extends the  canonical Lie algebra structure of $A^0$. Therefore, $\tau(X' - X, Y) = 0$ for all such  $Y$. In other words, $X' - X$ must lie in the centralizer of a parabolic subalgebra,  which is trivial, as noted above. We conclude $X'=X$ and whence  $\bar\nabla_XY = \tau(X, Y)$ for all $Y \in A|_m$.  In the global case, which is proven similarly, suppose $ D^1$ and $D^2$ are Cartan  connections compatible with model data $\tau $ and write  $\Delta_gY = \bar D^1_{g^{-1}} \bar D^2_g Y$. For each arrow $g \in {\mathcal G} $  beginning at some $m \in M$, we may regard $\Delta_g$ as a vector space automorphism  $A|_m \rightarrow A|_m$ and, because $\tau $ is both $\bar D^1$ and  $\bar D^2$-invariant, it follows that $\Delta_g$ is even a Lie algebra automorphism,  with respect to the bracket $\tau$. Since $\bar D^j$ extends the canonical  representation of $\mathcal G$ on $A^0$, $\Delta_gY = Y$ for all $Y \in A^0|_m$. So  $\Delta_g$ is an automorphism of a semisimple Lie algebra fixing all points in a  parabolic subalgebra, and whence in some Borel subalgebra, implying  $\Delta_g=\identity$. We conclude $\bar D^1= \bar D^2$ and, by Proposition \ref{cartan},  that $D^1 = D^2$.\end{proof}\subsection{All parabolic geometries admit compatible Weyl gauges}\label{all}Suppose $\tau $ is the model data associated with a parabolic geometry (global orinfinitesimal). Accordingly, any lift $\epsilon$ of $E$ through  $A^0 \rightarrow A^0/A^1$ will be a Weyl gauge for $A$. It is not hard to see that every  Weyl connection is of this form.\end{proof}Having already established, in Theorem \ref{uniquely_determined}, that the (infinitesimal)Cartan connections associated with (infinitesimal) parabolic geometries areautomatically strongly compatible with the model data, we obtain:\begin{corollary}  Every (infinitesimal) parabolic geometry is an (infinitesimal) filtered Cartan geometry.\end{corollary}\subsection{On the Lie subgroupoid ${\mathcal G}^1$ integrating $A^1$}\label{subgroups}\begin{proposition}  Let $({\mathcal G}, \tau)$ be a parabolic geometry, and let  ${\mathcal G}^1 \subset {\mathcal G} $ denote the source-connected subgroupoid  integrating $A^1$. For convenience, here is a restatement:\begin{lemma}  Let $\mathfrak g$ be a filtered Lie algebra and let $\epsilon \in {\mathfrak g}^0 $ be a  lift of some grading element $E \in \graded({\mathfrak g})$. Let $G^0$ be {\em any} Lie  group integrating ${\mathfrak g}^0 $, and let $G^1 \subset G^0$ be the connected Lie  subgroup with Lie algebra ${\mathfrak g}^1$. Then:  \begin{conditions}  \item Under the adjoint action, $G^1$ acts freely and transitively on the affine space    $\epsilon + {\mathfrak g}^1\subset {\mathfrak g}^0$, and consequently on $E$-splittings of    ${\mathfrak g} $. In particular, $G^1$ is simply connected (i.e.,    $G^1 \cong \exp({\mathfrak g}^1)$) so that the infinitesimal adjoint action of    ${\mathfrak g}^1$ on ${\mathfrak g} $ has a unique globalization    $\Adjoint\colon G^1 \rightarrow \automorphism({\mathfrak g})$ extending the adjoint    action just mentioned.\label{splittings1_}  % \item For any $g \in G^1$, $\Adjoint_g - \identity$ sends ${\mathfrak g}^j$ to  %   ${\mathfrak g}^{j+1}$, for any $j \in {\mathbb Z}$, i.e., is homogeneous of degree  %   one.\label{splittings30}  % \item The only linear map $\varphi \colon {\mathfrak g} \rightarrow {\mathfrak g} $ that  %   commutes with $\adjoint_e$ and such that $\varphi - \identity$ is homogeneous of degree  %   one is the identity.\label{splittings40}  \item For any $\epsilon_1, \epsilon_2 \in \epsilon + {\mathfrak g}^1$, we have    $\Phi_{\epsilon_2} = \Adjoint_g\circ \Phi_{\epsilon_1}$, where $g \in G^1$ is the unique element for    which $\epsilon_2 = \Adjoint_g \epsilon_1$. \label{splittings2_}  \end{conditions}\end{lemma}\begin{proof}  Note first that the filtration property ensures that the action of ${\mathfrak g}^1$ on  ${\mathfrak g}^0 $ is filtration-preserving, and the corresponding action on  $\graded({\mathfrak g}^0)$ is trivial. The same must be true for adjoint action of  $G^1$, as $G^1$ is connected. In particular $e + {\mathfrak g}^1$ must be  $G^1$-invariant, for any lift $e \in {\mathfrak g}^0$ of $E$. Suppose that the action of  $G^1$ on $e + {\mathfrak g}^1$ is infinitesimally free. Then, by a dimension count, this  action is infinitesimally transitive, and so transitive, since $G^1$ is  connected. Infinitesimal freeness means the stabilizer subgroup $H$ for the action is  discrete, so that $G^1 \rightarrow G^1/H \cong e + {\mathfrak g}^1$ is a covering  space. Since $e + {\mathfrak g}^1$ is simply connected, $H$ is trivial and the remaining  claims in \eqref{splittings1_} follow.  To show that the action of $G^1$ on $e + {\mathfrak g}^1$ is infinitesimally free, for  each $X \in {\mathfrak g}^1$, let $X^\dagger$ denote the infinitesimal generator of $X$,  a vector field on $e + {\mathfrak g}^1$. We have $X^\dagger(e') = [X, e']$, for  $e' \in e + {\mathfrak g}^1$. Supposing $X^\dagger(e') = 0$, write $X=\sum_{j>0} X_j$  for some $X_j \in {\mathfrak g}_j$, where ${\mathfrak g}=\oplus_j {\mathfrak g}_j$ is  the $E$-splitting corresponding to $e'$. Then we conclude that $\sum_{j>0}jX_j=0$, from  which it follows that $X=0$.  To prove \eqref{splittings2_}, observe that $\Phi_e$ is characterized among all  isomorphisms $\graded({\mathfrak g}) \rightarrow {\mathfrak g}$ by the requirement  \begin{equation*}    [\Phi_e(X), e] = j \Phi_e(X)\quad    \text{for all $X \in \graded_j({\mathfrak g}),\, j \in {\mathbb Z}$}.  \end{equation*}  Taking $e = e_1$ and applying $\Adjoint_g$ to both sides, we obtain  $[\Adjoint_g \Phi_{e_1}X, \Adjoint_g e_1] = j\Adjoint_g e_1$, i.e.,  \begin{equation*}    [(\Adjoint_g \circ\, \Phi_{e_1})X, e_2] = je_2.  \end{equation*}  So, by the aforementioned characterization, we conclude  $\Adjoint_g\circ\,\Phi_{e_1} = \Phi_{e_2}$.\end{proof}\section{Cartan connections on transitive Lie groupoids from representations}%\label{rut}In this section we prove \ref{cartan}, restated here for convenience:\begin{proposition}  Suppose ${\mathcal G} $ is a Lie groupoid with a transitive Lie algebroid $A$. Then for  every representation $\rho $ of ${\mathcal G} $ on $A$, extending the canonical  representation of ${\mathcal G} $ on the anchor kernel, there exists a unique Cartan  connection $D$ on ${\mathcal G} $ such that $\bar D = \rho $.\end{proposition}First note that the kernel of the canonical projection$J^1 {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G} $ is a totally intransitive Lie groupoid whoseform we can show is independent of the particular integration ${\mathcal G} $ of $A$. Thiskernel is denoted $\automorphism(TM, A)$ and described further in Section\ref{deform}. For present purposes, the following properties suffice:\begin{lemma}  If the Lie algebroid $A$ of a Lie groupoid ${\mathcal G} $ is transitive, then:  \begin{conditions}  \item The adjoint representation of $J^1 {\mathcal G} $ on $A$ restricts to    representation of $\automorphism(TM, A) \subset J^1 {\mathcal G} $ that is    faithful.\label{kr1}  \item For any $m \in M$ and each linear map $L \colon A|_m \rightarrow A|_m$,    restricting to the identity on the anchor kernel, and whose corresponding factoring    $T_mM \rightarrow T_mM$ through the anchor $\# \colon A \rightarrow TM$ is an    isomorphism, there exists a unique $\psi \in \model|_m$ such that    $\Adjoint_\psi = L$.\label{kr2}  \end{conditions}\end{lemma}\begin{proof}[Proof of lemma]  Part \eqref{kr1} follows from Proposition \ref{kernel}, and part \eqref{kr2} from  \ref{opal2a} and transitivity.\end{proof}\begin{proof}[Proof of proposition]  We seek a Lie groupoid morphism $S \colon {\mathcal G} \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G} $  that is a right-inverse for the projection  $\pi \colon J^1 {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G} $ and satisfies  \begin{equation}    \Adjoint_{S(g)} = \rho_g \qquad g \in {\mathcal G}.\label{kr3}  \end{equation}  To that end, let $\tilde S \colon U \rightarrow \pi^{-1}(U)$ be any smooth, local, rightinverse for $\pi $, defined on some open neighborhood $U \subset {\mathcal G}$ (notnecessarily respecting groupoid multiplication).  Then, for each arrow $g \in U$ beginningat some $m \in M$, $L = \Adjoint_{\tilde S(g)}^{-1}\circ \rho_g $ satisfies thecondition in \eqref{kr2}, because $\rho$ extends the adjoint representation of${\mathcal G} $ on the anchor kernel of $A$. Accordingly, there exists unique$\psi(g) \in \model$ such that$\Adjoint_{\psi(g)} =\Adjoint_{\tilde S(g)}^{-1}\circ \rho_g$, so that \eqref{kr3} holdsif we define $S(g) = \tilde S(g)\psi(g)$. It is not hard to see that$S \colon U \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G} $ is smooth. Moreover, from the uniqueness, itis clear that $S(g)$ does not actually depend on the choice of local right inverse$\tilde S$, which means we can collate local definitions of $S$ as just described into aglobal map $S \colon {\mathcal G} \rightarrow J^1 {\mathcal G} $. From \eqref{kr3}, wewill have $\Adjoint_{S(g)S(h)}=\Adjoint_{S(gh)}$ for all multipliable$g, h \in {\mathcal G} $. Using \eqref{kr1} it is not hard to see that $S(gh)=S(g)S(h)$must follow, because $S(g)S(h)$ and $S(gh)$ share a common image under the projection$J^1 {\mathcal G} \rightarrow {\mathcal G}$, namely $gh$. A similar argument shows that$S$ is uniquely determined by requirement \eqref{kr3}.\end{proof}\bibliography{dynamics2}\end{document}%%% Local Variables:%%% mode: latex%%% TeX-master: t%%% End:
